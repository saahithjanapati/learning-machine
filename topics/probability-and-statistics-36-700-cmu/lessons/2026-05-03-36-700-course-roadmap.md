## What This Course Is

36-700 is a mathematical statistics course with a probability runway.

It is not just a collection of formulas for tests. It teaches why statistical procedures work: how randomness behaves, how samples relate to populations, how estimators fluctuate, and how evidence is formalized.

The local syllabus describes the course as covering probability first, then point estimation, hypothesis testing, asymptotic theory, and possible special topics such as nonparametric inference, bootstrap, Bayesian inference, and regression. The public course reference also emphasizes concentration, convergence, maximum likelihood, interval estimation, and resampling.

The course's central question is:

> Given random data, what can we responsibly infer about the process that generated it?

## The Main Arc

The course moves in a natural order.

First, probability defines randomness. You learn events, probability laws, conditioning, independence, Bayes' rule, random variables, distributions, expectation, and variance.

Second, convergence explains why large samples help. You learn inequalities, laws of large numbers, and central-limit behavior.

Third, inference begins. You define parameters, construct estimators, measure their quality, and use data to estimate unknown quantities.

Fourth, testing formalizes evidence. You state a null hypothesis, choose a statistic, control type I error, think about power, and interpret p-values or rejection regions.

Fifth, nonparametric methods relax rigid parametric assumptions. Instead of assuming a small family like Normal or Exponential, you estimate densities or regression functions more flexibly.

## Why Probability Comes First

Probability is the model of uncertainty before data are observed.

For example, before flipping a coin, the result is random. We can talk about events, probabilities, independence, and distributions. Once we flip the coin many times, the observed data become evidence about the unknown process.

Statistics reverses probability's direction:

- probability: assume a model, predict data behavior;
- statistics: observe data, infer the model or population quantity.

You need probability first because every estimator, confidence interval, and hypothesis test has a sampling distribution. Without probability, inference becomes recipe memorization.

## Where Statistics Begins

Statistics begins when something is unknown.

Suppose $X_1,\dots,X_n$ are data from a distribution with unknown mean $\mu$. The sample mean

$$
\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i
$$

is an estimator of $\mu$.

Then the statistical questions start:

- Is $\bar{X}$ biased?
- What is its variance?
- Does it converge to $\mu$?
- How quickly does it fluctuate?
- Can we build an interval likely to contain $\mu$?
- Can we test whether $\mu$ equals a proposed value?

This is the heart of 36-700.

## How To Study It

For each topic, keep three columns:

- Definition: the exact mathematical object.
- Computation: the algebra or procedure.
- Interpretation: what the result says about data.

For example, with conditional probability:

- Definition: $\mathbb{P}(A|B)=\mathbb{P}(A\cap B)/\mathbb{P}(B)$.
- Computation: plug in event probabilities.
- Interpretation: how the probability of $A$ changes after learning $B$ occurred.

With hypothesis testing:

- Definition: $H_0$, $H_1$, type I error, power.
- Computation: statistic, rejection region, p-value.
- Interpretation: whether the observed data are surprising under the null.

The most common failure mode is doing algebra without interpretation. The second most common failure mode is having intuition without exact conditions. The course requires both.
