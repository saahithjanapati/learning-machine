A **potential** is just a local **score function**.

2. Multiply those scores.
3. Normalize at the end to get a real probability.

$$

MCMC from the basics:

## 1) What problem are we trying to solve?

In PGMs, exact inference can be very expensive (large state spaces, high treewidth, loops).
But we still want quantities like:
- marginal probabilities,
- expectations,
- samples from the model.

So we need an approximate method that works when exact methods are too hard.

## 2) Monte Carlo idea (before Markov chains)

Monte Carlo means:
- estimate a quantity by averaging random samples.

Example:
- If we can draw samples $x^{(1)},\dots,x^{(N)}$ from target distribution $p(x)$,
- then
$$
\mathbb E_p[f(X)] \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^N f(x^{(t)}).
$$

So sampling turns hard integrals/sums into averages.

## 3) Why MCMC specifically?

Usually we cannot sample directly from complicated $p(x)$.
But we can often evaluate it up to a constant:
$$
p(x)\propto \tilde p(x).
$$

MCMC idea:
- build a random process (a Markov chain) whose long-run distribution is the target $p$.
- then run the chain and treat visited states as samples.

## 4) Markov chain in plain language

A Markov chain is a random walk where:
- next state depends only on current state (not full history).

Notation:
- current state: $X_t$
- next state: $X_{t+1}$
- transition rule: $P(X_{t+1}=x' \mid X_t=x)$

## 5) What makes the chain useful for inference?

We design the chain so target distribution $\pi$ is its stationary distribution.

Stationary means:
- if $X_t \sim \pi$, then also $X_{t+1}\sim \pi$ after one transition.

If chain conditions are good (irreducible + aperiodic), running long enough makes samples approach $\pi$ regardless of start.

## 6) Big practical picture

MCMC workflow:
1. pick a chain construction (MH, Gibbs, etc.)
2. run chain for many steps
3. discard early part (burn-in, rough heuristic)
4. use later samples to estimate marginals/expectations

Costs/caveats:
- consecutive samples are correlated,
- chain may mix slowly (especially multimodal/high-dimensional settings).

## 7) Where MH and Gibbs fit

- Metropolis-Hastings: propose move, accept/reject with correction ratio.
- Gibbs: update one coordinate from full conditional (special MH case with accept probability 1).

We will do MH and Gibbs step-by-step next.

## Part 1: Setup (why we need these algorithms)

You usually have a target distribution over states:
$$
\pi(x)\propto \tilde\pi(x)
$$
where:
- $x$ = one full state (full assignment of variables),
- $\tilde\pi(x)$ = score you can compute,
- normalizer $Z$ is hard:
$$
\pi(x)=\frac{\tilde\pi(x)}{Z},\quad Z=\sum_x \tilde\pi(x)\ (\text{or integral}).
$$

You want:
- marginals,
- expectations,
- posterior summaries.

Direct brute-force is hard, so we build a Markov chain that has $\pi$ as long-run distribution.

---

## Part 2: Metropolis-Hastings (MH)

### 2.1 Core idea

At current state $x$:
1. Propose a candidate $x'$ from proposal rule $q(x' \mid x)$.
2. Accept with probability
$$
\alpha(x,x')=\min\left(1,\frac{\tilde\pi(x')\,q(x\mid x')}{\tilde\pi(x)\,q(x'\mid x)}\right).
$$
3. If accepted, move to $x'$; else stay at $x$.

That is one MH step.

### 2.2 Intuition for acceptance ratio

The ratio compares:
- how much target prefers candidate over current (`tilde pi` ratio),
- corrected by proposal asymmetry (`q` ratio).

If candidate is much better, accept often.
If candidate is worse, sometimes accept (important for exploration).

### 2.3 Why we do not need $Z$

Because ratio uses:
$$
\frac{\pi(x')}{\pi(x)}
=\frac{\tilde\pi(x')/Z}{\tilde\pi(x)/Z}
=\frac{\tilde\pi(x')}{\tilde\pi(x)}.
$$
Normalizer cancels.

### 2.4 Tiny numeric MH example

Suppose:
- current $x=A$, proposal $x'=B$,
- $\tilde\pi(A)=2,\ \tilde\pi(B)=6$,
- $q(B\mid A)=1,\ q(A\mid B)=0.5$.

Then:
$$
\alpha(A,B)=\min\left(1,\frac{6\cdot 0.5}{2\cdot 1}\right)=\min(1,1.5)=1.
$$
So always accept and move to $B$.

If ratio had been 0.2, we would accept with probability 0.2 and otherwise stay.

### 2.5 Practical tuning intuition

Proposal too small:
- high acceptance, but tiny moves -> slow exploration.

Proposal too large:
- low acceptance, many rejections -> also slow.

Need balance (mixing quality).

---

## Part 3: Gibbs Sampling

### 3.1 Core idea

State is $x=(x_1,\dots,x_n)$.

One Gibbs step:
1. pick coordinate $i$,
2. sample new value from full conditional:
$$
x_i \sim p(x_i \mid x_{-i}),
$$
3. replace only that coordinate.

Repeat.

### 3.2 Why Gibbs is often convenient in PGMs

In many models:
- each full conditional depends only on local neighborhood (Markov blanket),
- so conditional sampling can be easier than direct global sampling.

### 3.3 Tiny Gibbs example (2 variables)

State currently $(x_1,x_2)=(0,1)$.

Step:
- choose $i=1$,
- sample new $x_1$ from $p(x_1\mid x_2=1)$,
- suppose sampled $x_1=1$,
- new state becomes $(1,1)$.

Next step may update $x_2$ similarly.

### 3.4 Why Gibbs can be seen as MH special case

If proposal is exactly the full conditional, MH acceptance ratio becomes 1.
So Gibbs always accepts.

That is why Gibbs has no reject step in standard form.

---

## Part 4: MH vs Gibbs (conceptual comparison)

MH:
- need proposal $q$ and acceptance rule,
- flexible (works widely),
- may reject moves.

Gibbs:
- need ability to sample full conditionals,
- no rejection in standard form,
- updates often one variable at a time.

When conditionals are easy -> Gibbs is attractive.
When conditionals are hard or unavailable -> MH-style proposals are common.

---

## Part 5: What \"moving between states\" means

A state = full assignment of all variables.

Example with 3 binary vars:
- state could be $(1,0,1)$.

Move means:
- go to another full assignment like $(1,1,1)$ or $(0,0,1)$ according to algorithm rule.

Over long run:
- visit frequencies approximate target distribution.

---

## Part 6: Why these methods can still fail or be slow

Even if correct asymptotically:
- chain can mix slowly,
- high dimensions are hard,
- multimodal targets can trap chain in one region.

So \"correct method\" does not always mean \"fast enough in practice.\"

---

## Part 7: Exam-ready summary statements

1. MH update:
- propose, compute $\alpha$, accept/reject.

2. Gibbs update:
- sample one variable from full conditional; always accept.

3. MCMC objective:
- build chain whose stationary distribution is target; estimate queries from long-run samples.

4. Stationary vs one-step stay:
- stationary distribution = long-run occupancy,
- $P(i\to i)$ = one-step self-transition probability.
