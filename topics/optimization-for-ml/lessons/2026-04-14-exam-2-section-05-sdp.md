## 5.0 Where This Topic Came From in the Course

This topic sits on top of:

- convex sets and convex cones
- duality and Lagrangians
- matrix analysis

In the course flow:

1. `March 24`: SDP lecture
2. `HW3`: SDP relaxation / MaxCut-style material
3. `HW4`: sum-of-squares via SDP

So SDP is not just another optimization family. It is where linear algebra and convex duality become tightly coupled.

## 5.1 Why SDP Matters

An LP optimizes over vectors with nonnegativity constraints.

An SDP optimizes over symmetric matrices with positive-semidefinite constraints.

That one change is powerful because:

- it still preserves convexity
- it can express richer geometry than LP
- many hard discrete problems admit useful SDP relaxations

So the high-level story is:

- LP uses the nonnegative orthant
- SDP replaces it with the PSD cone
- convexity survives, but the modeling power increases

## 5.2 The PSD Cone

The space of symmetric matrices is

$$
S^n = \{X \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}: X=X^T\}.
$$

The positive-semidefinite cone is

$$
S_+^n = \{X \in S^n : X \succeq 0\}.
$$

The definition of positive semidefiniteness is

$$
X \succeq 0
\iff
v^T X v \ge 0
\quad \forall v \in \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

Equivalent intuition:

- $X \succeq 0$ means the quadratic form generated by $X$ is never negative
- for symmetric matrices, this is equivalent to all eigenvalues being nonnegative

You should think of `S_+^n` as the matrix analogue of the nonnegative orthant.

## 5.3 PSD Facts You Keep Using

These are the recurring short facts from lecture and quizzes.

### Diagonal entries

If $X \succeq 0$, then every diagonal entry is nonnegative:

$$
X_{ii} = e_i^T X e_i \ge 0.
$$

This is just the PSD definition with $v=e_i$.

### Zero diagonal entry implies zero row/column

If $X \succeq 0$ and $X_{ii}=0$, then the entire $i$th row and column are zero.

This fact is easy to forget because it is much stronger than merely saying the diagonal entry is zero.

### Convex cone structure

The PSD cone is a closed convex cone:

- if $X,Y \succeq 0$, then $\lambda X + (1-\lambda)Y \succeq 0$ for $\lambda \in [0,1]$
- if $X \succeq 0$ and $t\ge 0$, then $tX \succeq 0$

This is why SDP remains a convex optimization problem.

## 5.4 Matrix Inner Product

For symmetric matrices, the standard inner product is

$$
A \bullet B = \operatorname{Tr}(A^T B).
$$

Since the matrices are symmetric, this is just the Frobenius inner product:

$$
A \bullet B = \sum_{i,j} a_{ij}b_{ij}.
$$

The most important theorem is:

if $A \succeq 0$ and $B \succeq 0$, then

$$
A \bullet B \ge 0.
$$

This is the matrix fact that makes SDP weak duality work.

Another useful PSD-specific fact from lecture is:

$$
A \bullet B = 0
\iff
AB = 0
\qquad \text{for } A,B \succeq 0.
$$

That is the SDP analogue of complementary-slackness structure.

### 5.4.1 Why the PSD inner product is nonnegative

This fact gets used so often that it is worth seeing why it is true.

Suppose

$$
A \succeq 0,
\qquad
B \succeq 0.
$$

Because $A$ is PSD, it has a symmetric square root $A^{1/2}$. Start from the definition of the matrix inner product:

$$
A \bullet B
=
\operatorname{Tr}(A^T B).
$$

Since $A$ is symmetric,

$$
A^T=A,
$$

so

$$
A \bullet B
=
\operatorname{Tr}(AB).
$$

Now use the square root identity

$$
A=A^{1/2}A^{1/2}.
$$

Substitute this into the trace:

$$
\operatorname{Tr}(AB)
=
\operatorname{Tr}(A^{1/2}A^{1/2}B).
$$

Next use the cyclic property of trace:

$$
\operatorname{Tr}(XYZ)=\operatorname{Tr}(ZXY).
$$

Applying this gives

$$
\operatorname{Tr}(A^{1/2}A^{1/2}B)
=
\operatorname{Tr}(BA^{1/2}A^{1/2})
=
\operatorname{Tr}(A^{1/2}BA^{1/2}).
$$

So we have shown

$$
A \bullet B
=
\operatorname{Tr}(A^{1/2}BA^{1/2}).
$$

Now define

$$
C:=A^{1/2}BA^{1/2}.
$$

We want to show that $C$ is PSD. Take any vector $v$. Then

$$
v^T C v
=
v^T A^{1/2}BA^{1/2} v.
$$

Regroup this as

$$
v^T A^{1/2}BA^{1/2} v
=
(A^{1/2}v)^T B (A^{1/2}v).
$$

Now set

$$
w=A^{1/2}v.
$$

Then

$$
v^T C v=w^T B w.
$$

Because $B \succeq 0$, its quadratic form is always nonnegative:

$$
w^T B w \ge 0
\qquad \text{for every } w.
$$

So

$$
v^T C v \ge 0
\qquad \text{for every } v,
$$

which means

$$
C \succeq 0.
$$

Now use a basic PSD fact: if a matrix is PSD, then its trace is nonnegative. You can justify that either because:

1. a PSD matrix has nonnegative eigenvalues and trace is the sum of eigenvalues, or
2. a PSD matrix has nonnegative diagonal entries and trace is the sum of the diagonal entries.

Therefore

$$
\operatorname{Tr}(C)\ge 0.
$$

Substitute back $C=A^{1/2}BA^{1/2}$:

$$
\operatorname{Tr}(A^{1/2}BA^{1/2})\ge 0.
$$

Finally, since

$$
A \bullet B
=
\operatorname{Tr}(A^{1/2}BA^{1/2}),
$$

we conclude

$$
A \bullet B \ge 0.
$$

The structure to remember is:

1. rewrite $A \bullet B$ as a trace
2. insert the square root of one PSD matrix
3. cyclically move factors to get $A^{1/2}BA^{1/2}$
4. show that middle matrix is PSD
5. conclude its trace is nonnegative

This is exactly the step that makes the SDP weak-duality gap nonnegative.

## 5.5 Standard SDP Primal-Dual Pair

The standard primal form is

$$
\min_X C \bullet X
\quad \text{s.t. } \quad
A_i \bullet X = b_i,\; i=1,\dots,m,
\qquad
X \succeq 0.
$$

The dual is

$$
\max_{y,S} y^T b
\quad \text{s.t. } \quad
S = C - \sum_{i=1}^m y_i A_i,
\qquad
S \succeq 0.
$$

Here:

- $X$ is the primal matrix variable
- $y \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is the vector of dual multipliers
- $S$ is the dual slack matrix

This is exactly parallel to LP duality, except:

- the primal slack is replaced by a PSD constraint on $X$
- the dual slack is replaced by a PSD constraint on $S$

## 5.6 Weak Duality for SDP

This is the central short proof to know.

### 5.6.1 The key gap identity

Suppose:

- $X$ is primal-feasible
- $(y,S)$ is dual-feasible

Then

$$
C \bullet X - y^T b
=
C \bullet X - \sum_{i=1}^m y_i (A_i \bullet X).
$$

Because $X$ is primal-feasible, we can replace each $b_i$ with $A_i \bullet X$.

Now factor out $X$:

$$
C \bullet X - y^T b
=
\left(C-\sum_{i=1}^m y_i A_i\right)\bullet X.
$$

Using dual feasibility,

$$
S = C-\sum_{i=1}^m y_i A_i,
$$

so

$$
C \bullet X - y^T b = S \bullet X.
$$

This is the SDP gap identity.

### 5.6.2 Why S bullet X is nonnegative

Now use the PSD inner-product theorem.

Since dual feasibility gives $S \succeq 0$ and primal feasibility gives $X \succeq 0$,

$$
S \bullet X \ge 0.
$$

Therefore

$$
C \bullet X - y^T b \ge 0.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
y^T b \le C \bullet X.
$$

That is weak duality.

This exact identity is one of the most exam-likely short proofs in the later half of the course.

## 5.7 Complementary-Slackness Style Consequences

If primal and dual feasible points satisfy zero gap, then

$$
C \bullet X - y^T b = 0.
$$

From the gap identity, this means

$$
S \bullet X = 0.
$$

Since both matrices are PSD, the lecture theorem gives

$$
SX = 0.
$$

This is the SDP version of complementary slackness.

So the progression is:

- weak duality gives a nonnegative gap
- zero gap means the PSD inner product vanishes
- vanishing PSD inner product implies a strong algebraic interaction between the primal and dual slack structures

## 5.8 Schur Complement

For a block matrix

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
A & B \\
B^T & C
\end{pmatrix},
$$

the lecture used the Schur complement condition

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
A & B \\
B^T & C
\end{pmatrix}
\succeq 0
\iff
A \succeq 0
\text{ and }
C-B^T A^{-1}B \succeq 0,
$$

under the usual invertibility assumptions on $A$.

Why this matters:

- it converts a PSD block constraint into a more familiar matrix inequality
- it often turns nonlinear-looking conditions into an SDP-compatible form

This is one of those facts that may appear either as:

- a direct recall question
- or a short application inside a modeling problem

## 5.10 HW3 and HW4 Connections

### HW3 connection

`HW3` pushed SDP into relaxation territory. That tells you the course expects you to see SDP not just as a matrix LP, but as a way to convexify or relax harder problems.

### HW4 connection: sum of squares

`HW4` gives one of the cleanest modeling examples in the whole unit, because it shows how a polynomial question can be turned into a PSD-matrix question.

### 5.10.0 What question are we trying to answer

Go slowly here, because the algebra can obscure the actual goal.

We start with a polynomial

$$
p(x).
$$

We want to know whether it is always nonnegative, or at least whether we can certify nonnegativity in a tractable way.

Checking direct nonnegativity of a polynomial can be hard. So the course introduces an easier-to-check sufficient condition:

$$
p(x)\text{ is a sum of squares}.
$$

That means:

$$
p(x)=q_1(x)^2+\cdots+q_m(x)^2
$$

for some simpler polynomials $q_1,\dots,q_m$.

Why is this useful? Because a square is never negative, and a sum of nonnegative terms is never negative. So if you can write

$$
p(x)=\sum_{j=1}^m q_j(x)^2,
$$

then you immediately know

$$
p(x)\ge 0 \qquad \text{for all } x.
$$

So the real question in HW4 is:

$$
\text{Can I recognize or test this SOS property using convex optimization?}
$$

The surprising answer is yes: it can be tested using an SDP.

### 5.10.1 What sum of squares means

For a univariate polynomial of even degree `2d`, say

$$
p(x)=r_0+r_1x+\cdots+r_{2d}x^{2d},
$$

we say that $p$ is a **sum-of-squares** polynomial if

$$
p(x)=\sum_{j=1}^m q_j(x)^2
$$

for some polynomials $q_1,\dots,q_m$ of degree at most $d$.

Why degree at most $d$? Because when you square a polynomial of degree at most $d$, the result has degree at most

$$
2d.
$$

So if $p(x)$ has degree $2d$, then it is reasonable to search for square roots $q_j$ of degree at most $d$.

A tiny example:

$$
x^2+2x+3=(x+1)^2+(\sqrt{2})^2.
$$

So this polynomial is SOS. The point is not that this example is deep; the point is to see what “sum of squares” literally means before any matrix notation appears.

Why this matters:

- every SOS polynomial is automatically nonnegative
- checking nonnegativity directly can be hard
- checking SOS can often be reduced to convex feasibility

So SOS is a tractable surrogate for polynomial nonnegativity.

The monomial-basis notation from HW4 is

$$
[x]_d = \begin{pmatrix}1 & x & x^2 & \cdots & x^d\end{pmatrix}^T.
$$

This vector just packages the monomials up to degree $d$ into one object.

That is useful because any polynomial of degree at most $d$ can then be written as a dot product with this vector.

For example, if

$$
q(x)=a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2,
$$

then

$$
q(x)=
\begin{pmatrix}a_0 & a_1 & a_2\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}1\\x\\x^2\end{pmatrix}.
$$

So the coefficient vector and the monomial vector together give a compact linear-algebra representation of the polynomial.

### 5.10.2 Why SOS becomes a PSD matrix condition

This is the Gram-matrix trick.

If $q_j(x)$ has degree at most $d$, then it can be written as

$$
q_j(x)=a_j^T [x]_d
$$

for some coefficient vector $a_j \in \mathbb{R}^{d+1}$.

This is just saying: every polynomial can be represented by its coefficient vector in the monomial basis.

Now take one square:

$$
q_j(x)^2=(a_j^T [x]_d)^2.
$$

This expression looks scalar, but it can be rewritten in matrix form:

$$
(a_j^T [x]_d)^2
=
([x]_d^T a_j)(a_j^T [x]_d).
$$

Since the middle part is just a scalar, we can regroup it as

$$
([x]_d^T a_j)(a_j^T [x]_d)
=
[x]_d^T (a_j a_j^T) [x]_d.
$$

That is the key trick. One scalar square turns into one matrix outer product

$$
a_j a_j^T.
$$

Now square and sum:

$$
p(x)=\sum_{j=1}^m q_j(x)^2
=
\sum_{j=1}^m ([x]_d^T a_j)^2.
$$

Rewrite each square as a quadratic form:

$$
([x]_d^T a_j)^2
=
[x]_d^T a_j a_j^T [x]_d.
$$

So

$$
p(x)
=
[x]_d^T \left(\sum_{j=1}^m a_j a_j^T\right)[x]_d.
$$

Define

$$
Q:=\sum_{j=1}^m a_j a_j^T.
$$

Then

$$
p(x)=[x]_d^T Q [x]_d.
$$

And crucially,

$$
Q \succeq 0,
$$

because each matrix

$$
a_j a_j^T
$$

is rank-one PSD. To see that, take any vector $z$:

$$
z^T(a_j a_j^T)z
=
(a_j^T z)^2
\ge 0.
$$

So each outer product is PSD, and sums of PSD matrices remain PSD.

So every SOS polynomial has a representation of the form

$$
p(x)=[x]_d^T Q [x]_d
\qquad \text{with } Q \succeq 0.
$$

The converse also holds. If

$$
p(x)=[x]_d^T Q [x]_d
\qquad \text{with } Q \succeq 0,
$$

then factor $Q$ as

$$
Q=B^T B
$$

using Cholesky or the spectral theorem.

This is the reverse direction, and it is the part people often find magical the first time. The point is:

- if $Q \succeq 0$, then it can be written as “something transposed times itself”
- that automatically turns the quadratic form into a squared norm

Then

$$
p(x)=[x]_d^T B^T B [x]_d = \|B[x]_d\|^2.
$$

If the rows of $B$ are $b_1^T,\dots,b_m^T$, then

$$
\|B[x]_d\|^2
=
\sum_{j=1}^m (b_j^T [x]_d)^2,
$$

which is explicitly a sum of squares. Each term

$$
b_j^T [x]_d
$$

is just another polynomial of degree at most $d$.

So the exact equivalence is:

$$
p(x)\text{ is SOS}
\iff
\exists Q \succeq 0 \text{ such that } p(x)=[x]_d^T Q [x]_d.
$$

This matrix $Q$ is often called a **Gram matrix** for the polynomial.

### 5.10.3 The quartic case from HW4

Now make this concrete.

Suppose

$$
p(x)=r_4x^4+r_3x^3+r_2x^2+r_1x+r_0.
$$

This is degree `4`, so `2d=4` and therefore `d=2`. That means the right monomial vector is

$$
[x]_2=\begin{pmatrix}1\\x\\x^2\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Pause here: this is exactly the list of monomials up to degree $2$, because if you square a degree-2 polynomial, you get degree at most $4$.

Let

$$
Q=
\begin{pmatrix}
q_{00} & q_{01} & q_{02}\\
q_{01} & q_{11} & q_{12}\\
q_{02} & q_{12} & q_{22}
\end{pmatrix}
\succeq 0.
$$

Now expand

$$
[x]_2^T Q [x]_2.
$$

It is worth doing this carefully once, because this is where the SDP constraints actually come from:

$$
[x]_2^T Q [x]_2
=
\begin{pmatrix}1 & x & x^2\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
q_{00} & q_{01} & q_{02}\\
q_{01} & q_{11} & q_{12}\\
q_{02} & q_{12} & q_{22}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}1\\x\\x^2\end{pmatrix}.
$$

Expanding and collecting like powers gives

$$
[x]_2^T Q [x]_2
=
q_{00}
+2q_{01}x
+(q_{11}+2q_{02})x^2
+2q_{12}x^3
+q_{22}x^4.
$$

Why do the factors of `2` appear? Because $Q$ is symmetric, so the cross terms show up twice:

- $q_{01}x$ appears once from the `(1,x)` position and once from the `(x,1)` position
- similarly for the `(1,x^2)` and `(x,x^2)` pairs

That is why the off-diagonal entries get doubled in the polynomial expansion.

Now compare this coefficient-by-coefficient with the target quartic

$$
p(x)=r_4x^4+r_3x^3+r_2x^2+r_1x+r_0.
$$

Matching coefficients gives the linear constraints

$$
q_{00}=r_0,
$$

$$
2q_{01}=r_1,
$$

$$
q_{11}+2q_{02}=r_2,
$$

$$
2q_{12}=r_3,
$$

$$
q_{22}=r_4.
$$

That is the real modeling step: “polynomial equality” becomes “linear equations in the entries of $Q$.”

This is the slow conceptual translation:

- original question: “is this polynomial SOS?”
- matrix version: “does there exist a PSD matrix $Q$ whose quadratic form reproduces the polynomial?”
- coefficient matching: “do the entries of $Q$ satisfy the right linear equations?”

### 5.10.4 Why this is an SDP

At this point the SOS question

$$
\text{Is } p(x)\text{ a sum of squares?}
$$

has become:

find a symmetric matrix $Q$ such that

$$
Q \succeq 0
$$

and

$$
q_{00}=r_0,\quad
2q_{01}=r_1,\quad
q_{11}+2q_{02}=r_2,\quad
2q_{12}=r_3,\quad
q_{22}=r_4.
$$

This is exactly an SDP feasibility problem because:

- the unknown is a symmetric matrix $Q$
- the matrix must satisfy the PSD constraint $Q \succeq 0$
- the remaining constraints are linear equalities in the entries of $Q$

So HW4 is teaching the broader lesson:

`SDP is a language for expressing positivity through PSD matrices.`

That is why SOS is such a standard SDP application: “being a sum of squares” is really “having a PSD Gram matrix.”

## 5.11 Common Traps

- forgetting symmetry when defining $S^n$
- confusing $X \succeq 0$ with entrywise nonnegativity
- flipping the sign in the gap identity
- writing the primal objective minus dual objective incorrectly
- using the Schur complement without checking the needed assumptions
- forgetting that SDP is convex because the PSD cone is convex

The single most important identity to know cold is:

$$
C \bullet X - y^T b = S \bullet X \ge 0.
$$
