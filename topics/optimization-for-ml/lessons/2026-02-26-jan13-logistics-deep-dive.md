## How to read this lesson

This lecture is not theory-heavy, but it determines how well you can execute the theory. Treat it as your operating manual.

## 1) Build your course control panel

The lecture defines a multi-platform workflow:

- course site: schedule, slides, syllabus, deadlines
- Piazza: announcements and discussion
- Gradescope: submission and grading
- Canvas: grade display and recordings

A practical interpretation is simple: if you check only one platform, you will miss something important.

## 2) Understand where your points come from

The grading structure means performance is distributed, not concentrated in one event:

- homework: steady effort
- quizzes: frequent conceptual recall
- exams: large single-shot assessments

The strategic implication is that consistency wins. Last-minute cramming is mathematically incompatible with this grade design.

## 3) Late policy is a budget, not a suggestion

Grace days are a finite resource. The policy in lecture is explicit about total and per-assignment limits.

Read this as a budgeting problem:

- do not spend all flexibility early
- reserve buffer for heavy weeks
- do not assume policy exceptions

## 4) Collaboration policy and independence boundary

The lecture allows discussion but requires independent final artifacts (writeups and code). That boundary is stricter than many students assume.

Use this rule of thumb:

- discuss ideas and sanity checks
- never reproduce another person's solution path verbatim
- never rely on variable renaming as "new work"

## 5) Exam constraints shape preparation style

Lecture constraints include in-class timing, device restrictions, and a limited cheat-sheet allowance.

That means preparation should include:

- repeated closed-book retrieval practice
- cheat-sheet drafting over time, not one-night compression
- careful notation matching to class conventions

## 6) Why notation discipline is emphasized

The lecture explicitly warns against post-hoc claims like "my definition is different so my answer should count."

In optimization, sign conventions and symbol definitions change derivations. You must state your convention clearly or follow class convention exactly.

## 7) Time allocation is part of the curriculum

The lecture's weekly time breakdown is a planning signal. If you skip the reading/slides/hw rhythm, the later proof-heavy topics become much harder than they need to be.

## Checkpoint

Make sure you can state your own execution plan in one sentence:

- where you check announcements daily,
- when you start each homework,
- how you preserve grace-day budget,
- how you maintain notation consistency.

## Common mistakes

- treating logistics as optional
- missing announcements because of fragmented platform use
- starting homework too late for conceptual questions
- learning notation from random sources and mixing conventions on graded work

## One-paragraph recap

Jan 13 logistics defines constraints on your learning process just like feasibility constraints define optimization problems. If you manage channels, deadlines, notation, and collaboration boundaries correctly, your technical work can actually translate into grades.
