## What This Course Is

10-715 is not just "machine learning with more algorithms." It is a proof-heavy, fast-paced introduction to the mathematical foundations of machine learning.

The course assumes you can already survive the language of linear algebra, probability, calculus, optimization, and programming. It then uses that language to ask deeper questions:

- What exactly is a learning problem?
- What is an algorithm optimizing?
- Why should training performance say anything about future performance?
- Which assumptions make a theorem true?
- Where do modern methods fit relative to classical theory?

The public CMU description frames 10-715 as a class for students whose research depends strongly on ML. That is the right mental model. The goal is not only to use algorithms. The goal is to understand algorithms well enough to modify, analyze, and critique them.

## The Through-Line

The course has one repeated pattern:

1. define a learning setup;
2. choose a hypothesis class;
3. choose a loss;
4. define the optimization problem;
5. derive or implement an algorithm;
6. prove something about the algorithm or the learned predictor;
7. ask when the result generalizes.

For example, perceptrons are not just a historical curiosity. They introduce linear separators, margins, mistakes, and proof-by-progress. SVMs are not just a classifier. They convert geometry into convex optimization and duality. Kernels are not just a trick. They show how inner products can change the effective feature space. Learning theory is not just abstract math. It is the answer to the question: why should empirical success transfer to unseen data?

## The Main Modules

The course starts with supervised learning grammar: examples, labels, hypothesis classes, losses, true risk, and empirical risk. This gives the notation used everywhere else.

It then moves into linear classification. Perceptrons teach mistake-bound reasoning. SVMs teach margin geometry, constrained optimization, and dual variables. Kernels teach how linear algorithms can act nonlinear after changing the representation.

Learning theory then becomes the central mathematical block. Instead of asking whether a training set can be fit, it asks whether a whole class of predictors can be controlled. This is where concentration inequalities, union bounds, and capacity ideas enter.

Neural networks then stretch the classical picture. They are expressive hypothesis classes, but their practical success raises harder questions about optimization, generalization, and model selection.

Bandits and reinforcement learning change the feedback model. Instead of labeled examples, the learner acts, receives rewards, and must balance exploration with exploitation.

Graphical models and causality introduce structured probabilistic reasoning: variables, dependence, conditional independence, and the danger of confusing association with causal structure.

The late-course diffusion and score-matching block is a modern generative-modeling capstone. It asks how to learn distributions through noise, scores, denoising, and reverse processes.

## How To Study It

The wrong way to study 10-715 is to memorize algorithm names.

The right way is to keep a proof-and-model notebook with four recurring boxes:

- Setup: What are $\mathcal{X}$, $\mathcal{Y}$, $\mathcal{H}$, the loss, and the data assumption?
- Algorithm: What is the actual update or optimization problem?
- Guarantee: What does the theorem claim, and what assumptions does it need?
- Failure mode: What breaks if separability, independence, convexity, boundedness, or capacity control fails?

After every lecture block, do one small derivation from memory. For example:

- prove the perceptron progress lemma;
- derive the SVM primal from maximum margin geometry;
- write the Lagrangian and identify support vectors;
- sketch a finite-class generalization bound;
- explain why a kernel matrix must be positive semidefinite.

This course rewards active reconstruction. If you can only recognize a proof after seeing it, you are not yet ready for exam or research use.

## What Counts As Mastery

At "0," the learner may recognize terms like SVM, kernel, generalization, RL, and diffusion.

At "1," the learner can do four things:

1. translate informal ML language into mathematical objects;
2. derive core algorithms from objectives;
3. prove or sketch guarantees under stated assumptions;
4. explain why modern methods fit, strain, or extend the classical framework.

That is the target of this curriculum.

## Quick Check

1. Why is 10-715 more than a list of ML algorithms?
2. What is the difference between risk and empirical risk?
3. Why do perceptrons, SVMs, and kernels form a natural sequence?
4. Why does learning theory become necessary after empirical risk minimization?
5. What changes when the course moves from supervised learning to bandits and RL?
