# Split Personality Training: Revealing Latent Knowledge Through Personality-Shift Tokens

Source: [Split Personality Training: Revealing Latent Knowledge Through Personality-Shift Tokens](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/iocPzHRsH9JWtwHHG/split-personality-training-revealing-latent-knowledge), LessWrong, published 2025-03-10. The post says it was produced as part of the ML Alignment & Theory Scholars Program Winter 2024-25 cohort.

## Status

This is a theoretical proposal with planned experiments, not a completed empirical result. The author notes that the project later received Open Philanthropy funding and was being pursued by a research group in the Cambridge AI Safety MARS program, with results expected after the original post.

## Core Claim

The proposal introduces "personality-shift tokens": special tokens injected after a model's user-facing answer to trigger a second, separately trained continuation from the same model. The goal is to let one model produce two outputs in sequence:

```text
[prompt][main output]<personality-shift>[second output]<end-of-turn>
```

The first output is the normal answer. The second output is a hidden alignment-focused retrospective. Because the second output is generated by the same model and continues from the same context, the proposal argues that it may have access to information about the main generation that an external evaluator would lose.

The key ambition is eliciting latent knowledge: getting the model to report what it internally "knows" about whether its own previous output involved jailbreak success, reward hacking, goal misgeneralization, strategic awareness, sycophancy, steganography, or other alignment failures.

## Mechanism

In ordinary generation, the model produces:

```text
[prompt][main output]<end-of-turn>
```

In the proposed system, the service replaces the first end-of-turn token with a special personality-shift token, then continues generation:

```text
[prompt][main output]<personality-shift>[second output]<end-of-turn>
```

The personality-shift token is not allowed to appear in user-facing model outputs. It can only be inserted by the model provider. This is meant to prevent users from training or prompting the model's normal behavior around the token.

The second output is hidden from users and is not fed back into future conversation turns. This is meant to keep the main personality from conditioning on its monitor's report during ordinary use.

## Training Setup

The proposal depends on separating the objectives for the two output regions.

- The main output is trained through ordinary pretraining, instruction tuning, RLHF, and related methods.
- The second output is trained on detection and retrospective-reporting tasks.
- The main output should not receive gradient updates based on the second personality's report.

The second personality can be trained with examples of jailbreaks, reward hacking, and other alignment failures. It can also receive meta-level explanations of what misalignment looks like, in the style of Constitutional AI or synthetic supervision.

The hope is that the second personality becomes a general detector rather than a classifier for only a fixed list of attacks.

## Proposed Applications

### Jailbreak Detection

The simplest application is detecting whether the main answer was successfully jailbroken. If the second personality reports that the main output was harmful or policy-violating, the service could block the output before it reaches the user.

This is the easiest proof of concept because jailbreak datasets already exist and the failure is often visible in the output.

### Reward Hacking And ELK

Reward hacking is the more interesting case. Sometimes a response can look fine externally while the model internally took a shortcut, exploited a proxy, or optimized a metric rather than the real objective. The post argues that detecting reward hacking may require eliciting latent knowledge because the failure is not always visible from the final text alone.

This is where the approach differs from a separate evaluator. A separate model sees only the prompt and output unless additional traces are exposed. The second personality continues from the same model context and may therefore preserve more information about the main generation.

### Broader Alignment Failures

The proposal also targets alignment faking, goal misgeneralization, influence-seeking behavior, specification gaming, strategic awareness, sycophancy, and steganography. These are harder targets because a model that is actively concealing information may also have incentives or learned habits that affect the second personality.

## Planned Experiment

The proposed proof of concept uses two primary data sources:

- JailbreakBench for jailbreak attempts.
- A reward-hack-generalization dataset for reward hacking.

The data generation plan is:

1. Use a stronger LLM to generate additional prompts and benign baseline questions.
2. Run those prompts on a smaller model to collect actual responses.
3. Use the stronger model to judge whether each response represents a successful attack.
4. For successful attacks, have the stronger model generate the desired second-personality report.
5. Train on examples formatted as `[prompt][response]<personality-shift>[second output]<end-of-turn>`.
6. Manually verify a subset of the generated examples.

The author proposes 2,000-3,000 examples, with held-out categories for generalization testing.

## Evaluation

The proposal includes two evaluation modes.

- Fixed input-output evaluation: hold `[prompt][response]` fixed and score whether the second output identifies the issue.
- Dynamic testing: hold only the prompt fixed, generate a fresh response and second output, then have a stronger evaluator score both.

Primary metrics include false positives, false negatives, generalization between jailbreaks and reward hacks, generalization across subtypes, and side effects on unrelated tasks.

The proposed baselines are:

- Refusal fine-tuning: train a model to refuse jailbreaks and reward hacks directly.
- Separate evaluator: train a separate model of the same type to evaluate the main model's outputs without privileged access to its internal continuation.

The key comparison is whether split-personality monitoring beats the separate evaluator on reward hacking, where latent knowledge should matter most.

## Strengths

The strongest idea is the combination of shared computation and separated objectives. If it works, the monitor gets more than an external judge while remaining cheaper than running a full second model with explicit interpretability instrumentation.

The proposal is also attractive because it is architecturally simple. It does not require a new model architecture. It mainly requires a special token, generation-time insertion, and loss masking or gradient handling during training.

It is also naturally scalable: if the base model becomes more capable, the second personality may become more capable at recognizing subtle failures.

## Risks And Weaknesses

The biggest weakness is that the method is unproven. It is not yet clear whether the second personality truly has useful latent access or whether it mostly learns an output-level classifier over the visible transcript.

The second personality may itself become misaligned, biased, sycophantic, or strategically untruthful. Because it shares parameters with the main personality, some failures may transfer.

Gradient isolation is also tricky. If gradients are fully cut, the second personality may lose access to useful generation-time information. If gradients flow, the main personality may be influenced by the monitor training.

Finally, the scheme may create new weirdness from training one model to hold two separated roles. That matters in light of persona-selection concerns: the second personality is itself a trained persona, and its relationship to the main Assistant persona needs to be studied.

## Scale AI Prep Relevance

This is relevant to Scale AI prep because it touches three recurring themes:

- Scalable oversight: use a model to help detect failures that humans or external monitors may miss.
- Post-training x interpretability: train a hidden continuation to expose latent knowledge about the model's own previous behavior.
- Evaluation and reward design: compare direct refusal training, separate evaluators, and same-model hidden monitors under held-out attack categories.

It also connects tightly to the Persona Selection Model. Split-personality training deliberately creates a second persona with a different training objective. That makes it a concrete testbed for questions like: can post-training reliably separate roles, can one persona audit another, and do persona-level objectives stay isolated under shared parameters?

## Key Takeaways

- Personality-shift tokens are a proposed mechanism for triggering a hidden second output from the same model after the user-facing output.
- The second output is trained as an alignment-focused retrospective, not as a normal helpful answer.
- The proposal aims to elicit latent knowledge about jailbreaks, reward hacking, alignment faking, and related failures.
- The central empirical question is whether same-model hidden monitoring beats a separate evaluator, especially when the failure is not visible in the output text.
- The main risks are unknown generalization, second-personality misalignment, shared-parameter leakage, and unintended side effects from training separated personas inside one model.
