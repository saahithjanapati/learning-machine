In this Sequoia AI Ascent conversation, Demis Hassabis traces the path from games and neuroscience to DeepMind, AGI, AlphaFold, AI for science, drug discovery, simulations, information as a fundamental substance, and the philosophical question of whether advanced AI should remain a tool or become something more agentic.

## Transcript Digest

### 00:00-00:38 - Introduction

Konstantine Buhler introduces Hassabis as one of the original thinkers and founders in modern AI. The conversation is framed around three periods: Hassabis' early path, the founding and mission of DeepMind, and the science questions opened by AI.

The tone is partly biographical and partly philosophical. Hassabis is not only asked about products and capabilities, but also about what AGI is for and how AI fits into the broader project of understanding reality.

### 00:38-01:29 - Origins And The Common Thread

Hassabis says the apparent variety in his life has a common thread: he wanted to build AI from a young age. He decided as a teenager that AI was the most important and interesting thing he could work on, then chose experiences that might eventually help him build something like DeepMind.

Chess, games, neuroscience, and entrepreneurship were therefore not separate arcs. They were preparation for the same long mission.

### 01:29-02:59 - Games As AI Training Ground

Hassabis explains that games were the right technology frontier in the 1990s. Games pushed graphics, hardware, GPUs, simulation, and interactive behavior. The earliest GPUs were built for graphics engines, and game AI gave him a way to experiment with intelligent behavior in simulated worlds.

He cites `Theme Park` as an example. It simulated an amusement park where thousands of virtual visitors chose rides, bought things, and reacted to the environment. Seeing people enjoy interacting with AI systems helped convince him that AI was worth a career.

He also connects games to DeepMind's later work. Games became early proving grounds for general AI ideas because they offered controlled worlds, feedback, and measurable performance.

### 02:59-04:39 - Elixir Studios And Startup Timing

Asked about his first company, Elixir Studios, Hassabis says he wanted to combine advanced AI with creative game design. In retrospect, the most important startup lesson was about timing: it is good to be five years ahead, but bad to be fifty years ahead.

The example is `Republic`, an ambitious game that tried to simulate an entire country, including living cities and many people, on late-1990s home PCs. It was an interesting idea, but too far ahead of available hardware and tools.

The founder lesson is direct: if the opportunity is obvious to everyone, it may already be late; but if the world is not technically ready, even a brilliant idea can be too early.

### 04:39-07:25 - Founding DeepMind In 2009

Hassabis describes founding DeepMind in 2009, when AGI still sounded like science fiction to most people. The team believed several ingredients were coming together.

First, deep learning had begun to show promise, though most of industry had not yet understood how important it would become. Second, reinforcement learning was a powerful framework that had not been seriously combined with deep learning at scale. Third, GPUs and accelerated computing were becoming useful for large-scale learning. Fourth, computational neuroscience suggested principles that might transfer into machine learning.

He says the early team felt like keepers of a secret. Traditional AI centers still had many people focused on expert systems, logic languages, and older symbolic approaches. DeepMind's team thought that if they failed, at least they would fail in a new way rather than repeat the failures of 1990s AI.

### 07:25-08:52 - DeepMind Mission And AGI Bet

Hassabis says he would have spent his life on AI even if it had remained niche, because he saw it as both the most important technology and one of the most interesting scientific artifacts.

DeepMind's original mission statement was effectively: step one, solve intelligence; step two, use it to solve everything else. Hassabis saw AGI as a tool for science, medicine, and understanding the mind itself.

He also says DeepMind's broad timeline has roughly tracked the field. The original mission felt like a twenty-year project, and he still sees the field as broadly on that schedule.

### 08:52-10:37 - AI For Science Culture

Hassabis says AI for science was always central to his personal motivation. He wanted to build the ultimate tool, then use it to make scientific and medical breakthroughs.

DeepMind formally expanded its AI-for-science effort after AlphaGo, around ten years before the talk. For Hassabis, solving Go showed that the algorithms and ideas had become general enough to apply to major real-world scientific challenges.

The highest-value uses he names are medicine, healthier lifespans, material science, environment, and energy. AlphaFold is treated as the first major proof point rather than the endpoint.

### 10:37-12:42 - Biology, AlphaFold, And Isomorphic Labs

Hassabis argues that biology has already had a major AI moment through AlphaFold. Protein folding had been a fifty-year grand challenge, and knowing 3D protein structure is central to biology and drug discovery.

He then describes Isomorphic Labs as the next layer: not only predicting protein structure, but designing compounds that bind to the right targets without binding elsewhere and causing toxic side effects.

The dream is to move most drug-discovery exploration into silicon, leaving wet-lab work mainly for validation. If that works, discovery timelines could move from ten years toward months, weeks, or eventually days. That would make personalized medicine and many currently unreachable disease areas much more tractable.

### 12:42-15:02 - New Sciences Through Simulation

Asked whether AI will create new sciences, Hassabis gives two answers.

First, the study of AI systems themselves will become a new engineering science. These systems are complex artifacts that may eventually approach the complexity of minds and brains. Mechanistic interpretability is one part of the future science of understanding them.

Second, AI may unlock new sciences by making much better simulations possible. Hassabis is especially interested in emergent systems where controlled experiments are hard: economics, social systems, biology, weather, and other messy domains.

In economics, for example, a real-world interest-rate change can only be tried once in a particular historical context. A sufficiently accurate simulator could let researchers sample many counterfactuals and make better decisions in uncertain domains.

### 15:02-17:05 - Learned Simulators And Biology

Hassabis describes work on learned simulators: systems that learn to simulate domains where humans cannot write accurate equations directly.

He cites weather forecasting as one success area, saying DeepMind has highly accurate and fast weather simulation systems. He also describes the goal of a `virtual cell`, a learned simulator for biological dynamics.

His claim is that machine learning may be the right descriptive language for biology in the same way mathematics has been the right descriptive language for physics. Biology contains weak signals, weak correlations, enormous amounts of data, and complex causal structure that may be hard for human minds or traditional equations to capture.

### 17:05-18:31 - Extracting Equations From Simulators

Buhler asks whether learned simulators might eventually yield explicit equations as fundamental as Maxwell's equations.

Hassabis says that may be possible if such equations exist for emergent systems. A learned simulator gives researchers something they can sample many times. From that implicit model, it might be possible to extract explicit laws or equations.

This is one of the talk's most interesting scientific claims: AI may not only predict complex systems, but also help convert implicit learned structure into explicit scientific understanding.

### 17:32-20:29 - Information As Fundamental

Hassabis says he suspects information may be more fundamental than matter or energy. Matter and energy are famously connected by physics. He thinks information may have an equivalence with them as well, especially when looking at structure, life, entropy, and biological organization.

From this viewpoint, AI is profound because it is about information processing, organizing information, understanding information, and constructing informational objects.

He also connects this to Turing machines. Hassabis calls Alan Turing one of his scientific heroes and says DeepMind can be thought of as championing the Turing-machine view: that everything computable can be computed by a relatively simple abstract machine.

AlphaFold is used as evidence that classical computational systems can approximate phenomena that might appear quantum or physically complex if modeled directly. A modern neural network on classical hardware can sometimes find the useful structure without simulating every quantum detail.

### 20:29-21:42 - AI As Tool First

Asked when AI stops being a tool, Hassabis says the goal should be to build an incredibly intelligent, useful, precise tool first. That is already profound enough.

He acknowledges that systems are becoming more autonomous and agent-like, but separates that from deeper questions about agency and consciousness. His recommendation is to cross those later rubrics in a second step, ideally using powerful AI tools to help understand the questions better.

The practical position is cautious: build useful tools, understand minds and consciousness more precisely, then decide how to approach agency and consciousness.

### 21:42-23:37 - Consciousness And Substrate

Hassabis does not offer a full definition of consciousness. He points to likely necessary components such as self-awareness, a self/other distinction, and continuity over time, while saying these are probably not sufficient.

He also distinguishes behavioral consciousness from experiential consciousness. A system may behave as if conscious, especially near AGI, but humans also infer consciousness in each other partly because we run on the same biological substrate. Artificial systems will never share that substrate equivalence, so the question may remain harder to close.

### 23:37-25:16 - Kant, Spinoza, And Reality

Asked about Kant and Spinoza, Hassabis says Kant's idea that the mind helps create reality resonated with his neuroscience work. To understand reality, one must also understand the mind interpreting reality.

Spinoza matters to him as a way of thinking about the deeper, almost spiritual dimension of science. For Hassabis, doing AI and science is partly about reading the language of the universe.

This philosophical section loops back to the talk's beginning: AGI is not only a technology project, but also a way to study intelligence, consciousness, and the nature of reality.

### 25:16-26:51 - Rapid Fire

In rapid fire, Hassabis keeps his AGI estimate around 2030. Asked for a book to read around AGI, he names David Deutsch's `The Fabric of Reality`, saying he hopes AGI can help answer the questions in that book.

Asked for proudest DeepMind moments, he names AlphaFold. Asked which scientist he would want on his team in a high-stakes strategy game, he picks John von Neumann, pointing to strategy and game theory.
