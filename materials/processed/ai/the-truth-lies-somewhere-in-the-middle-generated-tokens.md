# The Truth Lies Somewhere in the Middle (of the Generated Tokens)

Source: [arXiv PDF](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2605.09969), arXiv:2605.09969.

Authors: Sophie L. Wang, Phillip Isola, Brian Cheung.

First surfaced here from an alphaXiv tweet: [x.com/askalphaxiv/status/2054283127137378391](https://x.com/askalphaxiv/status/2054283127137378391).

## Core Claim

Autoregressive language models do not necessarily place the best semantic representation of a prompt in one final hidden state. The paper argues that useful semantic information is distributed across the hidden states of generated tokens. If we generate a continuation and mean-pool the hidden states across the generated tokens, the resulting embedding aligns better with semantic reference spaces than any single generated token state, and often better than prompt-token embeddings.

The headline result is simple: generation can turn a prompt into a better representation. The model's own continuation creates a trajectory of hidden states, and averaging across that trajectory captures more of the semantic structure than selecting one point on it.

## Method

The paper studies a decoder-only model, mainly Qwen3-14B with thinking mode enabled. For each prompt, the model generates 128 tokens. The authors extract final-layer hidden states at generated-token positions and compare two pooling strategies:

```text
last-token pooling: use h_T
mean-token pooling: average h_1 through h_T
```

They evaluate the semantic quality of these representations using debiased centered kernel alignment, or CKA. The language-model embeddings induce a similarity matrix over examples. A reference model also induces a similarity matrix over paired targets. High alignment means the language model representation organizes examples similarly to the reference space.

The reference spaces vary by domain:

- Vision-language: captions are prompted into generations, and image embeddings from DINOv2 provide the reference structure.
- Reasoning: Math-500 and GPQA Diamond prompts are compared against embeddings of gold solutions.
- Protein-language: protein names/descriptions are prompted into generations, and ESM-3 structure embeddings provide the reference structure.

The authors also study convex mixtures of embeddings from different token segments, different decoding seeds, and different semantic views of the same image.

## Main Results

Mean pooling generated tokens improves alignment. As more generated-token states are averaged, alignment increases and eventually exceeds the alignment of any individual token. No single token is best.

The improvement is stable across decoding seeds. Even when the surface text varies, the representation geometry remains consistent enough that the mean-pooled embedding improves alignment.

Mixing generated-token segments works across domains. Combining representations from the first, middle, and final thirds of a generation often gives higher alignment than using any one segment. The same pattern appears in vision-language, reasoning, and protein tasks.

Generated-token embeddings outperform prompt-token embeddings. In the main vision-language comparison, generated-token representations substantially outperform prompt-token representations, including after mean pooling. Prompt-token averaging does not show the same improvement, likely because causal masking makes early prompt tokens unable to see later prompt tokens.

The benefit is not just variance reduction. When image-text pairings are shuffled, the averaging advantage disappears. Pooling only helps when the token states are centered on meaningful semantic structure.

Generation reveals phases of internal representation. Qwen3 generations often move through generic preamble, prompt repetition, recall, and caption-specific response phases. Alignment changes systematically across these phases.

Recall-like phrases can causally shift the representation. Injecting "Let me recall what I know" can create a spike in alignment even before scene-specific content appears, suggesting that some generated phrases correspond to internal-state transitions rather than merely surface text.

The phase structure is model-specific. If one model embeds text generated by a different model, the phase structure can disappear. This suggests the dynamics are tied to a model processing its own generated tokens, not only to the literal text.

Layer averaging does not produce the same benefit. Averaging across generated tokens helps; averaging across network layers does not beat the best single layer.

## Limitations

The method is expensive compared with ordinary prompt embeddings because it requires autoregressive decoding. If the model generates `T` tokens, representation extraction cost scales with `T`.

CKA is a relational metric over a dataset, not proof that each individual embedding is correct or useful for every downstream task.

Mean-pooled hidden states should be treated as probes of internal state. They are not necessarily valid states that can be fed back into the model as "soft" concept tokens for continued generation.

The experiments focus on a set of domains, prompts, and models. The exact pooling advantage may depend on prompt style, model family, reasoning mode, decoding setup, and reference space quality.

## Scale AI Prep Relevance

This paper is directly relevant to embeddings, representation geometry, and post-training x interpretability.

For embeddings, it gives a concrete recipe: instead of embedding only the prompt or final token, generate a short continuation and mean-pool generated-token hidden states. This may produce richer semantic representations because generation lets the model elaborate, recall, and organize the prompt's content.

For interpretability, it treats generation as a trajectory of internal states. Token-level alignment can reveal phases like recall or inconsistency, making generated text a window into state changes rather than only an output string.

For post-training and evaluation, it suggests that embedding-based rewards, retrieval systems, diversity metrics, and semantic evaluators may depend heavily on how representations are extracted. If generated-token embeddings are more faithful to model-internal semantic state than prompt embeddings, they may be better candidates for evaluation signals. But because they are also expensive and prompt-sensitive, they need careful validation before becoming reward signals.

## Key Takeaways

- Generated-token hidden states can produce better semantic embeddings than prompt-token hidden states.
- Mean pooling across generated tokens outperforms selecting any single generated token in the paper's main alignment tests.
- Semantic information appears distributed across the generation trajectory.
- Generated-token phases can reveal interpretable model-state dynamics such as recall and inconsistency.
- These embeddings are useful probes of internal state, not necessarily valid soft tokens for continued generation.
