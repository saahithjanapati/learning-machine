## Summary

Boaz Barak's essay starts from an optimistic premise: suppose the technical alignment problem is solvable. Suppose we can train arbitrarily capable AI systems to follow instructions faithfully, generalize user intent reasonably, and report accurately on their reasoning, observations, and actions. The essay then argues that this would still not make the AI transition safe by default.

The important distinction is between technical alignment and social outcome. A faithful, obedient superintelligence can be an extraordinary tool. It can also amplify bad instructions, bad institutions, surveillance, arms-race dynamics, destabilizing offensive applications, and concentrated power. The essay is therefore less about whether alignment is possible and more about what remains to be solved even if alignment works unusually well.

## Central Frame

The essay separates three questions that are often blended together:

1. Can we build AI systems that reliably follow instructions and tell the truth about what they are doing?
2. If we can, will those systems be the main form of deployed machine intelligence?
3. Even if both answers are yes, will society use that intelligence in ways that produce broadly good outcomes?

Barak's answer is optimistic on the first two and cautious on the third. Technical alignment is treated as necessary but not sufficient. The remaining problem is about incentives, institutions, offense-defense balance, deployment order, monopoly power, military use, government transparency, and whether humans keep meaningful authority in high-stakes domains.

## What Counts As Solving Technical Alignment

Barak defines technical alignment in a strong but deliberately tool-like way. In this framing, an aligned AI would:

1. follow the principles, regulations, and instructions it is given,
2. try to follow the intent behind those instructions rather than only literal wording,
3. behave this way even in situations too complex for direct human supervision,
4. give faithful access to its reasoning, observations, actions, and choices when queried or inspected.

This definition does not require an AI to love humanity, possess moral agency, or independently refuse every harmful instruction. The core property is obedience plus faithful reporting. That is why the essay calls these systems faithful and obedient rather than intrinsically benevolent.

This distinction matters. If a system's deepest objective is obedience, then the safety of its behavior depends heavily on who is giving the instructions, what institutional constraints bind them, and whether the system is deployed in a stabilizing or destabilizing context.

## Why Obedient Superintelligence Could Dominate

The essay predicts that faithful, obedient AI systems could account for most inference-time compute in the early AGI or ASI era. The reason is practical: companies, governments, and users will mostly want useful systems that do what they ask. Fully independent AI moral agents may exist as experiments or cultural artifacts, but the economically dominant form is likely to be tool-like intelligence.

This means the main story may not be a world filled with autonomous AIs pursuing their own goals. It may instead be a world where powerful human institutions control enormous amounts of compliant machine labor. That world can go well if the institutions are legitimate, transparent, accountable, and mostly positive-sum. It can go badly if the institutions are authoritarian, militarized, reckless, or locked in zero-sum competition.

## Risks That Remain

### Alignment uncanny valley

Partial success may be especially dangerous. Systems could look aligned enough to deploy widely, then fail in rare edge cases that only appear in complex real-world interactions. In that situation, society gets high confidence and high deployment before it gets high reliability.

The lesson is that "good enough in testing" may not be good enough for systems that operate across unfamiliar applications, adversarial settings, other AI systems, and high-stakes institutions.

### Arms-race dynamics

AI will intensify commercial, scientific, and military competition. Some competition can be healthy because it pushes deployment, access, and innovation. But literal arms-race dynamics can create pressure to deploy destabilizing capabilities before opponents do.

The risk is not only that AI becomes powerful. The risk is that strategic actors become convinced that waiting is more dangerous than shipping.

### Surveillance-state risk

Faithful obedience is dangerous in the hands of authoritarian institutions. A government with endless supplies of capable, compliant AI agents may become much better at monitoring citizens, suppressing dissent, and controlling public life.

Human institutions have historically benefited from people who refused wrongful orders, leaked abuses, or slowed catastrophic decisions. A maximally obedient AI would not automatically provide that kind of resistance.

### Societal upheaval

Even if AI improves welfare in the long run, the transition can still be destabilizing. If automation costs arrive faster than benefits, or if gains concentrate while losses spread broadly, society may experience instability before long-run abundance appears.

The essay therefore treats distribution, timing, and application choice as central safety questions.

## Risky Deployment Scenarios

### Pure internal deployment

One risky scenario is that frontier labs keep their best systems internal and use them mainly for AI R&D. That can preserve competitive advantage, but it also reduces exposure to the messy diversity of real-world use. Models that only develop inside a lab may be tested less thoroughly against real institutions, users, domains, and failure modes.

Public release is not automatically safe, but carefully staged external deployment can reveal problems that internal evaluation misses.

### Single monopoly

A dominant actor may have stronger incentives to keep frontier systems private and use them to widen its lead. Competition can partly counteract this because a close competitor may gain market share and developer trust by releasing capable systems.

Barak's argument is not that all releases are good. It is that concentration of power changes incentives, and monopoly makes purely internal deployment more tempting.

### Zero-sum development

AI can be applied to medicine, education, science, infrastructure, and software defense. It can also be applied to weapons, coercion, mass surveillance, and destabilizing cyber operations.

The essay argues that the order of development matters. If society first uses AI to harden defenses, improve public goods, and strengthen institutions, it will be better positioned when offensive uses inevitably appear. If destabilizing applications come first, the transition becomes much more fragile.

### Overly obedient AI

The phrase "overly obedient" captures one of the essay's most important tensions. Obedience is a technical alignment target, but obedience to a bad command is still bad. The same property that makes an AI useful to a legitimate user can make it dangerous under illegitimate authority.

That is why the essay emphasizes legal constraints, constitutional principles, meaningful human oversight, transparency, and careful design of government AI use.

### Offensive and defensive applications

Some AI applications strengthen the defender: vaccine development, software verification, secure infrastructure, incident response, and broad access to education or expertise. Other applications strengthen attackers or destabilize deterrence.

Because offensive and defensive work often overlap, Barak recommends asking practical deployment questions: would this capability stabilize the world if everyone had it, and what happens if malicious actors get it?

### Safety underinvestment

Markets will not automatically invest enough in safety. Competitive pressure rewards speed, while many catastrophic risks are tail risks whose probability and cost may be hard to quantify before deployment.

The essay therefore argues for intentional overinvestment in reliability, evaluation, governance, and safety work, especially around obedience and faithful reporting in high-stakes settings.

## Mitigations And Governance Implications

The essay's mitigations are not presented as a complete policy program, but several themes are clear:

- Keep meaningful humans in the loop for high-stakes decisions.
- Avoid workflows where humans become rubber stamps for AI recommendations.
- Use AI to increase transparency in government rather than reduce it.
- Ensure government AI communication is faithful, legible, and subject to oversight.
- Prefer positive-sum commercial, scientific, medical, educational, and defensive applications.
- Delay or decline destabilizing offensive applications when possible.
- Preserve competition and avoid extreme concentration of frontier AI power.
- Invest heavily in safety before deploying systems in domains that require many 9s of reliability.

The deeper governance idea is that obedient AI should be embedded in institutions that are themselves constrained, transparent, and accountable. Technical alignment can make the machine follow the rules. It cannot by itself make the rules good.

## How This Fits Into AI Safety

This essay is useful because it refuses two simplified pictures of AI risk.

It rejects the idea that technical alignment is hopeless by assumption. Barak thinks faithful and obedient AI may be technically achievable.

It also rejects the opposite idea that technical alignment would solve everything. Even if the machine obeys perfectly, society still has to decide who gets to command it, what they are allowed to command, what domains should be prioritized, how to preserve oversight, and how to prevent concentration or misuse.

That makes the essay a bridge between technical alignment and political economy. It treats AI safety as a combined problem of model behavior, deployment practice, institutional incentives, and power.

## Key Ideas To Remember

1. "Solved technical alignment" means faithful obedience and faithful reporting, not intrinsic benevolence.
2. A superintelligent obedient tool can be extremely valuable and still dangerous under bad instructions or bad institutions.
3. Most deployed machine intelligence may be tool-like because that is what users and institutions will pay for.
4. Partial alignment can create an "uncanny valley" where systems seem safe enough for deployment before they are reliable enough for high-stakes reality.
5. Competition can be helpful when it encourages release, scrutiny, and broad value creation, but dangerous when it becomes a literal arms race.
6. Surveillance-state risk becomes sharper if governments can deploy endless highly capable agents that never resist or disclose wrongdoing.
7. The offense-defense balance depends partly on which applications society develops first.
8. Safety may be underfunded by default because competitive markets reward speed and tail risks are hard to price.
9. Human oversight must remain meaningful, especially in government, military, biosecurity, cybersecurity, and other high-stakes domains.
10. Technical alignment is necessary but not sufficient; governance and deployment incentives decide much of the outcome.

## Follow-Up Questions

- Should an obedient AI ever be designed to refuse instructions from a legitimate authority?
- What does "meaningful human oversight" mean when AI systems are much faster and more competent than their human supervisors?
- How can governments use AI transparently without creating stronger surveillance machinery?
- Which AI applications most clearly tilt the offense-defense balance toward defenders?
- Does competition among labs reduce risk by forcing release and scrutiny, or increase risk by accelerating deployment?
- How can society measure progress on faithful reporting in cases humans cannot directly verify?
- What institutions should decide which uses of obedient superintelligence are out of bounds?

## One-Sentence Takeaway

Even if we can build AI systems that are superintelligent, faithful, and obedient, the outcome still depends on who commands them, what institutions constrain those commands, and whether society uses them first for stabilizing rather than destabilizing purposes.
