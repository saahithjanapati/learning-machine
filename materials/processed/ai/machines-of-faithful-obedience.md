## Summary

This essay argues that even if we solve the core technical alignment problem and can build superintelligent AIs that are both faithful and obedient, major societal and geopolitical risks would still remain. The post’s key move is to define a relatively strong notion of “solved alignment,” then argue that this would still not guarantee a good world outcome.

## What “Solving” Alignment Means Here

Barak uses a demanding notion of technical alignment. In his framing, aligned AIs would:

1. faithfully follow the principles, instructions, and intent they are given, even in settings too complex for direct human supervision,
2. provide accurate and faithful access to their reasoning, observations, and choices at whatever level of granularity we want.

This does **not** require the AI to intrinsically care about humanity or to refuse harmful instructions on its own. It only requires robust obedience and faithful reporting.

## Core Claim

The main thesis is:

- faithful and obedient superintelligent AIs could be incredibly useful,
- but their existence does not automatically imply a safe or flourishing society,
- because the broader deployment environment, power structure, and application choices still matter enormously.

In other words, “technical alignment solved” is not the same as “the AI transition goes well.”

## Why Barak Still Thinks These Systems Would Be Dominant

The essay predicts that in an AGI/ASI world, the bulk of inference-time compute would likely be spent on obedient, tool-like AIs deployed by major actors, rather than on fully autonomous moral agents. So if aligned systems are possible, they may constitute most of the world’s effective machine intelligence.

This matters because the main risk is then not just rogue independent agents, but what powerful institutions choose to do with vast amounts of faithful machine labor.

## Main Risks That Remain

### 1. Alignment uncanny valley

Partial success may be more dangerous than total failure. We may get systems that are aligned well enough for deployment but still fail in rare edge cases in complex real-world settings, discovering those failures only after major harm.

### 2. Arms-race dynamics

Competition between firms or nations could push development toward rapid deployment, military applications, or risky races for strategic advantage.

### 3. Surveillance-state risk

Obedient superintelligent systems could dramatically strengthen authoritarian control by giving governments highly capable agents that never leak, resist, or defect.

### 4. Societal upheaval

Even if AI is beneficial in the long run, the near-term transition could produce severe instability if automation harms people faster than benefits are distributed.

## Risky Scenarios

The essay highlights several scenarios that would make these risks worse.

### Pure internal deployment

If labs keep their most capable models private and use them only internally for AI R&D, they may create dangerous feedback loops where models are used to improve successor models without the outside-world exposure and stress-testing that public deployment provides.

### Single monopoly

A dominant actor has stronger incentives to keep frontier systems internal. Competition, in contrast, can create pressure to release models and expose them to broader testing and public value creation.

### Zero-sum development

If governments and institutions focus AI progress primarily on military or surveillance applications rather than positive-sum commercial or scientific uses, risks rise sharply.

### Overly obedient AI

Barak stresses that perfect obedience is not unambiguously good. Historically, humanity has benefited from people who disobeyed unjust or catastrophic orders. A maximally obedient AI would not naturally play the role of a whistleblower or conscientious dissenter.

### Offense-defense imbalance

The order in which AI capabilities are developed matters. Using AI first to strengthen vaccines, software security, and defense is much safer than prioritizing destabilizing offensive applications.

## Proposed Mitigations

The post suggests several broad mitigations:

- keep humans in the loop in high-stakes decisions,
- avoid turning humans into mere rubber stamps for AI outputs,
- maintain transparency and legibility for AI used in government,
- invest heavily in defensive and stabilizing applications,
- overinvest in safety because market incentives alone will likely underprovide it.

## Main Takeaway

The essay’s central message is that technical alignment is only one part of a much bigger governance and deployment problem. Even in a world full of faithful and obedient AIs, social structure, institutional incentives, concentration of power, and application choices still determine whether the outcome is good or catastrophic.
