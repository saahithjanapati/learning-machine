## Summary

Benjamin Todd asks whether the last few months of frontier AI progress are the beginning of a true acceleration. His answer is cautious: there are signs of acceleration, especially around Anthropic and agentic coding, but the evidence is still too thin to distinguish a durable feedback loop from catch-up, an unusually large training run, or a noisy single data point.

The article reviews four evidence streams:

1. benchmark trends,
2. frontier AI revenue,
3. AI uplift for AI researchers,
4. compute prices.

Todd's bottom line is that acceleration is possible but not yet established. Even without acceleration, he argues that continuation of current trends would still be extremely fast.

## Core Thesis

The strongest current acceleration case comes from Anthropic:

- Claude Opus 4.6 arrived soon after Opus 4.5 and improved real-world agentic tasks.
- Mythos appears especially strong on agentic and cyber-relevant capabilities.
- Anthropic revenue has grown much faster than OpenAI revenue.
- Anthropic researchers report large AI productivity uplifts.

But Todd does not treat this as settled. The apparent acceleration could instead reflect:

- Anthropic catching up after lagging,
- a one-off larger training run,
- unusually agentic benchmark selection,
- fast but still subcritical AI assistance to researchers.

## Evidence Stream 1: Benchmarks

Todd treats benchmark acceleration as the clearest possible signal, but also the easiest place to overinterpret a single release.

### Epoch ECI

Epoch's ECI combines 37 benchmarks into one index. Todd notes that Epoch already sees a faster trend starting in early 2024. The question is whether Mythos is above that newer trend.

External estimates suggest Mythos may be on trend for Epoch's ECI. Anthropic's internal ECI-like index, however, makes Mythos look more like about six months of progress in roughly two months.

Todd's interpretation is that the difference may come from benchmark composition. Anthropic's internal index may be heavier on agentic and coding tasks, while Epoch's index may be more influenced by high-end math. For AI R&D feedback loops, Todd thinks the agentic coding-heavy view may be more important to watch.

### METR Time Horizon

Todd highlights METR's time horizon benchmark as a particularly relevant signal because it measures agentic coding and AI R&D-like tasks.

His read:

- Claude Opus 4.6 is slightly above trend for 50% success, but within uncertainty.
- At 80% success, Opus 4.6 looks on trend or slightly below.
- If Mythos correlates with Anthropic's ECI, it might imply a 50% success horizon around 40 hours and an 80% horizon around 6 hours, though those estimates need actual METR results.

Todd says the key watch item is whether Mythos lands around that 6-hour 80% reliability mark.

## Evidence Stream 2: Revenue

Todd likes revenue as a hard-to-game benchmark because customers paying more usually means the product is more useful.

His main revenue claims:

- frontier AI revenue excluding Gemini looks roughly linear on a log chart, which means steady exponential growth,
- 2024 growth was about 3.2x,
- 2025 growth was about 4.7x,
- 2026 growth annualized to date is about 8x,
- OpenAI has grown around 3-4x per year,
- Anthropic has grown around 10x per year.

As Anthropic becomes a larger share of frontier AI revenue, aggregate revenue growth trends upward. Todd frames the next question as: does Anthropic keep growing near 10x per year after it becomes a larger share of the market, or does it converge toward OpenAI's lower growth rate?

He also notes that Anthropic's first-three-months-of-year revenue growth was extremely high when annualized, but likely not sustainable because compute and pricing constraints eventually bind.

## Evidence Stream 3: AI Uplift For AI Researchers

Todd is skeptical that AI uplift alone explains the apparent Mythos jump.

He reports that Anthropic researchers estimated large productivity gains:

- Opus 4.6 made researchers about 2x more productive at the median and 2.5x at the mean.
- Mythos had a reported geometric mean uplift around 4x.
- In an internal survey, one of 18 researchers thought Mythos Preview was already a drop-in replacement for an entry-level research scientist or engineer, while four thought it had a 50% chance with three months of scaffolding iteration.

Todd is cautious about these numbers. He points to evidence that software engineers can overestimate AI productivity gains and cites outside estimates around 1.6x true labor productivity uplift. Since AI progress also depends on compute and other inputs, he estimates a 1.6x labor uplift might translate into only about a 1.2x increase in the overall rate of AI progress.

Even if the 4x researcher productivity number were right, he says Anthropic estimates this would imply less than a 2x overall speedup in AI progress, not enough to explain the full acceleration story by itself.

## Evidence Stream 4: Compute Prices

Todd argues that if AI systems become economically valuable remote workers, the value of compute should rise toward the marginal value produced by those workers.

The rough argument:

- current H100 rental cost is around $2/hour,
- one H100 can run roughly ten GPT-5-level workers,
- typical US white-collar wages are around $50/hour,
- if AI workers become close substitutes for human workers, compute prices could rise a lot.

Historically, compute prices have fallen about 30% per year as hardware improves. Todd notes that in the last four months, prices instead rose about 30%. He treats this as a possible signal, but not yet decisive. It could be a temporary blip caused by strong demand from products like Claude Code and Cowork, or it could be the start of an upward trend.

## Key Indicators Todd Wants To Watch

Todd closes with five indicators for the next three months:

- Mythos's METR time horizon at 80% reliability.
- Whether the next one or two major model releases are also above trend on ECI.
- Whether Anthropic revenue stays on its faster trend or converges toward OpenAI's trend.
- Whether better AI-uplift estimates become available.
- Whether compute prices keep rising.

## What To Remember

- The acceleration case is plausible but not proven.
- Anthropic is the main source of the apparent acceleration signal.
- Agentic coding benchmarks may matter more for AI R&D feedback loops than broad benchmark aggregates.
- Revenue is an important check because it is harder to game than benchmarks.
- AI researcher uplift seems real, but Todd doubts it is already large enough to explain a major acceleration.
- Compute prices are a market signal worth watching, especially if AI workers become compute-constrained.
- A continuation of current trends is already extremely fast; acceleration would make timelines shorter still.
