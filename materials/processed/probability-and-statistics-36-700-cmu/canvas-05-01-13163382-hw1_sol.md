2. Use the axioms of probability to prove all five statements in Wasserman Equation 1.1 on page 6. (Note that these are some properties of $\mathbb{P}$ that are derived from the axioms, and not part of the axioms themselves)

**Solution:** (i) Let $A \subseteq \Omega$. Define $\{A_i\}_{i=1}^\infty$, where $A_1 = A$ and $A_i = \emptyset$ for $i > 1$. Then
$$\mathbb{P}(A) = \mathbb{P}(\cup_{i=1}^\infty A_i) \implies \mathbb{P}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}(A_i) \quad \text{Axiom 3}$$
$$\implies \mathbb{P}(A) = \mathbb{P}(A) + \sum_{i=2}^\infty \mathbb{P}(\emptyset)$$
$$\implies \sum_{i=2}^\infty \mathbb{P}(\emptyset) = 0$$
$$\implies \mathbb{P}(\emptyset) = 0 \quad \text{Axiom 1}$$

(ii) Let $C = B \setminus A$. Then $A \cap C = \emptyset$ and $A \cup C = B$. So by applying (v),
$$\mathbb{P}(B) = \mathbb{P}(A \cup C) = \mathbb{P}(A) + \mathbb{P}(C) \geq \mathbb{P}(A).$$

(iii) Since $A \subset \Omega$, $\mathbb{P}(A) \leq \mathbb{P}(\Omega) = 1$ by (ii). Also, $\emptyset \subset A$ so $0 = \mathbb{P}(\emptyset) \leq \mathbb{P}(A)$.
(iv) Since $\Omega = A \cup A^c$ and $A$ and $A^c$ are disjoint, $1 = \mathbb{P}(\Omega) = \mathbb{P}(A) + \mathbb{P}(A^c)$ by (v) and hence $\mathbb{P}(A^c) = 1 - \mathbb{P}(A)$.
(v) Let $A_1 = A, A_2 = B, A_i = \emptyset$ for $i \geq 3$. Then $A_1, A_2, \dots$ are disjoint. Hence,
$$\mathbb{P}(A \cup B) = \mathbb{P}(\cup_{i=1}^\infty A_i) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}(A_i)$$
$$= \mathbb{P}(A) + \mathbb{P}(B) + \mathbb{P}(A_3) + \mathbb{P}(A_4) + \dots$$
$$= \mathbb{P}(A) + \mathbb{P}(B) + 0 + 0 + \dots \quad \text{by (i)}$$
$$= \mathbb{P}(A) + \mathbb{P}(B).$$

3. **Union bound:** Consider events $A_1, \dots, A_n$. Prove (using the axioms of probability) that
$$\mathbb{P}\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mathbb{P}(A_i).$$

*Hint: Define a sequence of events $B_n$ for $n = 1, 2, \dots$, where $B_1 = A_1$ and $B_n = A_n \setminus \cup_{i=1}^{n-1} A_i$ for $n > 2$. Argue that these events are disjoint. Show that $\mathbb{P}(\cup_{n=1}^\infty A_n) = \mathbb{P}(\cup_{n=1}^\infty B_n)$, and use the countable additivity property of probability measures.*

**Solution:** Let $B_n = A_n \setminus \cup_{i=1}^{n-1} A_i = A_n \cap (\cup_{i=1}^{n-1} A_i)^c$; we want to show that $B_n$ are mutually disjoint and that $\cup_{n=1}^\infty B_n = \cup_{n=1}^\infty A_n$.

* Consider any arbitrary $B_j$ and $B_k$. In order to prove that the $B_n$ are disjoint, we need to prove that $B_j \cap B_k = \emptyset$.
Without loss of generality, assume $k > j$. Then
$$B_j \cap B_k = \left[ A_j \cap \left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{j-1} A_i\right)^c \right] \cap \left[ A_k \cap \left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{k-1} A_i\right)^c \right].$$
But since $j < k$, $A_j \cap (\cup_{i=1}^{k-1} A_i)^c = \emptyset$. Thus, $B_j \cap B_k = \emptyset$.

* In order to prove $\cup_{i=1}^\infty B_i = \cup_{i=1}^\infty A_i$, we use induction to show that $\cup_{i=1}^N B_i = \cup_{i=1}^N A_i$ for all $N \in \mathbb{N}$.
If $N = 1$, then $\cup_{i=1}^N B_i = B_1 = A_1 = \cup_{i=1}^N A_i$.
Now suppose $\cup_{i=1}^N B_i = \cup_{i=1}^N A_i$ for some arbitrary $N \in \mathbb{N}$. Then:
$$\bigcup_{i=1}^{N+1} B_n = \left[ \bigcup_{i=1}^N B_i \right] \cup B_{N+1} = \left[ \bigcup_{i=1}^N A_i \right] \cup B_{N+1}$$
$$= \left[ \bigcup_{i=1}^N A_i \right] \cup \left[ A_{N+1} \cap \left( \bigcup_{i=1}^N A_i \right)^c \right]$$
$$= \left[ \left( \bigcup_{i=1}^N A_i \right) \cup \left( \bigcup_{i=1}^N A_i \right)^c \right] \cap \left[ \left( \bigcup_{i=1}^N A_i \right) \cup A_{N+1} \right]$$
$$= \Omega \cap \left[ \bigcup_{i=1}^{N+1} A_i \right] = \bigcup_{i=1}^{N+1} A_i$$

**Alternative proof:** Let $S_0 = \emptyset$, $S_n = \cup_{i=1}^n A_n$ and $B_n = A_n - S_{n-1}$. Since $B_i \subset A_i$ for all $i$, clearly $\cup_{i=1}^\infty B_i \subset \cup_{i=1}^\infty A_i$. To prove the reverse inclusion, let $x \in \cup_{i=1}^\infty A_i$ be given. Let $k$ be the smallest number such that $x \in A_k$. Then $x \notin S_{k-1}$ and hence $x \in A_k - S_{k-1} = B_k$. Therefore the reverse inclusion holds and our claim is proven.

4. **The Monty Hall problem:** Do **Exercise 1.10** in Wasserman’s book page 14.

**Solution:** The only possible outcomes are
$$\Omega = \{(1, 2), (1, 3), (2, 3), (3, 2)\}.$$
By assumption
$$\mathbb{P}(\{(1, 2), (1, 3)\}) = \mathbb{P}(\{(2, 3)\}) = \mathbb{P}(\{(3, 2)\}) = 1/3.$$
If you don’t switch, you will win if $\omega = (1, 2)$ or $\omega = (1, 3)$ so the probability of winning is $1/3$. If you switch, you win if $\omega = (2, 3)$ or $\omega = (3, 2)$. In this case, the probability of winning is $2/3$. Therefore, you should switch for a higher probability of winning.

5. Do **Exercise 1.20** in Wasserman’s book page 16.

**Solution:** By choosing a coin at random, we assign the uniform distribution over every coin. That is we assume,
$$\mathbb{P}(C_i) = \frac{1}{5}, \quad \forall i = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5.$$

(a)
$$\mathbb{P}(C_i | H) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(H | C_i) \mathbb{P}(C_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^5 \mathbb{P}(H | C_i) \mathbb{P}(C_i)} = \frac{p_i \frac{1}{5}}{\sum_{i=1}^5 p_i \frac{1}{5}} = \frac{2}{5} p_i = \begin{cases} 0 & i = 1 \\ 1/10 & i = 2 \\ 1/5 & i = 3 \\ 3/10 & i = 4 \\ 2/5 & i = 5 \end{cases}.$$

(b)
$$\mathbb{P}(H_2 | H_1) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(H_1 H_2)}{\mathbb{P}(H_1)} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^5 \mathbb{P}(H_1 H_2 | C_i) \mathbb{P}(C_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^5 \mathbb{P}(H_1 | C_i) \mathbb{P}(C_i)} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^5 p_i^2 \frac{1}{5}}{\sum_{i=1}^5 p_i \frac{1}{5}} = \frac{15/8}{5/2} = \frac{3}{4}.$$

(c)
$$\mathbb{P}(C_i | B_4) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(B_4 | C_i) \mathbb{P}(C_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^5 \mathbb{P}(B_4 | C_i) \mathbb{P}(C_i)} = \frac{p_i(1 - p_i)^3 \frac{1}{5}}{\sum_{i=1}^5 p_i(1 - p_i)^3 \frac{1}{5}} = \frac{256}{46} p_i(1 - p_i)^3 = \begin{cases} 0 & i = 1 \\ 27/46 & i = 2 \\ 16/46 & i = 3 \\ 3/46 & i = 4 \\ 0 & i = 5 \end{cases}.$$

6. Suppose that $A$ and $B$ are independent events. Show that $A$ and $B^c$ are independent events.

**Solution:**
$$\mathbb{P}(A) = \mathbb{P}[(A \cap B) \cup (A \cap B^c)] \implies \mathbb{P}(A) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap B) + \mathbb{P}(A \cap B^c) \quad A \cap B \text{ and } A \cap B^c \text{ disjoint}$$
$$\implies \mathbb{P}(A \cap B^c) = \mathbb{P}(A) - \mathbb{P}(A \cap B)$$
$$\implies \mathbb{P}(A \cap B^c) = \mathbb{P}(A) - \mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(B) \quad A \text{ and } B \text{ independent}$$
$$\implies \mathbb{P}(A \cap B^c) = \mathbb{P}(A)[1 - \mathbb{P}(B)]$$
$$\implies \mathbb{P}(A \cap B^c) = \mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(B^c)$$
Therefore, $A$ and $B^c$ are independent.

7. Suppose that $A$ and $B$ are disjoint events. Give a necessary and sufficient condition for them to be independent.

**Solution:** Either $\mathbb{P}(A) = 0$ or $\mathbb{P}(B) = 0$.
($\implies$) Disjoint events implies $\mathbb{P}(A \cap B) = 0$. If the condition holds, $\mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(B) = 0$. Thus, $\mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(B) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap B)$ which implies the independence.
($\impliedby$) Independent events implies $\mathbb{P}(A \cap B) = \mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(B)$. Since $A$ and $B$ are disjoint, $\mathbb{P}(A \cap B) = 0$. Therefore, $\mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(B) = 0$ which implies the condition.

8. Show that if $\mathbb{P}(A) = 0$ or $\mathbb{P}(A) = 1$ then $A$ is independent of every other event. Show that if $A$ is independent of itself then $\mathbb{P}(A)$ is either 0 or 1.

**Solution:** Let $B$ be an event and assume that $\mathbb{P}(A)=1$. Then by property (v) from Question 2, we have that
$$\mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(B) = \mathbb{P}(B) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap B) + \mathbb{P}(A^c \cap B) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap B)$$
where $\mathbb{P}(A^c \cap B) \leq \mathbb{P}(A^c) = 0$ by Question 2 property (ii) and assumption.
The case $\mathbb{P}(A) = 0$ follows from the observation $\mathbb{P}(A \cap B) \leq \mathbb{P}(A) = 0$, which implies
$$\mathbb{P}(A \cap B) = 0 = \mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(B).$$

Now, assume that $A$ is independent of itself. Then, we have that:
$$\mathbb{P}(A) = \mathbb{P}(A \cap A) = \mathbb{P}(A)\mathbb{P}(A) = \mathbb{P}(A)^2.$$
The result follows from the fact that the root of polynomial equation $x = x^2$ is either 0 or 1.
