## 1 Basics of Stochastic Convergence

Remember the WLLN from last chapter. Assume that $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are i.i.d with $\text{Var}(X) < \infty$. Let $\mu = \mathbb{E}[X_1]$, $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}[X_1]$ and $\bar{X}_n = n^{-1} \sum_i X_i$. Then the weak law of large numbers says: $\epsilon > 0$,

$$\forall \epsilon > 0, \quad \mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \ge \epsilon) \to 0,$$

as $n \to \infty$, which essentially assures us that the average of independent and identically distributed random variables "converges" to their expectation.

This type of convergence is quite common and has a name: it is called **convergence in probability**. So the weak LLN tells us that the sample average converges to the population average in probability. This is called the weak law because convergence in probability is often referred to as weak convergence. We will discuss convergence more systematically soon.

The weak LLN is an example where we tried to reason about the limiting behaviour of a sequence of random variables:

$$Y_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i.$$

In statistics we commonly estimate parameters using data, and in this case our estimate forms a sequence of random variables (as we collect more data). We would like to reason about the limiting behaviour of our estimates (do they "converge" to the truth, what is their distribution around the truth and so on). This is what we refer to as **large sample theory**.

Suppose we have a sequence of random variables $Y_1, \dots, Y_n$, and another random variable $Y$. The two most basic forms of stochastic convergence are:

1. **Convergence in Probability**: We say the sequence $Y_n$ converges to $Y$ if:
   $$\forall \epsilon > 0, \quad \lim_{n \to \infty} \mathbb{P}(|Y_n - Y| \ge \epsilon) = 0,$$
   and we write
   $$Y_n \xrightarrow{P} Y.$$
   An important special case is when $X = c$ for some constant $c$, for example the weak LLN, where $Y_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ is the average of $n$ iid RVs and $c = \mu = \mathbb{E}(Y_n) = \mathbb{E}(X_1)$.

2. **Convergence in Distribution**: Let $F_n$ and $F$ denote the CDFs of $Y_n$ and $Y$, respectively. The sequence $Y_n$ converges in distribution to $Y$ if:
   $$\lim_{n \to \infty} F_n(t) = F(t),$$
   at all points $t$ where $F$ is continuous, and we write
   $$Y_n \xrightarrow{d} Y \quad \text{or} \quad Y_n \rightsquigarrow Y.$$
   An important example is the central limit theorem, which says that the average of i.i.d. RVs, rescaled appropriately converges in distribution to a standard normal distribution:
   $$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1).$$
   If you think about it, this is a really stunning result. You do not assume anything about the RVs except their first two moments need to exist, and you conclude that the average approaches a Gaussian distribution.

While convergence in distribution only makes a statement about the distribution, convergence in probability is a statement about the value of the random variables. It is not too difficult to show that convergence in probability $\implies$ convergence in distribution (see Wasserman's book). The reverse is not, in general, true.

Generally we care about convergence in probability because a desirable property of estimators is that they are consistent for the parameter they estimate, where consistent means "converge in probability". When we do estimation, we will see that MLEs, for example, are consistent. We care about convergence in distribution because it can facilitate probability calculations (the central limit theorem makes it easier to calculate many probabilities).

**Example 1**: Let $X \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$ with $p = 0.5$ and $n = 10,000$. Calculate $\mathbb{P}(X > 70)$.

## 2 The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Let $X_1, \dots, X_n$ be i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, both finite. Define:
$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i.$$
Then the standardized variable:
$$Z_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \rightsquigarrow N(0, 1).$$

1. Perhaps the first thing to verify is that at least the mean and variance are correct. Indeed:
   - $\mathbb{E}[Z_n] = 0$.
   - $\mathbb{E}[Z_n^2] = 1$.

2. There are two common ways to interpret the Central Limit Theorem (and more broadly convergence in distribution):

- The first is to suppose I repeated the experiment, i.e. I draw many sequences: $\{X_1^1, \dots, X_n^1\}, \{X_1^2, \dots, X_n^2\}, \dots, \{X_1^k, \dots, X_n^k\}$, and computed their centered and normalized averages $Z_n^1, \dots, Z_n^k$.
  The central limit theorem then tells us that these normalized averages will (approximately) have a standard Gaussian distribution. For instance, you could imagine computing a histogram of the averages, and this will follow a Gaussian law.

- The second way is to suppose that before I did the experiment I asked the question what is the probability of a certain outcome:
  $$\mathbb{P}(a \le \bar{X} \le b).$$
  Then the central limit theorem tells us that:
  $$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{P}(a \le \bar{X} \le b) &= \mathbb{P}\left(\frac{\sqrt{n}(a - \mu)}{\sigma} \le Z_n \le \frac{\sqrt{n}(b - \mu)}{\sigma}\right) \\ &\approx \mathbb{P}\left(\frac{\sqrt{n}(a - \mu)}{\sigma} \le Z \le \frac{\sqrt{n}(b - \mu)}{\sigma}\right) \\ &= \Phi\left(\frac{\sqrt{n}(b - \mu)}{\sigma}\right) - \Phi\left(\frac{\sqrt{n}(a - \mu)}{\sigma}\right) \end{aligned}$$
  where $\Phi$ is the cdf of a standard Normal.

**Example**: Let $X_i$ be iid RVs with finite mean $\mu = 0.5$ and variance $\sigma^2 = 1/4$. Calculate $\mathbb{P}(0.4 < \bar{X}_n < 0.6)$ using the CLT.
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{P}(0.4 < \bar{X}_n < 0.6) &= \mathbb{P}(-0.1 < \bar{X}_n - \mu < 0.1) \\ &= \mathbb{P}\left(\frac{-0.1 \times \sqrt{n}}{\sigma} < \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} < \frac{0.1 \times \sqrt{n}}{\sigma}\right) \\ &\approx \Phi(0.2\sqrt{n}) - \Phi(-0.2\sqrt{n}), \end{aligned}$$
where $\Phi$ is the cdf of $N(0, 1)$. In the special case of $n = 100$, we have $\mathbb{P}(0.4 < \bar{X}_n < 0.6) \approx 0.9545$.

This is a much sharper approximation than the bound obtained using Chebyshev's inequality (see Chapter 4):
$$\mathbb{P}(0.4 < \bar{X}_n < 0.6) = \mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| < 0.1) = 1 - \mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \ge 0.1) \ge 1 - \frac{25}{n}.$$
In particular, when $n = 100$ we have $\mathbb{P}(0.4 < \bar{X}_n < 0.6) \ge 0.75$.

In a sense concentration inequalities are non-asymptotic forms of the CLT. I.e. the CLT is something that holds when the number of random variables we are averaging $n \to \infty$, while concentration inequalities give us less precise control over the average, but are valid even for small $n$.

A key ingredient in the application of CLT is that we know the value of $\sigma$. In many applications, $\sigma^2$ is not known and needs to be estimated. Given an iid sample of size $n$, a popular estimator of $\sigma^2$ is
$$S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n - 1} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2.$$
One can show that $S_n^2 \xrightarrow{P} \sigma^2$. Then using Slutsky's theorem we obtain the following version of CLT:
$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{S_n} \rightsquigarrow N(0, 1).$$

3. Further topics:
   - **Rate of convergence**: If we define the distance between the CDF of the average, and the CDF of a Gaussian appropriately, we can ask how far the two CDFs are for a *finite* sample size $n$. The answer is $C/\sqrt{n}$ for some constant $C$. These results are typically called Berry-Esseen bounds. They assure us that the convergence to normality can happen quite quickly in some important cases.
   - **Multivariate CLT**: If we average a collection of independent random vectors then they will converge in distribution to a multivariate Gaussian.
     Let $X_n$ be a sequence of iid random vectors
     $$X_n = \begin{pmatrix} X_{1n} \\ X_{2n} \\ \vdots \\ X_{kn} \end{pmatrix}$$
     with mean
     $$\mu = \begin{pmatrix} \mu_1 \\ \mu_2 \\ \vdots \\ \mu_k \end{pmatrix}$$
     where $\mu_j = \mathbb{E}(X_{jn})$, and variance matrix $\Sigma$ where $\Sigma_{jl} = \text{Cov}(X_{jn}, X_{ln})$, then
     $$\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \Sigma).$$

## 3 The Delta Method

Given that $Y_n = \bar{X}_n$ converges in distribution to a Gaussian, one can ask about functions of $Y_n$. Under some regularity conditions these also converge to a Gaussian, and the delta method tells us how to compute the mean and variance of the new Gaussian.

If
$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(Y_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \rightsquigarrow N(0, 1)$$
and $g$ is a differentiable function such that $g'(\mu) \neq 0$. Then
$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(g(Y_n) - g(\mu))}{g'(\mu)\sigma} \rightsquigarrow N(0, 1).$$

The formal proof, though quite elementary, is a bit tricky and is given in the Appendix. Here we provide a quick heuristic argument. Using Taylor expansion, $g(Y_n) - g(\mu) = g'(\mu)(Y_n - \mu) + Rem$, where $Rem$ is the remainder term. Thus
$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(g(Y_n) - g(\mu))}{g'(\mu)\sigma} \approx \frac{\sqrt{n}(Y_n - \mu)g'(\mu)}{g'(\mu)\sigma} \rightsquigarrow N(0, 1).$$

**Example**: Let $X_1, \dots, X_n, \dots$ be iid with finite mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. By CLT we have $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \sigma^2)$. Let $W_n = e^{\bar{X}_n}$, then the Delta Method implies that $\sqrt{n}(W_n - e^\mu) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \sigma^2 e^{2\mu})$.

The Delta method extends to the multivariate case. Let $Y_n = (Y_{n1}, \dots, Y_{nk})^T$ be a sequence of random vectors and $g : \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}$ be a differentiable function with derivative
$$\nabla g(y) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial g}{\partial y_1} \\ \frac{\partial g}{\partial y_2} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial g}{\partial y_k} \end{pmatrix}.$$
If
$$\sqrt{n}(Y_n - \mu) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \Sigma)$$
then
$$\sqrt{n}(g(Y_n) - g(\mu)) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \nabla_\mu^T \Sigma \nabla_\mu),$$
where $\nabla_\mu$ is $\nabla g(y)$ evaluated at $y = \mu$.

**Example**: Let $X_n = \begin{pmatrix} X_{n1} \\ X_{n2} \end{pmatrix}$ be iid bivariate random variables with mean $\mu = (\mu_1, \mu_2)^T$ and covariance $\Sigma$. Let $\hat{\mu}_{1,n} = n^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^n X_{i1}$, and $\hat{\mu}_{2,n} = n^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^n X_{i2}$. By multivariate CLT,
$$\sqrt{n} \begin{pmatrix} \hat{\mu}_{1,n} - \mu_1 \\ \hat{\mu}_{2,n} - \mu_2 \end{pmatrix} \rightsquigarrow N(0, \Sigma).$$
Let $Y_n = \hat{\mu}_{1,n}\hat{\mu}_{2,n}$, then $Y_n = g(\hat{\mu}_{1,n}, \hat{\mu}_{2,n})$ with $g(s_1, s_2) = s_1s_2$, and
$$\nabla g(s) = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial g}{\partial s_1} \\ \frac{\partial g}{\partial s_2} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} s_2 \\ s_1 \end{pmatrix}.$$
Thus
$$\nabla_\mu^T \Sigma \nabla_\mu = (\mu_2, \mu_1) \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{11} & \sigma_{12} \\ \sigma_{21} & \sigma_{22} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mu_2 \\ \mu_1 \end{pmatrix} = \mu_2^2 \sigma_{11} + 2\mu_1 \mu_2 \sigma_{12} + \mu_1^2 \sigma_{22}.$$
By Multivariate Delta Method,
$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\mu}_{1,n}\hat{\mu}_{2,n} - \mu_1\mu_2) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \mu_2^2 \sigma_{11} + 2\mu_1 \mu_2 \sigma_{12} + \mu_1^2 \sigma_{22}).$$

## 4 Stronger Modes of Convergence

**Almost-sure convergence.** We say that a sequence $X_n$ almost surely converges to $X$ if:
$$\mathbb{P}(\lim_{n \to \infty} X_n = X) = 1,$$
and denoted as
$$X_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} X.$$
That is, the two random variables become the same at the limit, i.e. they have the same values with probability 1. Note that the limit is inside the probability statement, which makes it stronger than convergence in probability. Convergence in probability roughly tells us that after a point, most of the values $X_n$ are quite close to $X$. We can still have "failures" where we obtain a sudden erratic value of $X_n$ but these are rare (and increasingly rare further down the sequence). Almost sure convergence says that after a point there are no more failures, and every random variable of the sequence $X_n$ is close to $X$. At least roughly, convergence in probability allows for a few erratic values, as long as they are not too likely, while almost-sure convergence does not.

**Example – Kolmogorov's Strong Law of Large Numbers**: This is a famous result of a.s. convergence. Let $Y_1, \dots, Y_n, \dots$ be iid random variables with finite expectation $\mathbb{E}(Y_1) = \mu$ (these are the same conditions as the WLLN). Then
$$\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i \xrightarrow{a.s.} \mu.$$

The strong LLN is substantially harder to prove than the WLLN.

**Convergence in $\ell_1$.** We say that a sequence $X_n$ converges to $X$ in $\ell_1$ if:
$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \mathbb{E}|X_n - X| = 0,$$
denoted as
$$X_n \xrightarrow{\ell_1} X.$$
Note that $\ell_1$ convergence (and $\ell_2$ convergence below) is a convergence of values of a sequence of random variables, as opposed to convergence in probability and a.s. convergence.

**Convergence in quadratic mean (convergence in $\ell_2$).** We say that a sequence $X_n$ converges to $X$ in quadratic mean if:
$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \mathbb{E}(X_n - X)^2 = 0,$$
denoted as
$$X_n \xrightarrow{\ell_2} X \quad \text{or} \quad X_n \xrightarrow{qm} X.$$
Usually, we are concerned with convergence in quadratic mean to a constant $c$. This means that $\mathbb{E}(Y_n - c)^2 \to 0$, where often, $c = \mathbb{E}(Y_n)$ as $n \to \infty$. For example, let $Y_1, \dots, Y_n, \dots$ be iid random variables with finite expectation $\mathbb{E}(Y_i) = \mu$ and variance $\text{Var}(Y_i) = \sigma^2$, and let $X_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$. Then $\mathbb{E}(X_n - \mu)^2 = \text{Var}(X_n) = \sigma^2/n \to 0$.

Convergence in q.m. is interesting because it is often easy to prove, and it implies convergence in probability, which could be a bit harder to prove.

### Sketch of proof of CLT.

Assume further that the mgf of $X_1$ exists.

$$M_{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n-\mu)}(t) = M_{\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i-\mu)/\sqrt{n}}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_{(X_i-\mu)/\sqrt{n}}(t)$$
$$= \prod_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E} e^{(X_i-\mu)t/\sqrt{n}} = \left[ \mathbb{E} e^{(X_1-\mu)t/\sqrt{n}} \right]^n$$
$$= \left[ \mathbb{E} \left( 1 + (X_1 - \mu)t/\sqrt{n} + \frac{t^2}{2n}(X_1 - \mu)^2 + \dots \right) \right]^n$$
$$= \left( 1 + 0 + \frac{t^2}{2n} \mathbb{E}(X_1 - \mu)^2 + \dots \right)^n$$
$$\approx (1 + t^2 \sigma^2 / (2n))^n \to e^{t^2 \sigma^2 / 2} .$$

### Sketch of proof of the Delta method.

Let $h(y) = \frac{g(y)-g(\mu)}{y-\mu} - g'(\mu)$. When $y \to \mu$, $h(y) \to 0$ by definition of derivative. Define $h(\mu) = 0$, then $h$ is continuous at $\mu$.

Now we have $g(y) - g(\mu) = (y - \mu)g'(\mu) + (y - \mu)h(y)$. So,

$$\sqrt{n} [g(Y_n) - g(\mu)] = \sqrt{n}(Y_n - \mu)g'(\mu) + \sqrt{n}(Y_n - \mu)h(Y_n) := A_{1,n} + A_{2,n} .$$

By assumption the first term $A_{1,n} \rightsquigarrow N(0, g'(\mu)^2 \sigma^2)$. Now the proof concludes if we show that $A_{2,n} \xrightarrow{P} 0$.

First the assumption $\sqrt{n}(Y_n - \mu) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \sigma^2)$ implies that $Y_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$. Thus the continuous mapping theorem implies that $h(Y_n) \xrightarrow{P} 0$. Then Slutsky's theorem implies that $A_{2,n} \rightsquigarrow 0$.
