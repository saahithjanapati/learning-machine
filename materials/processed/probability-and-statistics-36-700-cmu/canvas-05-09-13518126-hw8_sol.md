1. [More practice on parametric inference] Suppose that $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim \text{Geom}(p)$, i.e. the samples have a geometric distribution with parameter $p$. A geometric distribution is the distribution of the number of coin flips needed to see one head.

(a) Compute the MOME of $p$.
**Solution:** For this geometric law,
$$\mathbb{E}(X_i) = \frac{1}{p}.$$
The method of moments estimator equates $\bar{X}$ and $\mathbb{E}(X_i)$:
$$\bar{X} = \frac{1}{p} \Rightarrow \hat{p}_{MOM} = \frac{1}{\bar{X}}.$$

(b) Compute the MLE of $p$. In order to do this you need to write the likelihood function, simplify it as much as you can and find the (global) maximum of the likelihood or log likelihood; check that it is a maximum.
**Solution:** Let $S = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. The likelihood is
$$L(p) = \prod_{i=1}^n (1 - p)^{X_i-1} p = p^n (1 - p)^{\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-1)} = p^n (1 - p)^{S-n}.$$
The log-likelihood is
$$\ell(p) = n \log p + (S - n) \log(1 - p).$$
Differentiate:
$$\ell'(p) = \frac{n}{p} - \frac{S - n}{1 - p}.$$
Setting $\ell'(p) = 0$ gives
$$\frac{n}{p} = \frac{S - n}{1 - p} \Rightarrow n(1 - p) = p(S - n) \Rightarrow n = pS \Rightarrow \hat{p}_{MLE} = \frac{n}{S} = \frac{1}{\bar{X}}.$$
The second derivative
$$\ell''(p) = -\frac{n}{p^2} - \frac{S - n}{(1 - p)^2} < 0$$
for $0 < p < 1$, so this is indeed the global maximum.
For this geometric law,
$$\mathbb{E}(X_i) = \frac{1}{p}.$$
The method of moments estimator equates $\bar{X}$ and $\mathbb{E}(X_i)$:
$$\bar{X} = \frac{1}{p} \Rightarrow \hat{p}_{MOM} = \frac{1}{\bar{X}}.$$

(c) Assume that $p$ has a $\text{beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ prior. What is the prior mean of $p$?
**Solution:** Assume $p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, with density
$$\pi(p) \propto p^{\alpha-1}(1 - p)^{\beta-1}, \quad 0 < p < 1.$$
The prior mean is
$$\mathbb{E}_\pi(p) = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}.$$

(d) Compute the posterior distribution of $p$.
**Solution:** The posterior is proportional to likelihood $\times$ prior:
$$\pi(p \mid x_1, \dots, x_n) \propto p^n(1 - p)^{S-n} p^{\alpha-1}(1 - p)^{\beta-1} = p^{\alpha+n-1}(1 - p)^{\beta+S-n-1}.$$
Hence
$$p \mid x_1, \dots, x_n \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + n, \beta + S - n).$$

(e) Re-write the mean of the posterior distribution (what we call the posterior mean) as a convex combination of the prior mean and the MLE. What happens to the posterior mean if $\alpha, \beta$ are fixed and $n \to \infty$?
**Solution:** For a $\text{Beta}(a, b)$ distribution the mean is $a/(a + b)$, so
$$\mathbb{E}(p \mid x_1, \dots, x_n) = \frac{\alpha + n}{\alpha + n + \beta + S - n} = \frac{\alpha + n}{\alpha + \beta + S}.$$
Let
$$m_0 = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} \text{ (prior mean), } m_1 = \frac{n}{S} = \hat{p}_{MLE}.$$
Then
$$\frac{\alpha + n}{\alpha + \beta + S} = \frac{S}{\alpha + \beta + S} \cdot \frac{n}{S} + \frac{\alpha + \beta}{\alpha + \beta + S} \cdot \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta},$$
so the posterior mean is the convex combination
$$\mathbb{E}(p \mid x) = w \hat{p}_{MLE} + (1 - w) m_0, \quad w = \frac{S}{\alpha + \beta + S} \in (0, 1).$$
If $\alpha, \beta$ are fixed and $n \to \infty$, then $S \to \infty$ in probability, so $w \to 1$ and the posterior mean converges to the MLE (and hence to the true $p$ under regularity).

(f) Obtain the Bayes estimator under squared error loss. (Remark: with absolute error loss, we would need to compute the posterior median which typically does not have a closed-form solution)
**Solution:**
Under squared error loss, the Bayes estimator is the posterior mean, so
$$\hat{p}_{\text{Bayes}}(x_1, \dots, x_n) = \mathbb{E}(p \mid x_1, \dots, x_n) = \frac{\alpha + n}{\alpha + \beta + S}.$$

(g) Look at the definition of a *posterior interval* (Wasserman p. 178, top). What is the difference between a posterior interval and a (frequentist) confidence interval of $p$? Why would taking level sets of the posterior (that is, computing highest posterior density intervals/sets) generally not give you valid confidence intervals/sets?
**Solution:** $(1 - \alpha)$ *posterior interval* (or credible interval) for $p$ is a set $C(x)$ such that
$$\mathbb{P}(p \in C(x) \mid x) = 1 - \alpha,$$
where the probability is with respect to the posterior distribution of $p$ given the observed data $x$ (and the chosen prior).
A $(1 - \alpha)$ (frequentist) *confidence interval* is a random set $C(X)$ (depending on the sample $X$) satisfying
$$\mathbb{P}_p(p \in C(X)) \geq 1 - \alpha \text{ for all } p,$$
where the probability is with respect to the sampling distribution of $X$ when the true parameter value is $p$. No prior is involved.
Taking highest posterior density (HPD) sets (i.e. level sets of the posterior density with posterior probability $1 - \alpha$) guarantees the desired posterior probability, but does *not* in general guarantee the frequentist coverage property above. In particular, the coverage probability $\mathbb{P}_p(p \in C(X))$ can vary with $p$ and need not equal (or exceed) $1 - \alpha$ uniformly in $p$, so HPD sets are typically not valid confidence intervals except in special models and priors where Bayesian and frequentist intervals coincide.

2. [Comparing statistical procedures] Suppose that we are in a Bernoulli experiment, $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim \text{Ber}(p)$, and we believe that $p$ is close to 0. A natural way to incorporate this knowledge might be to use a $\text{Beta}(1, b)$ prior with a high-value of $b$. In the following, assume we always use the squared error loss.

(a) Compute the Bayes estimator. *Hint: The posterior density should be a well-known one, saving you some calculations of expected values.*
**Solution:** Recall that $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ density can be expressed (up to normalizing constant):
$$\pi(p) \propto p^{\alpha-1}(1 - p)^{\beta-1}.$$
The posterior density (up to normalizing constant) is
$$f(p \mid X_1, \dots, X_n) = \pi(p)L(p \mid X_1, \dots, X_n)$$
$$\propto \left[ (1 - p)^{b-1} \right] \left[ p^{n\bar{X}_n} (1 - p)^{n(1-\bar{X}_n)} \right]$$
$$\propto p^{n\bar{X}_n} (1 - p)^{n(1-\bar{X}_n)+b-1}.$$
This is $\text{Beta}(n\bar{X}_n + 1, n(1 - \bar{X}_n) + b)$. The posterior mean is
$$\hat{p} = \frac{n\bar{X}_n + 1}{n + 1 + b}.$$

(b) Compute the risk of the Bayes estimator $R(p)$. *Hint: With squared error loss, the risk reduces to the MSE.*
**Solution:** Assume the squared loss. Notice that $n\bar{X}_n \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$, and thus
$\mathbb{E}_X(\hat{p}) = \frac{np+1}{n+1+b}$, $\mathbb{V}_X(\hat{p}) = \frac{np(1-p)}{(n+1+b)^2}$.
$$R(p) = \mathbb{E}_X(p - \hat{p})^2 = \left[ (\mathbb{E}_X\hat{p} - p)^2 + \mathbb{V}_X(\hat{p}) \right]$$
$$= \left( \frac{np + 1}{n + 1 + b} - p \right)^2 + \frac{np(1 - p)}{(n + 1 + b)^2}$$
$$= \frac{1 + [n - 2(1 + b)]p + [(1 + b)^2 - n]p^2}{(n + 1 + b)^2}.$$

(c) Suppose that $p = 1$ (i.e. our prior was wrong). Compare the risk of the Bayes estimator above to the minimax estimator. What happens as $b$ gets large?
**Solution:** Note that
$$R(1) = \frac{b^2}{(n + 1 + b)^2},$$
and recall from Wasserman that the minimax risk is
$$R^* = \frac{n}{4(n + \sqrt{n})^2}.$$
(In Example 12.3 of Wasserman as well as later in this HW, the estimator $\hat{p}_2$ is shown to have risk $\frac{n}{4(n+\sqrt{n})^2}$. Then in Example 12.12, it is argued $\hat{p}_2$ is the Bayes estimator with prior $\text{Beta}(\sqrt{n}/4, \sqrt{n}/4)$. Hence by Theorem 12.11 is the minimax estimator).
For any fixed $b$, the risk of Bayes estimator $O(1/n^2)$ converges to zero faster than the minimax estimator $O(1/n)$ as $n \to \infty$. This means with a sufficiently large sample size, Bayes estimator may achieve lower risk than the minimax estimator. For $b > 1$, notice that $R(1) < R^*$ when $n > 2b^2 + b - 1 + 2b\sqrt{b^2 + b - 1}$. Larger $b$ will thus allow the upperbound on sample size to grow quadratically for which the minimax estimator dominates the Bayes estimator.

(d) Suppose that $b$ is fixed, compute the ratio of the maximum risk of the above Bayes estimator to the minimax risk. What happens as $n \to \infty$?
**Solution:** The maximum risk of the Bayes estimator can be found from
$$\frac{d}{dp}R(p) \bigg|_{p=p^*} = \frac{1}{(n + 1 + b)^2} \left[ n - 2(1 + b) + 2p^*[(1 + b)^2 - n] \right] = 0$$
$$\Rightarrow p^* = \frac{n - 2(1 + b)}{2(n - (1 + b)^2)}.$$
$$R(p^*) = \frac{1 + [n - 2(1 + b)]p^* + [(1 + b)^2 - n]p^{*2}}{(n + 1 + b)^2} = \frac{(n - 4b)n}{4(n - (1 + b)^2)(n + 1 + b)^2}.$$
The ratio of $R(p^*)$ to $R^*$ is
$$\frac{R(p^*)}{R^*} = \frac{(n - 4b)n}{4(n - (1 + b)^2)(n + 1 + b)^2} \frac{4(n + \sqrt{n})^2}{n}$$
$$= \frac{(n - 4b)(n + \sqrt{n})^2}{(n - (1 + b)^2)(n + 1 + b)^2} \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 1.$$

3. [Wald test] Suppose that $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$. Let $\lambda_0 > 0$. Find the size $\alpha$ Wald test for $H_0 : \lambda = \lambda_0$ versus $H_1 : \lambda \neq \lambda_0$. (*Hint: With “Wald test” we here mean: use the MLE of $\lambda$ as a test statistic and use large-sample theory.*)
**Solution:** Consider the MLE $\hat{\lambda} = \bar{X}$ to estimate $\lambda = (X_i)$. Since Fisher information of $\lambda$ is $1/\lambda$, we know
$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\lambda} - \lambda) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \lambda)$$
by the asymptotic normality of MLE. Under the null hypothesis, a version of the Wald test is
$$T = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\hat{\lambda} - \lambda_0)}{\sqrt{\lambda_0}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1).$$
Then, reject the null hypothesis when $|T| > z_{\alpha/2}$, which gives the size $\alpha$ test for the two-sided alternative.

4. [Understanding type II error and power] Let $X_1, \dots, X_n$ be i.i.d. $N(\theta, 1)$ random variables, where $n = 20$. Let
$$H_0 : \theta = \theta_0 = 0 \quad \text{versus} \quad H_1 : \theta = \theta_1 < \theta_0$$

(a) Determine the rejection region of the Wald test at the 5% significance level.
**Solution:** Recall in the Wald Test we use the test statistic
$$T = T(X_1, \dots, X_n) = \sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta_0) = \sqrt{n} \cdot \bar{X}_n$$
since $\theta_0 = 0$. Under the null, clearly $T \sim N(0, 1)$. A rejection region is a set $R$ such that $\max_{\theta_0 \in \Theta_0} \mathbb{P}_{\theta_0}(T \in R) \leq \alpha$. In our case, $\Theta_0 = \{\theta_0\} = \{0\}$, so it suffices to find a $c$ such that $\mathbb{P}_{\theta_0}(T \leq c) = \alpha$, i.e., our rejection region is $T \leq c$. This can be achieved with $c = -z_\alpha$ with $\alpha = 0.05$.

(b) Say that you obtain a sample of size $n = 20$ for which the value of the Wald test statistic is in the rejection region, so that **you reject the null hypothesis.**
i. **Suppose $H_0$ is true.** Then rejecting $H_0$ is the wrong decision. What is the probability of rejecting $H_0$ when $H_0$ is true?
**Solution:** We just showed under the null that $\mathbb{P}_{\theta_0}(T \leq -z_{0.05}) = 0.05$ and rejecting $H_0$ means $T \leq -z_{0.05}$. One can look up that $z_{0.05} = 1.645$ (e.g., qnorm(0.05) in R), and indeed $\mathbb{P}(Z \leq -1.645) \approx 0.05$ if $Z \sim N(0, 1)$.
ii. **Suppose $H_1$ is true.** Then rejecting $H_0$ is the correct decision.
A. What is the probability of rejecting $H_0$ if $H_1 : \theta = \theta_1 = -1$ is true?
**Solution:** Note that under $H_1$, $Z := \sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta_1) \sim N(0, 1)$, so let us manipulate the below probability into a statement about a $N(0, 1)$ RV. Write $\Phi$ to be the standard normal CDF. We compute
$$\mathbb{P}_\theta(T \leq -z_{0.05} \mid H_1 \text{ is true}) = \mathbb{P}_{\theta_1}(T \leq -z_{0.05})$$
$$= \mathbb{P}(\sqrt{n} \cdot \bar{X}_n \leq -z_{0.05})$$
$$= \mathbb{P}(\sqrt{n} \cdot \bar{X}_n - \sqrt{n}\theta_1 \leq -z_{0.05} - \sqrt{n}\theta_1)$$
$$= \mathbb{P}(\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \theta_1) \leq -z_{0.05} - \sqrt{n}\theta_1)$$
$$= \mathbb{P}(Z \leq -z_{0.05} + \sqrt{n})$$
$$= \Phi(-z_{0.05} + \sqrt{20})$$
$$\approx 0.9976.$$

Suppose we reject $H_0$ when
$$T = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \geq t.$$
Construct a test according to the “Neyman-Pearson paradigm”, i.e. find a threshold that would maximize the power of the test under the constraint that the test has level $\alpha$. Calculate the power function $\beta(\theta)$.
**Solution:** Notice that $\sqrt{n}(T - \theta) \sim N(0, 1)$ and
$$\sup_{\theta \leq 1} \mathbb{P}_\theta(T \geq t) = \sup_{\theta \leq 1} \mathbb{P}_\theta(\sqrt{n}(T - \theta) \geq \sqrt{n}(t - \theta))$$
$$= \sup_{\theta \leq 1} \mathbb{P}_\theta(Z \geq \sqrt{n}(t - \theta))$$
$$= \mathbb{P}_{\theta=1}(Z \geq \sqrt{n}(t - 1)) = \alpha.$$
Thus, the threshold $t$ that makes the size $\alpha$ test is $t = 1 + \frac{z_\alpha}{\sqrt{n}}$. Then, the power function becomes
$$\beta(\theta) = \mathbb{P}_\theta \left( T > 1 + \frac{z_\alpha}{\sqrt{n}} \right)$$
$$= \mathbb{P}_\theta \left( \sqrt{n}(T - \theta) > \sqrt{n}(1 + \frac{z_\alpha}{\sqrt{n}} - \theta) \right)$$
$$= \mathbb{P}_\theta (Z > \sqrt{n}(1 - \theta) + z_\alpha).$$

7. **[Deriving the distribution of p-values]** An important way to interpret p-values is by observing that under the null their distribution is uniform. Under the alternate we expect the p-values to concentrate closer to zero. In this question we will prove this fact:
“If the test statistic has a continuous distribution, then under $H_0 : \theta = \theta_0$, the p-value has a Uniform(0, 1) distribution.”

(a) Prove the above statement. Here are a few potentially useful hints/assumptions:
(1) The CDF of the uniform distribution is: $\mathbb{P}(U \leq u) = u$ for $u \in [0, 1]$ and it suffices to show that the p-values satisfy this. (2) You can assume that any CDFs that you need/use are invertible. (3) You can also assume that the rejection region is simple and nested, i.e. for any $\alpha$ you can assume that the rejection region takes the form:
$$R_\alpha = \{ \widehat{T} \geq t_\alpha \}$$
where $\widehat{T}$ is the observed test statistic and $t_\alpha$ is some threshold.
**Solution:** For the rejection region $R_\alpha = \{ \widehat{T} \geq t_\alpha \}$, we have
$$P_{\theta_0}(R_\alpha) = P_{\theta_0}(\widehat{T} \geq t_\alpha) = \alpha$$
and thus $t_\alpha$ is the upper $\alpha$-quantile of the $\widehat{T}$ distribution. In addition, the rejection region can be written as
$$R_\alpha = \{ X : \widehat{T}(X) \geq t_\alpha \} = \{ X : F(\widehat{T}(X)) \geq 1 - \alpha \} = \{ X : 1 - F(\widehat{T}(X)) \leq \alpha \}$$
where $F$ is the CDF of $\widehat{T}$. Note that the inf over all $\alpha$ where the last inequality holds is
$$\hat{p} = 1 - F(\widehat{T}(X))$$
by the definition of the p-value. Then,
$$P_{\theta_0}(\hat{p} \leq \alpha) = P_{\theta_0}(1 - F(\widehat{T}(X)) \leq \alpha) = P_{\theta_0}(F(\widehat{T}(X)) \geq 1 - \alpha) = \alpha.$$
Hence, $\hat{p}$ is uniformly distributed on (0, 1).

(b) Use what you have shown above to prove that if we reject $H_0$ when the p-value is less than or equal to $\alpha$, our Type 1 error is $\alpha$.
**Solution:** From the result of 1.(a), we have
$$P_{\theta_0}(X \in R_\alpha) = P_{\theta_0}(\hat{p} \leq \alpha) = \alpha.$$
So, the Type 1 error is $\alpha$.
