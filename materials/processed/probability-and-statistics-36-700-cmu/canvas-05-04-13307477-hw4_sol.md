1. Suppose that $X \sim F$ (some unspecified distribution) and that $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}[X] = \sigma^2$. Suppose, further, that $\sigma \leq 5$. We draw $n$ iid samples $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim F$.
    (a) How large should the sample size $n$ be so that with probability at least 0.90, the sample average satisfies $|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \leq 2$?
    (b) Now suppose that $|X| \leq 5$. How large should the sample size $n$ be so that with probability at least 0.95, the sample average satisfies $|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \leq 2$?

**Solution:**
(a): Observe that the variance of $\bar{X}_n$ is $\sigma^2/n$. Then by Chebyshev's inequality and that $\sigma \leq 5$,
$$\mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq 2) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{4n} \leq \frac{25}{4n}.$$
Rearranging,
$$\mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \leq 2) \geq \mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| < 2) \geq 1 - \frac{25}{4n}.$$
The right hand side is $\geq 0.9$ provided $n \geq 62.5$. Thus, a sample size of 63 will suffice.

(b): Since $-5 \leq X_i \leq 5$ for each $i$, we apply Hoeffding's inequality to conclude
$$\mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq 2) \leq 2 \exp \left( -\frac{2n \cdot 2^2}{10^2} \right) = 2 \exp \left( -\frac{2n}{25} \right).$$
Rearranging,
$$\mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \leq 2) \geq \mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| < 2) \geq 1 - 2 \exp \left( -\frac{2n}{25} \right).$$
The right hand side is $\geq 0.95$ provided $n \geq \frac{25}{2} \log 40 \approx 46.1$. Thus, a sample size of 47 will suffice. Note that if we simply repeated the application of Chebyshev from (a), i.e., solving $1 - \frac{25}{4n} \geq 0.95$, we would end up requiring $n \geq 125$. Thus, Hoeffding gives us a much tighter bound.

A trivial example that actually holds for all $t > 0$ is given by taking any constant random variable. If $X = c$, then $\mu = c$ and $\mathbb{P}(|X - \mu| > t) = \mathbb{P}(0 > t) = \mathbb{P}(\emptyset) = 0$, while $\sigma^2/t^2 = 0$ since $\text{Var}(X) = 0$.

If we instead want tightness with $\mathbb{P}(|X - \mu| \geq t)$ (which is possibly larger than $\mathbb{P}(|X - \mu| > t)$), then we must use a different construction for each choice of $t$. Let's assume for now that we can require $\mu = 0$ and $\sigma^2 = 1$ to simplify the problem. Our goal is to achieve $\mathbb{P}(|X| \geq t) = \frac{1}{t^2}$. This means we need $\mathbb{P}(|X| < t) = 1 - \frac{1}{t^2}$.

An extremely simple way to achieve this is to assume $X$ is either 0 with probability $1 - \frac{1}{t^2}$ or has absolute value $|t|$ with probability $1/t^2$. By placing equal mass at both $t$ and $-t$, we can ensure the mean is 0. Thus, define $X$ to take value $t$ with probability $\frac{1}{2t^2}$, value $-t$ with probability $\frac{1}{2t^2}$, and value 0 with the remaining probability $1 - \frac{1}{t^2}$. Then one can check
$$\mathbb{E}[X] = t \cdot \frac{1}{2t^2} + (-t) \cdot \frac{1}{2t^2} + 0 \cdot \left( 1 - \frac{1}{t^2} \right) = 0.$$
Since the mean is 0,
$$\text{Var}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] = \frac{1}{2t^2} \cdot t^2 + \frac{1}{2t^2} \cdot (-t)^2 + \left( 1 - \frac{1}{t^2} \right) \cdot 0^2 = 1.$$
Thus, indeed Chebyshev's inequality is tight.

3. **CLT:** A Binomial RV is the sum of independent Bernoullis and is thus well approximated by a Gaussian by the central limit theorem. We will use this to perform a rudimentary hypothesis test.
We believe a coin is fair and toss it 100 times. It lands heads up 60 times. Use moment matching (or the CLT) to approximate the probability $\mathbb{P}(X \geq 60)$ assuming the coin is fair in terms of the standard Gaussian CDF.
Use R (or any package you prefer) to give a numerical value. Roughly, our intuition is that if this value is small then we should be "suspicious" of our initial hypothesis that the coin is fair. Are you suspicious?
(Include your code in your submission.)

**Solution:**
Since the coin is fair, the expected value of $X$ is $\mathbb{E}(X) = np = 50$. The variance of $X$ is $V(X) = np(1 - p)$, which is equivalent to 25 in this example. Moment matching or the CLT gives an approximation as
$$\mathbb{P}(X \geq 60) = \mathbb{P} \left( \frac{X - 50}{\sqrt{25}} \geq \frac{60 - 50}{\sqrt{25}} \right) \approx \mathbb{P}(Z \geq 2)$$
where $Z$ has a standard normal distribution. Numerically this is given by 0.0228. Alternatively, we could directly compute the probability a binomial random variable with parameters $n = 100$ and $p = 0.5$ returns 60 ones. This yields 0.0176.
The returned value is pretty small. So, we suspect that the coin is unfair.

4. **Confidence Intervals:** Suppose that $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are repeated measurements of a quantity $\mu$, and that $\mathbb{E}[X_i] = \mu$, and $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2$. Further, let us suppose that $X_i \in [0, 1]$. Let $\bar{X}_n$ denote the average of the measurements.
    (a) Use the fact that $X_i \in [0, 1]$ to give some bounds on $\mu$ and $\sigma$.
    (b) Suppose that we take 16 measurements and that $\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{12}$. Use the CLT to approximate the probability that $\bar{X}_n$ deviates from $\mu$ by more than 0.5.
    (c) Use Chebyshev's inequality to give an upper bound on the same quantity.
    (d) Repeat the above calculation but now use Mill's and Hoeffding's inequalities.
    (e) Now use R (or any package you prefer) to estimate this probability in the following way. Suppose that each $X_i$ is $U[0, 1]$. The mean is 0.5 and the variance is exactly 1/12. Draw 16 measurements and track if the sample mean is within 0.5 of the true mean. Repeat this 1000 times to get an accurate estimate.
    Compare the answer to what you obtained analytically. Particularly, order the intervals by their coverage.
    (Include your code in your submission)

**Solution:**
(a): Since $0 \leq X_i \leq 1$ and expectations preserve inequalities, we have $0 \leq \mu \leq 1$. Next, notice also that $0 \leq X_i^2 \leq 1$, so $0 \leq \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] \leq 1$. But also $0 \leq \mu^2 \leq 1$. Therefore $V(X_i) = \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] - \mu^2 \leq 1$.

(b): Let $Z$ denote a standard normal RV. Then
$$\mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \frac{1}{2}) = \mathbb{P} \left( \frac{\sqrt{n}|\bar{X}_n - \mu|}{\sigma} \geq \frac{\sqrt{n}}{2\sigma} \right) = \mathbb{P} \left( \frac{|\bar{X}_n - \mu|}{\sigma} \geq \sqrt{3n} \right) \approx \mathbb{P}(|Z| \geq \sqrt{3n}).$$
With $n = 16$, this becomes $\mathbb{P}(|Z| \geq \sqrt{48}) \approx 4.26 \cdot 10^{-12}$.

(c): Note that the variance of $\bar{X}_n$ is $\sigma^2/n$. Thus,
$$\mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \frac{1}{2}) \leq \frac{4\sigma^2}{n} = \frac{1}{3n}.$$
With $n = 16$, the bound is $1/48 \approx 0.0208$.

(d): In (b) we approximated the probability with $\mathbb{P}(Z \geq \sqrt{3n})$ where $Z$ is a standard normal. Using Mill's inequality with $n = 16$, this is upper bounded by
$$\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \frac{\exp(-3n/2)}{\sqrt{3n}} \approx 4.348 \cdot 10^{-12}.$$
Using Hoeffding, we have
$$\mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \frac{1}{2}) \leq 2 \exp \left( -2 \cdot \frac{n}{2^2 \cdot (1 - 0)^2} \right) = 2 \exp(-n/2).$$
With $n = 16$ this is $\approx 6.71 \cdot 10^{-4}$.

(e): Of our 1000 simulations, there are 0 instances in which the sample mean is NOT within 0.5 of the true mean (which is to be completely expected, since the empirical mean of $U[0, 1]$ RVs will always be in $[0, 1]$ and within 0.5 of the number 0.5.)
Thus, in order of worsening coverage, the best bounds were obtained by simulations (which achieved the true probability), the CLT, Mill's Inequality, Hoeffding, then Chebyshev.

6. **Convergence I:** In lecture we will show that convergence in quadratic mean implies convergence in probability. Suppose we have a sequence $X_1, \dots, X_n$. Fix $a > 0$. Show that if $\mathbb{E}|X_n - X|^a \to 0$ then $X_n$ converges to $X$ in probability.
**Solution:** For fixed $a > 0$, We have that for any $\epsilon > 0$, via Markov's inequality,
$$\mathbb{P}(|X_n - X| \geq \epsilon) = \mathbb{P}(|X_n - X|^a \geq \epsilon^a) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}|X_n - X|^a}{\epsilon^a} \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 0.$$

7. **Convergence II:** Show that the following RVs converge in probability to 1.
    (a) $Y_n = 1 + nX_n$ where $X_n \sim \text{Ber}(1/n)$.
**Solution:** Note that
$$nX_n = \begin{cases} n & \text{w.p. } 1/n, \\ 0 & \text{w.p. } 1 - 1/n. \end{cases}$$
For $\epsilon > 0$,
$$\mathbb{P}(|Y_n - 1| > \epsilon) = \mathbb{P}(|nX_n| > \epsilon) = 1/n \mathbb{I}(n > \epsilon) \leq 1/n \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} 0.$$
Note that this works because when $\epsilon > 0$, it rules out $|nX_n| = 0$.

8. **Convergence III:** (Order statistic) Wasserman Exercise 5.13 on page 84
**Solution:** Let $F$ be the CDF of $Z$. Let us find the CDF $F_n$ of $X_n$. For $x > 0$, we have
$$F_n(x) = \mathbb{P}(X_n \leq x)$$
$$= \mathbb{P}(n \min\{Z_1, \dots, Z_n\} \leq x)$$
$$= \mathbb{P} \left( \min\{Z_1, \dots, Z_n\} \leq \frac{x}{n} \right)$$
$$= 1 - \mathbb{P} \left( \min\{Z_1, \dots, Z_n\} > \frac{x}{n} \right)$$
$$= 1 - \mathbb{P} \left( Z_1 > \frac{x}{n}, \dots, Z_n > \frac{x}{n} \right)$$
$$= 1 - \prod_{i=1}^n \mathbb{P} \left( Z_i > \frac{x}{n} \right)$$
$$= 1 - \prod_{i=1}^n \left( 1 - F \left( \frac{x}{n} \right) \right)$$
$$= 1 - \left( 1 - F \left( \frac{x}{n} \right) \right)^n$$
$$= 1 - \exp \{n \log (1 - F(x/n))\}$$
$$= 1 - \exp \left\{ \frac{\log (1 - F(x/n))}{1/n} \right\}$$
$$\to 1 - e^{-x\lambda} \quad (n \to \infty)$$
In the last step, we noted that $\log(1 - F(x/n)) \to \log(1 - F(0)) = 0$ since $F(0) = \mathbb{P}(Z \leq 0) = 0$, while $1/n \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. Thus, we have a 0/0 scenario, so we apply L'Hopital's rule. Using the chain rule in the numerator (noting we are differentiating in $n$, not $x$),
$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\log (1 - F(x/n))}{1/n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{(xn^{-2})f(x/n)/(1 - F(x/n))}{-n^{-2}}$$
$$= - \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{xf(x/n)}{1 - F(x/n)} = -x\lambda.$$
Hence the exponential of this tends to $\exp(-x\lambda)$ as claimed. Differentiating, the density of $X_n$ is thus $\lambda e^{-x\lambda}$.
