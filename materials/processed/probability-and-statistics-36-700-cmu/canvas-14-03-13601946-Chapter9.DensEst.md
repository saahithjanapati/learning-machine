## 1 Models in Statistics: Big Picture

Throughout our discussions so far, and in a lot of the remainder of the course we often hypothesize a model and then study estimation and inference. When you analyze a sufficiently complex real dataset a model will inevitably be a simplification of the “true” generative process.

Statistics in general has a complicated relationship with models: particularly, eminent statisticians like George Box and David Cox have said things like: “All models are wrong but some are useful”, and “The very word model implies simplification and idealization.”

This of course leads to several questions: why are models useful at all? What happens when the model is mis-specified? Do we really need strong modeling assumptions in statistics?

Here are a few answers:

1. **Why are models useful?**
    * Models even when mis-specified can be a useful lens through which we can view data. As a very simple example: even if the data I have does not have a Gaussian distribution, fitting a Gaussian to the data gives me a sense of the mean and variance.
    More generally, even when the model is wrong, the estimated parameters or model are much easier to understand/summarize than the raw data.
    * Models often force us to think of generative mechanisms (which can be useful in itself): i.e. (at least approximately) how did the data come about?
    Once we have a model, we are naturally led to formalize quantities of interest (i.e. parameters) and think about estimating them (i.e. solving the inverse problem).

By this I mean, it might be quite difficult to come up with an algorithm to solve a certain (abstract) problem: I would like to understand a large collection of documents.

A powerful way to try to solve a problem of this form is to hypothesize a generative model: in this context this is called a “topic model”. We suppose that the collection is about a small number of topics, and that each topic has a different distribution over words: i.e. a topic like sports will be more likely to involve words like “bat”, “ball”, ”soccer” etc. So maybe we could write down a very simple generative model: we first sample a topic for a document (from say a multinomial) and then for each we sample a bunch of words (the text of the document) also from a multinomial.

From a statistical point of view, this is a simple mixture model, we can write down the likelihood of the text we observed as a function of the multinomial parameters, and then maximize the likelihood to figure out the topic distributions.

At the end of the day we will group the documents into topics, and further say for this collection here are the most likely words (useful for identifying the topics). More abstractly we hypothesized a generative model, and then used standard statistical tools to devise an algorithm for our problem.

* Models can help us think rigorously about inference: roughly, you observe some pattern in the data (or an extreme value of some parameter) and you would like to know if this pattern is really surprising or just due to the usual stochastic fluctuations. Hypothesis testing is the formal framework for stating and answering questions of inference.

2. **What happens when the model is mis-specified?** For example, one might wonder if the maximum likelihood, which is tailored to a particular model, is complete garbage if the model is wrong. Furthermore, since a model is always wrong, does the maximum likelihood make any sense?

This is a somewhat subtle question: most often the answer is yes, maximum likelihood is still a good idea even when you do not fully believe the model.

Roughly, the intuition you should have is that maximum likelihood will often estimate the projection of the true generative mechanism onto your model. This is some strong sense the best approximation of the true generative mechanism, i.e. maximum likelihood is just trying to fit the *best approximation* of the true model.

3. **Do we really need strong modeling assumptions?** More specifically, suppose we are doing regression. Do we really need to make the assumption that the relationship between the predictors $X$ and the response variable $Y$ is linear? This is a pretty strong modeling assumption.

## 2 1D Density Estimation

This material is taken from Chapter 20 of Wasserman’s book.

The task in density estimation is that we are given an i.i.d. sample
$$X_1, \dots, X_n \sim f$$
where $f$ is some (smooth) density function, and we would like to estimate $f$. Our earlier lectures on point estimation essentially addressed the parametric analogue of this problem. More specifically, we have seen how to estimate $\theta$ under the hypothesis that
$$X_1, \dots, X_n \sim f_\theta,$$
and here we study the non-parametric analogue of this problem. We will focus mostly on the one-dimensional case.

### 2.1 Risk Estimation

We will evaluate the quality of an estimator $\hat{f}_n$ with the risk, or integrated mean squared error, $R = \mathbb{E}(L)$ where
$$L = \int (\hat{f}(x) - f(x))^2 dx$$
is the integrated squared error loss function. The estimators will depend on some smoothing parameter $h$ and we will choose $h$ to minimize an estimate of the risk. The usual method for estimating risk is **leave-one-out cross-validation**. The details are different for density estimation (Wasserman 10.2-10.3) than for regression (Wasserman 10.4). In the regression case, the cross-validation score is defined as $\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \hat{r}_{(-i)}(X_i))^2$ but in density estimation, there is no response variable $Y$. Instead, we proceed as follows:

The loss function, which we now write as a function of $h$, (since $\hat{f}$ will depend on some smoothing parameter $h$) is
$$L(h) = \int (\hat{f}(x) - f(x))^2 dx$$
$$= \int \hat{f}^2(x) dx - 2 \int \hat{f}(x)f(x)dx + \int f^2(x) dx.$$

The last term does not depend on $h$ so minimizing the loss is equivalent to minimizing the expected value of
$$J(h) = \int \hat{f}^2(x) dx - 2 \int \hat{f}(x)f(x)dx. \quad (1)$$

We shall refer to $\mathbb{E}(J(h))$ as the risk, although it differs from the true risk by the constant term $\int f^2(x) dx$.

To calculate $J(h)$, we can simply evaluate the first term once we have $\hat{f}$ but the second term is tricky because $f$ is unknown. However, using the law of the lazy statistician, we can rewrite the second term as $\mathbb{E}(\hat{f}(X))$, where $X \sim f$. Since we do not know $f$, we cannot calculate that expectation. However, we happen to have a sample $X_1, \dots, X_n$, and we could in principle approximate the expected value with the sample average $\frac{1}{n} \sum_i \hat{f}(X_i)$. If we do it that way, we would have used the sample twice: first to estimate $f$, then to estimate the risk. Such an approach would lead to an estimate that is *negatively biased* (can be proved); that is, we would arrive at an overly optimistic estimate of the true risk. Hence, we need to decouple the two estimations. One solution is to use cross-validation.

> **2.1 Definition.** The **cross-validation estimator of risk** is
> $$\hat{J}(h) = \int (\hat{f}(x))^2 dx - \frac{2}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \hat{f}_{(-i)}(X_i) \quad (2)$$
> where $\hat{f}_{(-i)}$ is the density estimator obtained after removing the $i^{th}$ observation. We refer to $\hat{J}(h)$ as the cross-validation score or estimated risk.

### 2.2 Histograms

Suppose that $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim f$, where $f$ is a density supported on $[0, 1]$ (this restriction is not critical). A natural estimator in this context is to bin the samples in a certain way, i.e., we define the bins $B_1, \dots, B_m$ to be:
$$B_1 = \left[0, \frac{1}{m}\right), B_2 = \left[\frac{1}{m}, \frac{2}{m}\right), \dots, B_m = \left[\frac{m - 1}{m}, 1\right].$$

Let $\nu_1, \dots, \nu_m$ be the number of samples in each bin. We can then estimate the *probability mass* of each bin as:
$$\hat{p}_1 = \frac{\nu_1}{n},$$
$$\vdots$$
$$\hat{p}_m = \frac{\nu_m}{n}.$$

To estimate the density (our original goal) we use for an $x$ that lies in the $i^{th}$ bin:
$$\hat{f}(x) = \frac{m\nu_i}{n}.$$

We can also write this in terms of the *bin-width* $h = \frac{1}{m}$ as:
$$\hat{f}(x) = \frac{\hat{p}_i}{h}.$$

We can also express this as:
$$\hat{f}(x) = \sum_{i=1}^m \frac{\hat{p}_i}{h} \mathbb{I}(x \in B_i).$$

As a sort of basic sanity check we can see that if $x \in B_i$ then
$$\mathbb{E}[\hat{f}(x)] = \frac{\mathbb{E}[\hat{p}_i]}{h} = \frac{p_i}{h} = \frac{\int_{x \in B_i} f(x)dx}{h} \approx \frac{hf(x)}{h} = f(x).$$

The $\approx$ comes from assuming that the function $f$ does not change much over the bin if $h$ is small. You might already be able to smell a bias-variance tradeoff.

Figure 1 shows three different histograms based on $n = 1,266$ data points from an astronomical sky survey. Each data point represents a “redshift,” roughly speaking, the distance from us to a galaxy. Choosing the right number of bins involves finding a good tradeoff between bias and variance. The top left histogram has too many bins resulting in undersmoothing and too much bias. The bottom left histogram has too few bins resulting in oversmoothing. The top right histogram is based on 308 bins (chosen by cross-validation). The histogram reveals the presence of clusters of galaxies.

*Note: There is short-cut formula to calculate the CV risk estimate (we could prove it, but there is no time and it is just a calculation):*
$$\hat{J}(h) = \frac{2}{h(n - 1)} - \frac{n + 1}{h(n - 1)} \sum_{j=1}^m \hat{p}_j^2.$$

**Efficiency of the histogram** We have seen how to use CV to estimate the risk so we can choose a bandwidth. But what is the behavior of the risk as a function of $n$? To see this we once again decompose the risk into
$$R(\hat{f}, f) = \int_x b^2(x)dx + \int_x v(x)dx,$$
where $b(x)$ and $v(x)$ are the bias and variance of our estimator at $x$. We will assume that the unknown density is $L$-Lipschitz continuous. Note that unlike in the non-parametric regression case (to be discussed in the next lecture), we need very few assumptions for density estimation.

One can show that
$$R(\hat{f}, f) \leq (Lh)^2 + \left[ \frac{1}{nh} + \frac{L}{n} \right].$$
(The proofs for density estimation with histograms and regression with regressograms are very similar)

The optimal bandwidth is
$$h = \left( \frac{1}{2nL^2} \right)^{1/3},$$
so that the min risk is:
$$R(\hat{f}, f) \leq 4 \left( \frac{L}{n} \right)^{2/3} + \frac{L}{n} \leq 5 \left( \frac{L}{n} \right)^{2/3} = O(n^{-2/3}),$$
for sufficiently large $n$ (since $L$ is a constant). We observe that the rate again is slower than the usual $1/n$ rate in parametric problems, which means that the histogram makes much less efficient use of the data than a parametric method. Again, you trade statistical efficiency and faster convergence rates for flexibility: there is no free lunch.

### 2.3 Kernel Density Estimates

Histograms are not smooth. Now we discuss kernel density estimators which are smoother and which converge to the true density faster.

> **2.2 Definition.** Given a kernel $K$ and a positive number $h$, called the **bandwidth**, the **kernel density estimator** is defined to be
> $$\hat{f}(x) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{h} K \left( \frac{x - X_i}{h} \right). \quad (3)$$

This amounts to placing a smoothed out lump of mass of size $1/n$ over each data point $X_i$; see Figure 2.

As with kernel regression, the choice of kernel $K$ is not crucial, but the choice of bandwidth $h$ is important. Figure 3 shows density estimates with several different bandwidths. We see how sensitive the estimate $\hat{f}$ is to the choice of $h$. Small bandwidths give very rough estimates while larger bandwidths give smoother estimates. The bandwidth for the density estimator in the upper right panel of Figure 3 is based on cross-validation. In this case it worked well but of course there are lots of examples where there are problems. Do not assume that, if the estimator $\hat{f}$ is wiggly, then cross-validation has let you down. **The eye is not a good judge of risk.**

**Efficiency of the KDE** Instead of estimating the risk, let’s look at how it behaves as a function of $n$. We can prove that:
$$R \approx \frac{1}{4} \sigma_K^4 h^4 \int (f''(x))^2 dx + \frac{\int K^2(x)dx}{nh} \quad (4)$$
where $\sigma_K^2 = \int x^2 K(x)dx$. You can check (not expected – only if you would like to) http://www.stat.cmu.edu/~larry/=sml/densityestimation.pdf for a proof and many more properties about KDEs.

If we differentiate (4) with respect to $h$ and set it equal to 0, we see that the asymptotically optimal bandwidth is
$$h_* = \left( \frac{c_2}{c_1^2 A(f) n} \right)^{1/5} \quad (5)$$
where $c_1 = \int x^2 K(x)dx$, $c_2 = \int K(x)^2 dx$ and $A(f) = \int (f''(x))^2 dx$. This is informative because it tells us that the best bandwidth decreases at rate $n^{-1/5}$. Plugging $h_*$ into (4), we see that if the optimal bandwidth is used then $R = O(n^{-4/5})$. As we saw, histograms converge at rate $O(n^{-2/3})$ showing that kernel estimators are superior in rate to histograms.

## 3 Kernel Density Estimates in Higher Dimension

Suppose now that the data are $d$-dimensional so that $X_i = (X_{i1}, \dots, X_{id})$. The kernel estimator can easily be generalized to $d$ dimensions. Most often, we use the product kernel
$$\hat{f}(x) = \frac{1}{nh_1 \dots h_d} \sum_{i=1}^n \left\{ \prod_{j=1}^d K \left( \frac{x_j - X_{ij}}{h_j} \right) \right\}, \quad (6)$$

Let us again reflect on the curse of dimensionality. Suppose that the density we want to estimate is Lipschitz (bounded first derivatives). When using a $d$-dimensional histogram, how large of a sample size do we need in $d$ dimensions so as to not exceed a squared error of 0.1? Similar to the nonparametric regression case, the risk is in $O(n^{-2/(2+d)})$, so we solve $n^{-2/(2+d)} \le 0.1$ for $n$, i.e., we need:

$$\log_{10} n \ge \frac{2 + d}{2},$$

or in other words:

$$n \ge 10^{1+d/2}.$$
