1. Wasserman Problem 5.1 on page 82

**Solution:** (a) Let us write
$$S_n^2 = \frac{\sum_i (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2}{n - 1} = \frac{\sum_i X_i^2 - n\bar{X}_n^2}{n - 1}.$$
Now, $\mathbb{E}[\sum_i X_i^2] = n\mathbb{E}(X_1^2)$. To compute $\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2]$ we will make use of the following fact. If $a_1, \dots, a_n$ are real numbers then $(\sum_i a_i)^2 = \sum_i a_i^2 + \sum_{i \neq j} a_i a_j$ and the second sum has $n^2 - n = n(n - 1)$ terms. So,
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2] &= \mathbb{E} \left[ \left( \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \right)^2 \right] = \frac{1}{n^2} \mathbb{E} \left[ \left( \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \right)^2 \right] = \frac{1}{n^2} \mathbb{E} \left[ \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 + \sum_{i \neq j} X_i X_j \right] \\ &= \frac{1}{n^2} \left( \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] + \sum_{i \neq j} \mathbb{E}[X_i X_j] \right) = \frac{1}{n^2} \left( \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] + \sum_{i \neq j} \mathbb{E}[X_i] \mathbb{E}[X_j] \right) \\ &= \frac{1}{n^2} \left( \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] + \sum_{i \neq j} \mu^2 \right) = \frac{1}{n^2} (n\mathbb{E}[X_1^2] + n(n - 1)\mu^2) \\ &= \frac{\mathbb{E}[X_1^2]}{n} + \frac{(n - 1)\mu^2}{n}. \end{aligned}$$
Thus,
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[S_n^2] &= \frac{1}{n - 1} \cdot \mathbb{E} \left[ \sum_i X_i^2 \right] - \frac{n}{n - 1} \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2] \\ &= \frac{n}{n - 1} \mathbb{E}[X_1^2] - \frac{n}{n - 1} \cdot \left( \frac{\mathbb{E}[X_1^2]}{n} + \frac{(n - 1)\mu^2}{n} \right) \\ &= \mathbb{E}[X_1^2] - \mu^2 = \sigma^2. \end{aligned}$$

(b) We can write
$$S_n^2 = \left( \frac{n}{n - 1} \right) \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 - \left( \frac{n}{n - 1} \right) \bar{X}_n^2.$$
By the Weak Law of Large Numbers (WLLN),
$$\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 \xrightarrow{p} \mathbb{E}(X_1^2).$$

2. Wasserman Problem 5.9 on page 83

**Solution:** Observe for a fixed $\epsilon > 0$ that
$$\mathbb{P}(|X_n - X| > \epsilon) = \frac{1}{n} \to 0$$
and hence $X_n \xrightarrow{p} X$. This implies $X_n$ converges in distribution to $X$ (Theorem 5.4(b) in Wasserman).

Next, observe that $X_n - X = 0$ when $X_n = X$, otherwise $|X_n - X| \ge e^n - 1$ (with probability $1/n$). Hence,
$$\mathbb{E}[(X_n - X)^2] \ge (e^n - 1)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{n} \to \infty.$$
Thus $\mathbb{E}[(X - X_n)^2]$ does not converge to 0.

3. Wasserman Problem 5.14 on page 84

**Solution:** Note that $\mathbb{E}[X_1] = 1/2$ and $\mathbb{V}[X_1] = 1/12$. By the Central Limit Theorem, $\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - 1/2)}{(1/\sqrt{12})} = \sqrt{12n}(\bar{X}_n - 1/2) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$. By setting $g(x) = x^2$ and let us apply the Delta Method (Theorem 5.13 in Wasserman). Note that $g'(x) = 2x$. Thus, $\frac{\sqrt{12n}(\bar{X}_n^2 - g(1/2))}{2(1/2)} = \sqrt{12n}(\bar{X}_n^2 - 1/4) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, 1)$. Or rewritten another way,
$$\bar{X}_n^2 \approx N\left( \frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{12n} \right).$$

5. Wasserman Problem 5.15 on page 84

**Solution:** We first apply the multivariate central limit theorem (Theorem 5.12 of Wasserman) and obtain the limiting distribution of the random vector $\bar{X} = \begin{pmatrix} \bar{X}_1 \\ \bar{X}_2 \end{pmatrix}$, where $\bar{X}_1$ is the mean of the $n$ iid random variables $X_{11}, X_{12}, \dots, X_{1n}$ and $\bar{X}_2$ is the mean of the $n$ iid random variables $X_{21}, X_{22}, \dots, X_{2n}$. Then we have
$$\sqrt{n}(\bar{X} - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \Sigma),$$
where $N(0, \Sigma)$ is a bivariate Gaussian random variable with mean vector $(0, 0)$ and a $2 \times 2$ covariance matrix $\Sigma$.

Now define the function $g: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ by $g(x, y) = x/y$. We are asked to find the limiting distribution of
$$g(\bar{X}) = g \left( \begin{pmatrix} \bar{X}_1 \\ \bar{X}_2 \end{pmatrix} \right) = \bar{X}_1 / \bar{X}_2.$$

We then apply the Multivariate Delta Method (Theorem 5.15 of Wasserman). We compute the gradient of $g$ evaluated at $\mu = (\mu_1, \mu_2)$:
$$\nabla_\mu = \nabla g(x, y) \Big|_{(x,y)=\mu} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/y \\ -x/y^2 \end{pmatrix} \Big|_{(x,y)=\mu} = \begin{pmatrix} \mu_2^{-1} \\ -\mu_1 \mu_2^{-2} \end{pmatrix}.$$
Then
$$\sqrt{n}(Y_n - \mu_1/\mu_2) = \sqrt{n}(g(\bar{X}) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \nabla_\mu^T \Sigma \nabla_\mu).$$
Let us expand $\nabla_\mu^T \Sigma \nabla_\mu$. Writing $\sigma_{ij}$ to be the $(i, j)$ entry of $\Sigma$ and noting $\sigma_{12} = \sigma_{21}$, we compute
$$\begin{aligned} \nabla_\mu^T \Sigma \nabla_\mu &= \begin{pmatrix} \mu_2^{-1} & -\mu_1 \mu_2^{-2} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{11} & \sigma_{12} \\ \sigma_{21} & \sigma_{22} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \mu_2^{-1} \\ -\mu_1 \mu_2^{-2} \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \begin{pmatrix} \mu_2^{-1} & -\mu_1 \mu_2^{-2} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_{11} \mu_2^{-1} - \sigma_{12} \mu_1 \mu_2^{-2} \\ \sigma_{21} \mu_2^{-1} - \sigma_{22} \mu_1 \mu_2^{-2} \end{pmatrix} \\ &= \sigma_{11} \mu_2^{-2} - \sigma_{12} \mu_1 \mu_2^{-3} - \sigma_{21} \mu_1 \mu_2^{-3} + \sigma_{22} \mu_1^2 \mu_2^{-4} \\ &= \sigma_{11} \mu_2^{-2} - 2\sigma_{12} \mu_1 \mu_2^{-3} + \sigma_{22} \mu_1^2 \mu_2^{-4} \end{aligned}$$
If we write the random variable as $X = \begin{pmatrix} X_1 \\ X_2 \end{pmatrix}$ (dropping the second index), we can write this as
$$\nabla_\mu^T \Sigma \nabla_\mu = \frac{\mathbb{V}(X_1)}{(\mathbb{E}[X_2])^2} - 2\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2) \left( \frac{\mathbb{E}[X_1]}{(\mathbb{E}[X_2])^3} \right) + \frac{\mathbb{V}(X_2)(\mathbb{E}[X_1])^2}{(\mathbb{E}[X_2])^4}.$$

6. Wasserman Problem 6.2 on page 95

**Solution:** Note that the maximum of $n$ numbers is less than $\hat{\theta}$ iff each of the $n$ numbers are less than $\hat{\theta}$. The cdf $G$ of $\hat{\theta}$ is
$$\begin{aligned} G(\hat{\theta}) &= \mathbb{P}(\hat{\Theta} \le \hat{\theta}) = \mathbb{P}(\max\{X_1, \dots, X_n\} \le \hat{\theta}) \\ &= \mathbb{P}(X_1 \le \hat{\theta}, X_2 \le \hat{\theta}, \dots, X_n \le \hat{\theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^n \mathbb{P}(X_i \le \hat{\theta}) \\ &= F_\theta(\hat{\theta})^n = \left( \frac{\hat{\theta}}{\theta} \right)^n. \end{aligned}$$
Differentiating, the pdf $g$ is
$$g(\hat{\theta}) = \frac{n}{\theta} \cdot \left( \frac{\hat{\theta}}{\theta} \right)^{n-1}.$$
Thus,
$$\mathbb{E}_\theta(\hat{\theta}) = \int_0^\theta \hat{\theta} g(\hat{\theta}) d\hat{\theta} = \frac{n}{\theta^n} \int_0^\theta (\hat{\theta})^n d\hat{\theta} = \frac{n\theta}{n + 1}$$

7. Wasserman Problem 6.3 on page 96

**Solution:** Recall that $\mathbb{E}(X_i) = \theta/2, \mathbb{V}(X_i) = \theta^2/12$. So
$$\mathbb{E}_\theta(2\bar{X}) = 2\mathbb{E}_\theta(\bar{X}) = 2 \cdot \frac{\theta}{2} = \theta$$
and hence $\text{bias} = 0$. Now
$$\mathbb{V}_\theta(2\bar{X}) = 4\mathbb{V}_\theta(\bar{X}) = \frac{4\sigma^2}{n} = \frac{4\theta^2}{12n} = \frac{\theta^2}{3n}.$$
Since this estimator is unbiased,
$$\text{mse} = \mathbb{V}_\theta(\hat{\theta}) = \frac{\theta^2}{3n}.$$
