## 1 Motivation and Intuition
This chapter covers some basic probability inequalities: how far a random variable can deviate from its expectation. See Chapter 4 of Wasserman.

Suppose we have independent and identically distributed (iid) random variables $X_1, ..., X_n$ with $\mathbb{E}(X_1) = \mu$ and $\mathbb{V}(X_1) = \sigma^2$, and we want to estimate the unknown quantity $\mu$. It is common sense to consider the sample mean $\bar{X}_n = (X_1 + ... + X_n)/n$. But why is $\bar{X}_n$ better than other candidates, such as $X_1$, as an estimate of $\mu$? Both $\bar{X}_n$ and $X_1$ have the correct expectation: $\mathbb{E}\bar{X}_n = \mathbb{E}X_1 = \mu$. But $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \sigma^2/n$ while $\text{Var}(X_1) = \sigma^2$.

It is comforting to know that $\bar{X}_n$ has much smaller variance than $X_1$, but we could get unlucky and have the mean of a bunch of random variables be quite far from their expectation; consider this example:

**Example.** Suppose I toss a fair coin $n$ times, and denote the outcome of the $i^{th}$ toss $X_i$ where $X_i = -1$ if tails and $X_i = +1$ if heads. Note that $\mu \equiv \mathbb{E}[X_i] = 0$ and $\sigma^2 \equiv \text{Var}[X_i] = 1$. We call $\mu$ the population mean and $\sigma^2$ the population variance.

Let
$$Y_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$$
be the sample mean. First, let us compute some basic properties:

1. **The mean:**
$$\mathbb{E}[Y_n] = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}X_i = \mu = 0.$$

2. **The variance:**
$$\text{Var}(Y_n) = \mathbb{E}[Y_n^2] = \frac{1}{n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} = \frac{1}{n}.$$

3. **The worst case:** What if we got unlucky and every coin toss landed heads. Can this even happen? How often will this happen? In this case we would have that, $Y_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i = 1$.

4. **The best case:** We might get really lucky and have exactly $n/2$ heads and $n/2$ tails (assuming $n$ is even). In this case we would have that, $Y_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i = 0$.

To summarize, in the good case the sample mean of the random variables will coincide exactly with the population mean, and in the worst-case it can be quite far from it. What we would really like to understand is the *typical case*.

In this chapter, we build the tools to show that in this example (as well as many others) **most of the time the sample mean is within $c/\sqrt{n}$ from the true mean, for some small constant $c$.** This is called the concentration of measure phenomenon. It is trying to quantify our intuition that it is very unlikely that independent random variables will conspire against us to cause the sample mean to be very far from the population mean.

## 2 Markov and Chebyshev’s Inequalities

**Markov’s Inequality** Suppose $X$ is a non-negative random variable and that $\mu = \mathbb{E}[X]$ exists. Then for any $t \ge 0$:
$$\mathbb{P}(X \ge t) \le \frac{\mu}{t}, \quad \text{or equivalently,} \quad \mathbb{P}(X \ge t\mu) \le \frac{1}{t}.$$

In words: a positive random variable is unlikely to be much larger than its mean.

**Proof:** Since $X > 0$,
$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^\infty x f_X(x) dx = \int_0^t x f_X(x) dx + \int_t^\infty x f_X(x) dx$$
$$\ge \int_t^\infty x f_X(x) dx \ge t \int_t^\infty f_X(x) dx = t \mathbb{P}(X \ge t).$$

Unfortunately, Markov’s inequality applies only to positive RVs, and the random variable $Y$ from the previous section is not positive. We need to derive more general inequalities.

**Chebyshev’s Inequality** Assume that $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$ both exist. For any $t \ge 0$:
$$\mathbb{P}(|X - \mu| \ge t\sigma) \le \frac{1}{t^2}.$$

In words: The deviation from the mean of any random variable is on the order of the standard deviation (hence the name), and more concretely the probability that a random variable is more than $t$ standard deviations away from its mean is at most $1/t^2$.

**Previous example:** Recall that $Y$ was an average with:
$$\mu_Y = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \sigma_Y = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}},$$
so that by Chebyshev’s inequality we have that:
$$\mathbb{P}\left(|Y| \ge \frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \le \frac{1}{t^2}.$$

This is precisely the type of quantitative bound we were hoping for. The average of $n$ independent random variables is typically roughly $1/\sqrt{n}$ distance from its expectation.

**Proof:** Chebyshev’s inequality is a simple consequence of Markov’s inequality:
$$\mathbb{P}(|X - \mu| \ge t\sigma) = \mathbb{P}((X - \mu)^2 \ge t^2\sigma^2)$$
$$\le \frac{\mathbb{E}(X - \mu)^2}{t^2\sigma^2} = \frac{1}{t^2}.$$

As a quick note, Markov’s inequality only applies to positive random variables but only needs that the random variable have a finite mean, Chebyshev’s inequality requires a finite mean and a finite variance. More generally, if we can bound higher moments (or the MGF) of a random variable we can get even tighter inequalities.

## 3 The Weak Law of Large Numbers (WLLN)

Assume that $X_1, . . . , X_n$ are i.i.d with $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty$. For any $\epsilon > 0$, by Chebyshev’s inequality,
$$\mathbb{P}\left(\left|\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i - \mathbb{E}(X)\right| \ge \epsilon\right) \le \epsilon^{-2} \mathbb{V}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n\epsilon^2} \to 0$$

The WLLN is a famous consequence of Chebyshev’s inequality. This law essentially assures us that the average of independent and identically distributed random variables “converges” to their expectation.

This type of convergence is quite common and has a name “convergence in probability”. So the weak LLN says that the sample average converges to the population average in probability. It is called the “weak law” because there is a stronger version that uses a different type of convergence. We will discuss convergence of random variables more systematically.

**Example 1** Let $\bar{X}_n$ be the head frequency in $n$ tosses of a fair coin. We can calculate a lower bound of the probability of $0.4 < \bar{X}_n < 0.6$.
$$\mathbb{P}(0.4 < \bar{X}_n < 0.6) = \mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| < 0.1) = 1 - \mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \ge 0.1) \ge 1 - \frac{25}{n}.$$
In particular, when $n = 100$ we have $\mathbb{P}(0.4 < \bar{X}_n < 0.6) \ge 0.75$.

## 4 Mill and Hoeffding’s Exponential Concentration Inequalities

There are two aspects to Chebyshev’s inequality that are worth noting: one of them is the typical deviation which is on the order of $\sigma$ and the probability $\frac{1}{t^2}$. In general, by assuming more things we can improve the probability part but the deviation term will always be on the order of $\sigma$. For instance, you might hear the terminology *exponential concentration inequalities*: these will use more refined techniques to improve the probability to be something like $\exp(-t^2)$, i.e. something much smaller than $\frac{1}{t^2}$.

**Mill’s inequality:** Let $Z \sim N(0, 1)$. For any $t > 0$:
$$\mathbb{P}(|Z| \ge t) \le \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \frac{\exp(-t^2/2)}{t} \le \frac{\exp(-t^2/2)}{t}.$$

Note again that the deviation is on the order of the standard deviation but the probability is exponential. This is much sharper than what we would get from Chebyshev’s inequality.

**Proof:** First we need the standard Gaussian density:
$$f_Z(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{x^2}{2}\right).$$

So that,
$$\mathbb{P}(|Z| \ge t) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_t^\infty \exp\left(-\frac{x^2}{2}\right) dx$$
$$= \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \frac{1}{t} \int_t^\infty t \exp\left(-\frac{x^2}{2}\right) dx$$
$$\le \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \frac{1}{t} \int_t^\infty x \exp\left(-\frac{x^2}{2}\right) dx$$
$$= \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \frac{1}{t} \int_{t^2/2}^\infty \exp(-u) du,$$
by taking $u = x^2/2$. Integrating gives:
$$\mathbb{P}(|Z| \ge t) \le \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \frac{\exp(-t^2/2)}{t} \quad qed.$$

The inequality is valid for standard normal RVs but we can extend it easily to more general Gaussians. For example, suppose that $X_1, . . . , X_n \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, independent. An important property of Gaussians is that linear combinations of Gaussians are also Gaussian, so
$$Y_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$$
is also normally distributed. In particular, $Y_n \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$. (The standard way to prove this is to use MGFs. Particularly, the MGF of a Gaussian RV $X_i$ is given by $M_X(t) = \exp(\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2)$. Use this and independence to prove the required result.)

We can then use Mill’s inequality and some basic properties about Gaussians to get inequalities for general Gaussian random variables. First observe that if $Y_n \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$ then
$$Z = \frac{Y_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}},$$
has $N(0, 1)$ distribution. We apply Mill’s inequality to $Z$ and do some simple algebra to obtain:
$$\mathbb{P}\left(|Y_n - \mu| \ge t \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \le \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \frac{\exp(-t^2/2)}{t}.$$

To compare, Chebyshev’s inequality yields:
$$\mathbb{P}\left(|Y_n - \mu| \ge t \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \le \frac{1}{t^2},$$
which is much weaker for most values of $t$.

The main drawback is that Mill’s inequality only applies to Gaussian random variables. Another commonly useful exponential concentration applies to bounded random variables.

**Hoeffding’s inequality:** Suppose that $X_1, . . . , X_n$ are independent and that, $a_i \le X_i \le b_i$, and $\mathbb{E}[X_i] = 0$. Then for any $t > 0$, we have the two results:
$$\mathbb{P}\left(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \ge t\right) \le \exp\left(-\frac{2n^2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right),$$
and
$$\mathbb{P}\left(\left|\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right| \ge t\right) \le 2 \exp\left(-\frac{2n^2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right).$$

Check Wasserman’s book for a proof if you are curious.

Hoeffding’s inequality looks a bit different from the other inequalities we have seen today. If we change variables:
$$u = \frac{nt}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}},$$
Then $\forall t > 0$ we also have $u > 0$ and we get:
$$\mathbb{P}\left(\left|\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right| \ge u \frac{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}}{n}\right) \le 2 \exp(-2u^2),$$
which is more like the earlier inequalities (if you prefer, replace $u$ by $t$ to make it look more like the previous inequalities). But notice that the standard deviation no longer appears: this is really because if $a_i \le X_i \le b_i$ then $\text{Var}(X_i) \le (b_i - a_i)^2$.

**Exercise:** Prove that $\text{Var}(X_i) \le (b_i - a_i)^2$, and thus that
$$\text{Var}\left(\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) \le \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}{n^2},$$
and this will allow us to interpret Hoeffding’s inequality in a more familiar way. Roughly, it says that the probability that the sample average is more than $t$ standard deviations from its expectation is at most $\exp(-2t^2)$.

Let us now use Hoeffding’s inequality in our case study example of coin tosses. There each random variable is between $-1$ and $1$ so we have that by Hoeffding’s inequality:
$$\mathbb{P}(|Y_n| \ge t/\sqrt{n}) \le 2 \exp(-t^2/2).$$

Observe once again this inequality is similar to Chebyshev’s inequality on the left hand side, i.e. the deviation is on the order of $1/\sqrt{n}$ but the right hand side is much smaller $\exp(-t^2)$ instead of $1/t^2$.

## 5 Confidence Intervals

We will cover confidence intervals in more detail later on but here is a quick preview. Hoeffding’s inequality gives us a simple way to create a confidence interval for a binomial parameter $p$. Let $\hat{p} = n^{-1} \sum_i X_i$ be the fraction of tosses that are heads. Fix $\alpha > 0$ and let,
$$t = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2n} \log(2/\alpha)}.$$

By Hoeffding’s inequality,
$$\mathbb{P}(|\hat{p} - p| \ge t) \le 2 \exp(-2nt^2) = \alpha.$$

Let $C = [\hat{p} - t, \hat{p} + t]$. Then,
$$\mathbb{P}(p \notin C) \le \alpha$$
where $C$ is a random set and $p$ is fixed. Hence, the random interval $C$ traps the true parameter value $p$ with probability at least $1 - \alpha$; we call $C$ a $1 - \alpha$ confidence interval.
