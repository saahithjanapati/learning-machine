1. Fall is here! I lost my umbrella so I would like to estimate the probability that it will not rain during the whole month of October.

(a) Assuming that the climate has not changed in the past 4 years and assuming that we have no effects such as La Nina or El Nino, which could affect rainfall dramatically, estimate the probability of no rainfall in October 2024 using the data below. The data consist of the rainfall numbers in Pittsburgh in October 2024, 2023, 2022 and 2021, courtesy of wunderground.com.
*Hint: as discussed during lecture (see Chapter 1 lecture slides), use the chain rule, then assume that the rain process is first-order Markov, i.e. that the future only depends on the most recent past. Also assume a stationary process for the month of October, so that, for example, the probability of rain on Oct 1 is the same as the probability of rain on Oct 2, 3, 4, etc...*

**Solution:** Let $A_i$ be the event that Day $i$ is dry. The probability I want is
$$P(A_1, A_2, \dots, A_{31}) = P(\cap_{i=1}^{31} A_i).$$

In class we use the chain rule to rewrite
$$P(\cap_{i=1}^{31} A_i) = P(A_1)P(A_2 \mid A_1)P(A_3 \mid A_2, A_1) \dots P(A_{31} \mid A_{30}, A_{29}, \dots, A_1),$$
and then we assumed that the process could be Markov (not a bad first assumption), so we could rewrite
$$P(\cap_{i=1}^{31} A_i) \approx P(A_1)P(A_2 \mid A_1)P(A_3 \mid A_2) \dots P(A_{31} \mid A_{30})$$
$$= P(A_1) \prod_{i=2}^{31} P(A_i \mid A_{i-1}).$$

Now if we assume that all days in the month are exchangeable, we can rewrite this as
$$P(A_1) \prod_{i=2}^{31} P(A_i \mid A_{i-1}) \approx P_a(P_b)^{30},$$

(b) As a comparison, calculate the probability of no rainfall in October, assuming that the rainfall process is random, i.e. that the chance of rain on any day is independent of the past. In the class notes jargon, I am asking you to assume that all the days are mutually independent.

**Solution:** Here you are asked to make the dubious assumption that consecutive days are independent. In that case we would write
$$P(\cap_{i=1}^{31} A_i) \approx P(A_1)P(A_2)P(A_3) \dots P(A_{31}) = \prod_{i=1}^{31} P(A_i) \approx (P_a)^{31}.$$

Using our calculations above,
$$P(\cap_{i=1}^{31} A_i) \approx \left(\frac{41}{62}\right)^{31} \approx 2.70 \cdot 10^{-6}$$

Which result, (a) or (b), do you think is likely to be closer to the truth? Explain.

**Solution:** Approach (b) makes a much stronger assumption than approach (a), and it appears to be a dubious assumption. However, if the assumption in (b) were correct, both (a) and (b) would provide the same numerical answer asymptotically, although the answer to (b) would be more accurate (with lower variance), since we would only have to estimate $P_a$.

2. Prove that the function: $F_X(x) = 1 - \exp(-x)$ for $x \in (0, \infty)$, and $F_X(x) = 0$ for $x \le 0$, is a valid CDF.

**Solution:** In order to show $F_X$ is a valid CDF, we need to verify three conditions in the lecture notes.

(a) The CDF approaches 0 on the left: $F_X(t) = 0$ if $t < 0$, so
i. $\lim_{t \to -\infty} F_X(t) = 0$.
ii. $\lim_{t \to \infty} F_X(t) = \lim_{x \to \infty} [1 - \exp(-x)] = 1 - \lim_{x \to \infty} \exp(-x) = 1$.

(b) Non-decreasing: for $0 < a < b$,
$$F_X(b) - F_X(a) = \exp(-a) - \exp(-b) > 0.$$

(c) Right-continuous: $F_X$ is differentiable at any $x \in (0, \infty)$, i.e. $dF_X(t)/dt = \exp(-t)$, and thus $F_X$ is (right) continuous at any $x \in (0, \infty)$.

3. Let $(X, Y)$ have the uniform distribution on $[-1, 1] \times [-1, 1]$. Find the probability that
(a) $X = 1$.
(b) $0.5 \le X \le 1$.
(c) $0.5 \le X \le 1.5$.
(d) $X + Y \ge 1/2$.

**Solution:** The joint PDF is given by $f_{X,Y}(x, y) = 1/4$ on $[-1, 1] \times [-1, 1]$ and $f(x, y) = 0$ for all other $x, y$.

(a): Since $(X, Y)$ is a pair of continuous random variables, the probability must be 0. Or put another way, the integral of the PDF on the set $\{1\} \times [-1, 1]$ is 0 because that region has area 0.

(b):
$$\mathbb{P}(0.5 \le X \le 1) = \int_{1/2}^1 \int_{-1}^1 f_{X,Y}(x, y) dy dx = \int_{1/2}^1 \int_{-1}^1 \frac{1}{4} dy dx = \int_{1/2}^1 \frac{1}{2} dx = \frac{1}{4}.$$

(c): Note that $f_{X,Y}(x, y)$ is 0 if $x \ge 1$. Hence
$$\mathbb{P}(0.5 \le X \le 1.5) = \int_{1/2}^{1.5} \int_{-1}^1 f_{X,Y}(x, y) dy dx = \int_{1/2}^1 \int_{-1}^1 f_{X,Y}(x, y) dy dx = \frac{1}{4},$$
repeating the calculations in (b) in the final step.

(d): The region of integration can be seen shaded blue in the following figure. For any $x < -1/2$, there is no $y \in [-1, 1]$ such that $x + y \ge 1/2$. Thus, it suffices to consider all $x \in [-1/2, 1]$. For each such $x$, the set of $y \in [-1, 1]$ with our condition is the interval $[1/2 - x, 1]$ (the dotted line). Note that if $x \in [-1/2, 1]$,

4. Let $(X, Y)$ have the uniform distribution on the set $\{(x, y) : x^2 + y^2 \le 1\}$. Write down an expression for the joint density function of $(X, Y)$.

**Solution:** Let $S = \{(x, y) : x^2 + y^2 \le 1\}$. The PDF of a continuous uniform distribution on $S$ must be equal to a non-zero constant on $S$ and 0 elsewhere. Suppose that constant is $c$. Then we require $\int_S c dx dy = 1$. Performing a change of coordinates to polar form, we must have
$$1 = \int_S c \cdot dx dy = \int_0^1 \int_0^{2\pi} cr \cdot d\theta dr = c\pi.$$

Hence $c = \pi^{-1}$. The density is therefore
$$f_{X,Y}(x, y) = \begin{cases} \pi^{-1} & \text{if } x^2 + y^2 \le 1, \\ 0 & \text{elsewhere.} \end{cases}$$

5. Let $X$ be uniformly distributed on $[-5, 1]$. Let $Y = X^4$. Find the cdf and pdf of $Y$.

*Hint: determine first if $Y$ is continuous or discrete, then determine what values the new RV takes. This will help you identify the appropriate method to calculate PDF and CDF.*

**Solution:**

The density of the continuous random variable $X$ is $1/6$ on $[-5, 1]$ and 0 elsewhere. The random variable $Y$ is continuous and will take values in $[0, 5^4]$. Let us compute the CDF by splitting the range of $Y$ into cases.

If $t < 0$, then $\mathbb{P}(Y \le t) = 0$ since $Y$ is non-negative.

Suppose $t \in [0, 1]$, which means $t^{1/4} \in [0, 1]$. In this case, $Y \le t$ iff $-t^{1/4} \le X \le t^{1/4}$ which occurs with probability $(t^{1/4} - (-t^{1/4})) \cdot 1/6 = t^{1/4}/3$ using the PDF of $X$. In particular, note $\mathbb{P}(Y \le 1) = 1/3$.

Next, suppose $1 < t < 5^4$. Well $Y \in [1, t]$ iff $X \in [-t^{1/4}, -1]$ or $X \in [1, t^{1/4}]$. But $X \le 1$, so the latter event has probability 0. Hence
$$\mathbb{P}(1 \le Y \le t) = \mathbb{P}(X \in [-t^{1/4}, -1]) = \frac{-1 - (-t^{1/4})}{6} = \frac{t^{1/4} - 1}{6}.$$

Since we showed $\mathbb{P}(Y \le 1) = 1/3$, it follows
$$\mathbb{P}(Y \le t) = \mathbb{P}(Y \le 1) + \mathbb{P}(1 \le Y \le t) = 1/3 + \frac{t^{1/4} - 1}{6} = \frac{t^{1/4} + 1}{6}.$$

Lastly, if $t > 5^4$, then $Y \le t$ always holds, so $\mathbb{P}(Y \le t) = 1$.

Putting this together, we obtain the CDF
$$F_Y(t) = \mathbb{P}(Y \le t) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } t < 0 \\ t^{1/4}/3 & \text{if } t \in [0, 1] \\ (1 + t^{1/4})/6 & \text{if } t \in [1, 5^4] \\ 1 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}$$

Differentiating, the PDF is therefore given by
$$f_Y(t) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } t < 0 \text{ or } t > 5^4 \\ t^{-3/4}/12 & \text{if } t \in (0, 1] \\ t^{-3/4}/24 & \text{if } t \in (1, 5^4], \end{cases}$$
noting that this is continuous everywhere except at $t = 1$ and $t = 5^4$.

Let $X_1, \dots, X_n$ be i.i.d. exponential and set $Y = \max\{X_1, \dots, X_n\}$. For $y \ge 0$,
$$F_Y(y) = \text{Pr}(Y \le y) = \text{Pr}(X_1 \le y, \dots, X_n \le y) = \prod_{i=1}^n \text{Pr}(X_i \le y) = (F_X(y))^n.$$
Differentiating,
$$f_Y(y) = F'_Y(y) = n(F_X(y))^{n-1} f_X(y), \quad y \ge 0,$$
and $f_Y(y) = 0$ for $y < 0$.
The PDF and CDF of an exponential parameterized by scale $\beta > 0$ are $f_X(x) = \beta^{-1} e^{-x/\beta}$ and $F_X(x) = 1 - e^{-x/\beta}$. Then
$$f_Y(y) = \frac{n}{\beta} e^{-y/\beta} (1 - e^{-y/\beta})^{n-1} \mathbf{1}\{y \ge 0\}.$$

8. (**A universal random generator.**) Let $X$ have a continuous, strictly increasing CDF $F$.
(a) Let $Y = F(X)$. Find the density of $Y$. This is called the *probability integral transform*.
(b) Now let $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ and let $Z = F^{-1}(U)$. Show that $Z \sim F$.
Effectively this result lets us draw samples from any distribution whose CDF is known, via samples from the uniform distribution on $[0, 1]$. (Try for example writing a program that takes $\text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ random variables and generates random variables from an $\text{Exponential}(1)$ distribution; no need to hand in this last part)

**Solution:** Note that $0 \le Y \le 1$. Now, if $y \in (0, 1)$, then
$$F_Y(y) = \mathbb{P}(Y \le y) = \mathbb{P}(F_X(X) \le y) = \mathbb{P}(X \le F_X^{-1}(y)) = F_X(F_X^{-1}(y)) = y.$$
So $F_Y(y) = y$ for $y \in (0, 1)$. If $y \ge 1$, then $F_Y(y) = 1$ since $Y \in [0, 1]$ and similarly, if $y \le 0$ then $F_Y(y) = 0$. Thus, $F_Y$ is the CDF of a $\text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ random variable. The density of $Y$ is therefore equal to 1 on $(0, 1)$ and 0 elsewhere.

Next, let $Z = F^{-1}(U)$ where $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$. Pick any $z$ such that $F(z) \in (0, 1)$. Then,
$$\mathbb{P}(Z \le z) = \mathbb{P}(F^{-1}(U) \le z) = \mathbb{P}(U \le F(z)) = F(z)$$
where in the last step we used that $\mathbb{P}(U \le c) = c$ for every $0 < c < 1$. If $F(z) = 0$, then $\mathbb{P}(U \le F(z)) = 0 = F(z)$ since $U$ takes values in $[0, 1]$ and similarly if $F(z) = 1$, then $\mathbb{P}(U \le F(z)) = 1 = F(z)$. Since for any $z \in \mathbb{R}$ we have $F(z) \in [0, 1]$, we have exhausted all cases. Thus, $\mathbb{P}(Z \le z) = F(z)$ for all $z \in \mathbb{R}$. Hence $Z \sim F$.

9. Suppose that a random variable $X$ has a continuous distribution on an interval $I \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, with density $f_X$. Find the density of $Y = aX + b$, where $a \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$ (i.e. $a$ is not 0) and $b \in \mathbb{R}$. Use the Jacobian method, and justify why you can use the method here. (Remark: The Jacobian method won't occur on the exam, but I've included it in the HW so you can review your multivariable calculus and practice change of variables)

**Solution:** Since the transformation $T : x \mapsto ax + b$ is differentiable on $\mathbb{R}$, and either increasing (if $a > 0$) or decreasing (if $a < 0$), we can use the formula with $T^{-1} : y \mapsto (y - b)/a$:
$$f_Y(y) = f_X(T^{-1}(y)) \left| \frac{dT^{-1}(y)}{dy} \right|$$
$$= \begin{cases} \frac{1}{|a|} f_X\left(\frac{y-b}{a}\right), & y \in \{as + b : s \in I\}, \\ 0, & \text{o.w.} \end{cases}$$
