1. **[The $\chi^2$ test]** In class, we discussed that the $\chi^2$ test statistic for multinomials has $k - 1$ degrees of freedom under the null. This is somewhat counterintuitive since we are summing $k$ terms which appear to have $\chi^2$ distribution under the null. The catch however is that the counts and expected counts sum to $n$, and so there is one fewer degree of freedom.

We will try to rigorously understand this statement for binomials. There are $n$ trials. Under the null hypothesis we will denote the binomial by the vector $(p_1, p_2)$ where $p_1$ is the probability of seeing a zero and $p_2$ is the probability of seeing a one in each trial. Observe that $p_1 + p_2 = 1$. Suppose that the number of zeros is denoted by $X_1$ and the number of ones is $X_2$. Concretely, we would like to show that the $\chi^2$ statistic:
$$T = \frac{(X_1 - np_1)^2}{np_1} + \frac{(X_2 - np_2)^2}{np_2}$$
has a $\chi^2_1$ distribution.

(a) Show that the test statistic can be equivalently written as:
$$T = \frac{(X_1 - np_1)^2}{np_1(1 - p_1)}.$$

**Solution:**
$$T = \frac{(X_1 - np_1)^2}{np_1} + \frac{(X_2 - np_2)^2}{np_2}$$
$$= \frac{(X_1 - np_1)^2(1 - p_1) + (X_2 - np_2)^2 p_1}{np_1(1 - p_1)}$$
$$= \frac{(X_1 - np_1)^2(1 - p_1) + (n - X_1 - n(1 - p_1))^2 p_1}{np_1(1 - p_1)}$$
$$= \frac{(X_1 - np_1)^2(1 - p_1) + (X_1 - np_1)^2 p_1}{np_1(1 - p_1)}$$
$$= \frac{(X_1 - np_1)^2}{np_1(1 - p_1)}$$

(b) Use the above expression to argue that the distribution of $T$ under the null is a $\chi^2_1$ distribution (asymptotically). To do this you need to argue that $Z = \frac{(X_1 - np_1)}{\sqrt{np_1(1 - p_1)}}$ has an asymptotically standard Gaussian distribution.

**Solution:** Since $X_1 = \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$ where each $Y_i$ is i.i.d from Bernoulli distribution with $p_1$, the central limit theorem says that
$$\sqrt{n}(\bar{Y} - p_1) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, p_1(1 - p_1)).$$

2. **[Wald versus permutation testing]** Problem 7 in Wasserman page 171
**Solution:** (a) For Twain essays the sample statistics are
$$\bar{X} = 0.239, \quad S^2_X = 0.000212$$
For Snodgrass essays the sample statistics are
$$\bar{Y} = 0.2097, \quad S^2_Y = 0.000093$$
The standard error for the difference of means using the plug-in estimator is
$$\widehat{SE} = \sqrt{\frac{S^2_X}{n} + \frac{S^2_Y}{m}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.000212}{8} + \frac{0.000093}{10}} = 0.00598$$
The Wald test statistic is
$$W = \frac{|\bar{X} - \bar{Y}|}{\widehat{SE}} = \frac{|0.239 - 0.2097|}{0.00598} = 3.71$$
The p-value is $2(1 - \Phi(3.71)) = 0.0002$. A 95% confidence interval for the difference of means is
$$(\bar{X} - \bar{Y}) \pm 1.96\widehat{SE} = [0.0105, 0.0339]$$
Since we reject $H_0$, there is strong evidence that the proportion of three-letter words is different between the authors.

(b) $H_0$: the distributions of $X$ and $Y$ are the same. Calculate the observed difference $T_{obs} = \bar{X} - \bar{Y} = 0.0222$. For each permutation of the data points, split the data points into two groups of size 8 and 10 and then recompute the difference of means $T_i = \bar{X}_{perm_i} - \bar{Y}_{perm_i}$. Doing this exactly, the p-value is equal to
$$\frac{1}{\binom{18}{8}} \sum_i I(|T_i| \ge |T_{obs}|) \approx 0.0008.$$
So we reject $H_0$ at the 0.05 level, again suggesting that there is strong evidence that the proportion of three-letter words is different between the authors.

(c) List sufficient conditions on $h_n$ and $n$ to ensure that the $\hat{f}(x)$ is consistent for each $x$, followed by one such example for $h_n$ that is different from the optimal solution derived in class. Notes:
- "sufficient" conditions means that if these conditions are met, then we can prove consistency. However, these conditions may not be necessary, i.e. they may be stronger than we need to prove consistency.
- Go back to your past notes to review what consistency means and how other forms of convergence relate to consistency. This is also a good review for the final exam.

**Solution:**
(a): As $h$ grows, the variance of the histogram decreases and the bias of the histogram increases. Whereas, as $h$ decreases, the variance increases and the bias decreases. So as $h$ varies, there is a *bias-variance tradeoff*.

(b): To balance between the bias and the variance, we choose $h$ to optimize $g(h) = (Lh)^2 + \left[ \frac{1}{nh} + \frac{L}{n} \right]$. Since $g''(h) > 0$ for $h > 0$, we can easily minimize the $g(h)$ by taking derivatives to get
$$h_{op} = \left( \frac{1}{2L^2n} \right)^{\frac{1}{3}}.$$
We then compute the minimum risk:
$$g(h_{op}) = \left( \frac{L}{2n} \right)^{2/3} + \sqrt[3]{2} \cdot \left( \frac{L}{n} \right)^{2/3} + \frac{L}{n}$$
$$= \frac{3}{2^{2/3}} \cdot \left( \frac{L}{n} \right)^{2/3} + \frac{L}{n}.$$

(c): In general, if $h \to 0$ and $nh \to \infty$ as $n \to \infty$, the histogram estimator is consistent. For example, let $h$ be $n^{-1/2}$, which is sub-optimal but is nonetheless consistent.

(c) Show that if $h \to 0$ and $nh \to \infty$, as $n \to \infty$ then $\hat{f}(x) \to f(x)$ in probability.

**Solution:**
(a):
$$\mathbb{E}(\hat{f}(x)) = \mathbb{E} \left( \frac{1}{nh} \sum_{i=1}^n K \left( \frac{X_i - x}{h} \right) \right)$$
$$= \frac{1}{nh} \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E} \left( I \left( -\frac{1}{2} < \frac{X_i - x}{h} < \frac{1}{2} \right) \right) \text{ since } X_1, \dots, X_n \text{ are i.i.d.}$$
$$= \frac{1}{h} \mathbb{E} \left( I \left( -\frac{1}{2} < \frac{X_1 - x}{h} < \frac{1}{2} \right) \right)$$
$$= \frac{1}{h} \mathbb{P} \left( x - \frac{h}{2} < X_1 < x + \frac{h}{2} \right)$$
$$= \frac{1}{h} \int_{x-(h/2)}^{x+(h/2)} f(y)dy.$$

(b): Let $I \left( x - \frac{h}{2} < X_1 < x + \frac{h}{2} \right) = I(X_1 \in C_{x,h})$.
$$Var(\hat{f}(x)) = \frac{1}{nh^2} Var(I(X_1 \in C_{x,h}))$$
$$= \frac{1}{nh^2} \left[ \mathbb{E} \left( I^2(X_1 \in C_{x,h}) \right) - (\mathbb{E}(I(X_1 \in C_{x,h})))^2 \right]$$
$$= \frac{1}{nh^2} \left[ \int_{x-(h/2)}^{x+(h/2)} f(y)dy - \left( \int_{x-(h/2)}^{x+(h/2)} f(y)dy \right)^2 \right].$$

(c): It is enough to show that $MSE(\hat{f}(x)) \to 0$ since the convergence in quadratic mean implies the convergence in probability.
- **Bias:**
$$\lim_{h \to 0} \mathbb{E}(\hat{f}(x)) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \int_{x-(h/2)}^{x+(h/2)} f(y)dy$$
$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{F(x + (h/2)) - F(x - (h/2))}{h}$$
$$= f(x)$$
where $F$ is the CDF of $X$. Therefore, $bias^2(\hat{f}(x)) \to 0$ as $h \to 0$.
- **Variance:** Note that
$$Var(\hat{f}(x)) \le \frac{1}{nh^2} \int_{x-(h/2)}^{x+(h/2)} f(y)dy$$
and
$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \int_{x-(h/2)}^{x+(h/2)} f(y)dy = f(x)$$
