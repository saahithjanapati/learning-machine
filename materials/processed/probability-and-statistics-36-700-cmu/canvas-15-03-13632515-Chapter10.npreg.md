## 1 Introduction: Regression

Regression is one the most basic and important tools in data analysis. In regression, we study the relationship between a response variable $Y$, and a predictor, feature or covariate $X$. One way to understand the relationship between the predictor and covariate is through the *regression function*
$$r(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y | X = x] = \int_y y f(y|x) dy.$$

Broadly, the goal of regression is to *estimate* the regression function from observations, i.e. we observe pairs $(X_1, Y_1), \dots, (X_n, Y_n) \sim F_{X,Y}$, and we would like to estimate the regression function.

There are several motivations to study regression, e.g. explaining data, estimating a data generating model, interpreting linear regression coefficients, attempting to estimate causal effects, etc. The art of regression and model selection very much depends on the goal. Make sure to identify your goal before launching into some inappropriate analysis.

The methods described here address the problem of prediction from models when one needs to go beyond fitting linear models to data.

## 2 Prediction Risk and Bias-Variance Tradeoff in Regression

Suppose we observe $X$ and then predict $Y$ with $g(X)$. Using squared error loss, the **prediction error**, or **prediction risk**, is
$$R(g) = \mathbb{E}(Y - g(X))^2$$
which is minimized by taking $g(x) = r(x) = \mathbb{E}(Y | X = x)$. This explains why we are so interested in the regression function.

### 2.1 The Bias-Variance Decomposition

Let $\hat{r}(x)$ be a predictor estimated using $i = 1, \dots, n$ observations $(X_i, Y_i)$. As a function of random variables $(X_1, Y_1), \dots, (X_n, Y_n)$, $\hat{r}(x)$ is a random variable itself, calculated at a fixed value of $x$. In the calculations below think of $(X, Y)$ as new input and output variables, independent of $(X_1, Y_1), \dots, (X_n, Y_n)$. The risk of predicting $Y$ with $\hat{r}(X)$ is
$$R = \mathbb{E}(Y - \hat{r}(X))^2 = \int R(x) f(x) dx$$
where $R(x) = \mathbb{E}((Y - \hat{r}(x))^2 | X = x)$.$^1$

Let
$$\bar{r}(x) = \mathbb{E}(\hat{r}(x)),$$
$$V(x) = \mathbb{V}(\hat{r}(x))$$
$$\sigma^2(x) = \mathbb{V}(Y | X = x).$$

Now
$$R(x) = \mathbb{E}((Y - \hat{r}(x))^2 | X = x)$$
$$= \mathbb{E} \left[ ((Y - r(x)) + (r(x) - \bar{r}(x)) + (\bar{r}(x) - \hat{r}(x)))^2 \bigg| X = x \right]$$
$$= \underbrace{\sigma^2(x)}_{\text{irreducible error}} + \underbrace{(r(x) - \bar{r}(x))^2}_{\text{bias squared}} + \underbrace{V(x)}_{\text{variance}}, \quad (1)$$

---
$^1$ If $X$ and $Y$ are random variables, recall the rule of iterated expectations
$$\mathbb{E}[g(X, Y)] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}(g(X, Y) | X = x)],$$
where the inner expectation is taken with respect to $Y|X$ and the outer one is taken with respect to the marginal distribution of $X$. Throughout the following section, we use this rule, conditioning on $X$ to obtain the risk function $R(x)$ at $X = x$.

**Note:** The irreducible error is the error due to unmodeled variation, such as instrument error and population variability around the model. The bias is the lack of fit between the assumed model and the true relationship between $Y$ and $X$. This will be zero if the assumed model $r(x)$ includes the truth $\mathbb{E}[Y | X = x]$. The variance is the statistical variability in the estimation procedure. As $n \to \infty$ this quantity goes to zero. Finally, all the cross product terms have expectation 0 because $Y$ is independent of $\hat{r}(x)$ (Recall that $\bar{r}(x)$ is not a random variable, so its expectation is equal to itself).

## 3 1D Non-Parametric Regression

In linear regression, we assume that $r$ is linear (or is well approximated by a linear function). In particular, *simple linear regression* refers to the case when $X$ is 1-dimensional, i.e., we measure a single covariate. In this case we have
$$r(x) = \mathbb{E}(Y | X = x) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x.$$
and the problem of regression becomes equivalent to estimating the parameters $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$ that (typically) minimize a squared-error loss. (Refer to Wasserman Chapter 13 for Linear Regression)

In this lecture, we discuss the task of *nonparametric regression*. Unlike the CDF which we can estimate without assumptions about the distribution, here we will need *smoothness* assumptions, i.e. we will need to assume that $r(x)$ is a smooth function of $x$. This allows us to gain statistical strength by averaging near by points.

A natural strategy in non-parametric regression is to locally average the data, i.e. our estimate of the regression function at any point will be the average of the $Y$ values in a small neighborhood of the point.

The width of this neighborhood will determine the bias and variance. Too large a neighborhood will result in high bias and low variance (this is called **oversmoothing**) and too small a neighborhood will result in low bias but large variance (this is known as **undersmoothing**).

### 3.1 Regressogram

The simplest nonparametric estimator is the **regressogram**. Suppose that the $X_i$’s are in the interval $[0, 1]$. Divide the interval into $m$ bins of equal length. Thus each has length $h = 1/m$. Denote the bins by $B_1, \dots, B_m$. Let $k_j$ be the number of observations in bin $B_j$ and let $\bar{Y}_j$ be the mean of the $Y_i$’s in bin $B_j$. Define
$$\hat{r}_n(x) = \frac{1}{k_j} \sum_{i: X_i \in B_j} Y_i = \bar{Y}_j \quad \text{for } x \in B_j. \quad (2)$$
That is, the estimate $\hat{r}_n$ is a step function obtained by averaging the $Y_i$s over each bin. We can rewrite the estimator as
$$\hat{r}_n(x) = \sum_{i=1}^n w_i(x) Y_i$$
where $w_i(x) = 1/k_j$ if $(x, X_i) \in B_j$ and $w_i(x) = 0$ otherwise.

Note that large bins cause large bias and small bins cause large variance. When the data are oversmoothed, the bias term is large and the variance is small. When the data are undersmoothed the opposite is true; see Figure 3. This is the **bias–variance tradeoff**. Minimizing the risk corresponds to balancing bias and variance.

1. **Assumptions about the design:** We will assume that $x_i$ is one-dimensional, and equally spaced on $[0, 1]$, so that each bin has $k = n/m$ points, where $m$ is the number of bins. The width of the bins is $h = 1/m$.
In general, we do not need that the design is equally spaced but intuitively we do need to ensure that we see some points in the vicinity of each point where $f$ is non-zero.

2. **Assumptions about the regression function:** We will assume that the function $r(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y|X = x]$ is $L$-Lipschitz, i.e. that there is some constant $L$ such that:
$$\left| \frac{d}{dx}r(x) \right| \le L.$$

3. **Assumptions about the noise:** We will assume that the noise is i.i.d and that $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon_i] = 0, \text{Var}[\epsilon_i] = \sigma^2$.

**The main result:** The point-wise bias of the kernel regression estimator:
$$b(x) = \mathbb{E}[\hat{r}(x)] - r(x) \le Lh,$$
and the point-wise variance:
$$\text{Var}(\hat{r}(x)) = \mathbb{E}(\hat{r}(x) - \mathbb{E}(\hat{r}(x)))^2 \le \frac{\sigma^2}{nh}.$$

Before we prove this, let us observe that we can calculate the integrated risk:
$$R(\hat{r}, r) = \int b^2(x)dx + \int v(x)dx \le L^2h^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{nh},$$
(note that we obtain the same result $+\sigma^2$ if we use the prediction risk) a natural strategy would be to choose the bandwidth to minimize this expression. Differentiating wrt $h$ and setting to 0 yields:
$$h = \left( \frac{\sigma^2}{2nL^2} \right)^{1/3},$$
so the minimum risk is:
$$R(\hat{r}, r) \le \frac{2L^{2/3}\sigma^{4/3}}{n^{2/3}} = 2 \left( \frac{L\sigma^2}{n} \right)^{2/3}.$$
This result already reveals something fundamental about non-parametrics. We see that with an optimally chosen bandwidth, the MISE decreases to 0 at rate $n^{-2/3}$. By comparison, most parametric estimators converge at rate $n^{-1}$. The slower rate of convergence is the price we pay for being nonparametric. The variance is
$$\frac{\sigma^2}{k} = \frac{m\sigma^2}{n} = \frac{\sigma^2}{nh}.$$
The mean squared error at $x$ is the squared bias plus the variance:
$$\text{MSE}(x) \le h^2L^2 + \frac{\sigma^2}{nh},$$
and the MSE at other values of $x$ is the same, yielding the required result.

**4 pD Non-Parametric Regression**

More generally, suppose that the $\beta^{\text{th}}$ derivative of $r(x)$ is bounded, and we are in $d$-dimensions. In this case the bias will be roughly:
$$b^2(x) \approx h^{2\beta},$$
and the variance:
$$v(x) \approx \frac{1}{nh^d},$$
and balancing these will lead to the rate of convergence:
$$R(\hat{r}, r) \approx n^{-2\beta/(2\beta+d)}.$$
This reveals another crucial feature of non-parametrics. In linear regression, the rate of convergence is typically something like:
$$R(\hat{\beta}, \beta) \approx \frac{d}{n}.$$
In both cases, the situation gets worse as $d$ increases, however in non-parametrics the situation gets *exponentially* worse. This is often colloquially referred to as the *curse of dimensionality*.
