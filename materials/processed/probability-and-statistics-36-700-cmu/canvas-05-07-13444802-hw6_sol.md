1. Suppose we have i.i.d. samples $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim U[0, \theta]$.
(a) Sketch the likelihood function for $\theta \in [0, 10]$. (It is a function of $\theta$ — what shape does it have?)
**Solution:** First, we compute:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathcal{L}(\theta; X_1, \dots, X_n) &= \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\theta} \cdot \mathbb{I}(X_i \in [0, \theta]) \\
&= \frac{1}{\theta^n} \cdot \mathbb{I}(X_i \in [0, \theta] \text{ for all } 1 \le i \le n) \\
&= \frac{1}{\theta^n} \cdot \mathbb{I}(0 \le X_{(1)}) \cdot \mathbb{I}(X_{(n)} \le \theta) \\
&= \frac{1}{\theta^n} \cdot \mathbb{I}(X_{(n)} \le \theta)
\end{aligned}
$$
In the second to last step, we used that $X_i \in [0, \theta]$ for all $i \in \{1, \dots, n\}$ if and only if the smallest datapoint, denoted $X_{(1)}$, is $\ge 0$, and the largest, $X_{(n)}$, is $\le \theta$. In the last step, we noted that since all the $X_i \sim U[0, \theta]$, $X_{(1)} \ge 0$ is guaranteed.

**Figure 1:** Likelihood plotted for $n = 5$ and $X_{(n)} = 1.2$

Note: *In this case it is critical to ensure that the parameter we are seeking to estimate, i.e., $\theta$, is in the support of our likelihood. As such we can't just differentiate the log-likelihood per usual and have to carefully consider the shape of the likelihood over its support and choose the $\theta$ value that maximizes it.*

(b) Compute the MLE of $\theta$.
**Solution:** Notice that $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ is 0 for all $\theta < X_{(n)}$, and it is only positive for $\theta \ge X_{(n)}$. For all such $\theta \ge X_{(n)}$, $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \theta^{-n}$ is clearly decreasing in $\theta$. Therefore,
$$ \hat{\theta} = \arg \max_{\theta \in \mathbb{R}} \mathcal{L}(\theta; X_1, \dots, X_n) = \arg \max_{\theta \in \mathbb{R}} \theta^{-n} \mathbb{I}(X_{(n)} \le \theta) = X_{(n)}. $$

2. The exponential distribution (in its **scale** parameterization) has density
$$ p(x; \beta) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{\beta} \exp(-x/\beta) & x \ge 0 \\ 0 & x < 0, \end{cases} $$
where $\beta > 0$ is our parameter of interest. Suppose we are given a sample of size $n$ from an exponential distribution with unknown parameter $\beta$. Compute the MLE for $\beta$, compute the Fisher information, use this to determine the asymptotic distribution of the MLE, and construct an asymptotically valid $1 - \alpha$ confidence interval for $\beta$.
Explain why the limiting distribution you obtained is reasonable (how else could you have obtained this limiting distribution?).
**Solution:** The log-likelihood function and the score function are
$$ \ell(\beta; X_1, \dots, X_n) = -n \log(\beta) - \sum_{i=1}^n X_i/\beta, $$
$$ s(\beta; X_1, \dots, X_n) = -n/\beta + \sum_{i=1}^n X_i/\beta^2. $$

Thus, the first order optimality condition gives the MLE as follows.
$$ -n/\hat{\beta} + \sum_{i=1}^n X_i/\hat{\beta}^2 = 0 \implies \hat{\beta} = \bar{X}_n. $$
The Fisher information is calculated as
$$ I(\beta) = \mathbb{V}(s(\beta; X)) = \mathbb{V}(X/\beta^2) = \mathbb{V}(X)/\beta^4 = 1/\beta^2. $$
The asymptotic normality of MLE and Slutsky’s theorem implies
$$ \sqrt{n}(\hat{\beta} - \beta) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \hat{\beta}^2). $$
Thus, an asymptotically valid confidence interval for $\beta$ is
$$ \hat{\beta} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \frac{\hat{\beta}}{\sqrt{n}} = \bar{X}_n \left[ 1 \pm \frac{z_{\alpha/2}}{\sqrt{n}} \right]. $$
Despite we used the asymptotic normality of MLE and the Fisher information, notice that CLT can be applied to $\hat{\beta} = \bar{X}_n$. Since the mean and the variance of exponential distribution are $\beta, \beta^2$, we obtain the same limiting distribution.

3. The exponential distribution (in its **rate** parameterization) has density
$$ p(x; \lambda) = \begin{cases} \lambda \exp(-\lambda x) & x \ge 0 \\ 0 & x < 0, \end{cases} $$
where $\lambda > 0$ is our parameter of interest. Suppose we are given a sample of size $n$ from an exponential distribution with unknown parameter $\lambda$. Compute the MLE for $\lambda$, compute the Fisher information, use this to determine the asymptotic distribution of the MLE, and construct an asymptotically valid $1 - \alpha$ confidence interval for $\lambda$.
Explain why the limiting distribution you obtained is reasonable (how else could you have obtained this limiting distribution?). It might be helpful to recall the delta method.
**Solution:** We follow the similar footstep of the previous question. The log-likelihood function and the score function are
$$ \ell(\lambda; X_1, \dots, X_n) = n \log \lambda - \lambda \sum_{i=1}^n X_i, $$
$$ s(\lambda; X_1, \dots, X_n) = n/\lambda - \sum_{i=1}^n X_i. $$
Thus, the first order optimality condition gives the MLE as follows.
$$ \hat{\lambda} = 1/\bar{X}_n. $$
The Fisher information is calculated as
$$ I(\lambda) = \mathbb{V}(s(\lambda; X)) = \mathbb{V}(X) = 1/\lambda^2. $$

The asymptotic normality of MLE and Slutsky’s theorem implies
$$ \sqrt{n}(\hat{\lambda} - \lambda) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \hat{\lambda}^2). \quad (1) $$
Thus, an asymptotically valid confidence interval for $\lambda$ is
$$ \hat{\lambda} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \frac{\hat{\lambda}}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{1}{\bar{X}_n} \left[ 1 \pm \frac{z_{\alpha/2}}{\sqrt{n}} \right]. $$
Note that CLT can be applied to $1/\hat{\lambda} = \bar{X}_n$,
$$ \sqrt{n}(1/\hat{\lambda} - 1/\lambda) \rightsquigarrow N(0, 1/\lambda^2), $$
using the fact that mean and the variance of exponential distribution are $1/\lambda, 1/\lambda^2$. Now applying delta method with transformation $g : t \mapsto 1/t$ yields
$$ \sqrt{n}(\hat{\lambda} - \lambda) \rightsquigarrow N(0, 1/\lambda^2 \cdot \lambda^4), $$
from $g'(t) = -t^{-2}$. This is the same as (1).

4. Let $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$.
(a) Find the method of moments estimator, the maximum likelihood estimator (MLE) and the Fisher information $I(\lambda)$.
**Solution:**
i. MOME: Since $\mathbb{E}(X) = \lambda$, MOME is simply the sample mean $\bar{X}_n$.
ii. The log-likelihood function of $\lambda$ is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\ell_n(\lambda; x_1, \dots, x_n) &= \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i \log \lambda - \lambda - \log x_i!) \\
&= \sum_{i=1}^n x_i \log \lambda - n\lambda - \sum_{i=1}^n \log x_i!.
\end{aligned}
$$
To get the MLE,
$$ \ell'_n(\lambda) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i}{\lambda} - n \quad \text{and} \quad \ell''_n(\lambda) = -\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i}{\lambda^2} < 0. $$
By setting $\ell'_n(\lambda) = 0$, we get $\hat{\lambda}_{\text{MLE}} = \bar{X}_n$.
iii. From the calculation above,
$$ I(\lambda) = -\mathbb{E} \left( \frac{\partial^2}{\partial \lambda^2} \log f_\lambda(X) \right) = 1/\lambda. $$
(b) Use the fact that the mean and variance of the Poisson distribution are both $\lambda$ to propose *two unbiased* estimators of $\lambda$. Show that one of these estimators has a larger variance than the other. Explain why the result makes sense based on your answer in (a).

**Solution:** We know that sample mean and sample variance are unbiased estimator of population mean and population variance, respectively. Since the mean and variance are the same, both sample counterparts are unbiased estimator of $\lambda$. Let
$$ \hat{\lambda}_1 = \bar{X}_n, \quad \hat{\lambda}_2 = \frac{1}{n - 1} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2. $$
Note that Poisson distribution is a regular exponential family. The Cramér-Rao lower bound states that for *any* unbiased estimator $\hat{\lambda}$,
$$ \mathbb{V}(\hat{\lambda}) \ge 1/ [nI(\lambda)] = \lambda/n. $$
Since $\mathbb{V}(\hat{\lambda}_1) = \mathbb{V}(X)/n = \lambda/n$, $\hat{\lambda}_1$ achieves the Cramér-Rao lower bound meaning that any unbiased estimator (including $\hat{\lambda}_2$) cannot have lower variance than $\hat{\lambda}_1$ unless $\mathbb{V}(\hat{\lambda}_2) = \lambda/n$. To see this directly, one can check (e.g., on the Wikipedia page for variance) that the unbiased sample variance is given by
$$ \mathbb{V}(\hat{\lambda}_2) = \frac{1}{n} \left( \mu_4 - \frac{n - 3}{n - 1} \sigma^4 \right), $$
where $\mu_4$ is the fourth central moment $\mathbb{E}(X - \mu)^4$ and in our setting $\sigma^2 = \lambda$. One can also check that the fourth central moment for a Poisson distribution is $\lambda(1 + 3\lambda)$. Therefore,
$$ \mathbb{V}(\hat{\lambda}_2) = \frac{1}{n} \left( \lambda(1 + 3\lambda) - \frac{n - 3}{n - 1} \lambda^2 \right) = \frac{\lambda}{n} + \frac{2\lambda^2}{n - 1} > \frac{\lambda}{n} = \mathbb{V}(\hat{\lambda}_1). $$
