1. Let $X_1, X_2, \dots$ be a sequence of random variables such that
$$\mathbb{P}\left(X_n = \frac{1}{n}\right) = 1 - \frac{1}{n^2} \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbb{P}(X_n = n) = \frac{1}{n^2}$$

(a) Does $X_n$ converge in probability?
**Solution:** We claim that $X_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$. Let $\epsilon > 0$ be arbitrary. Then
$$\mathbb{P}(|X_n - 0| > \epsilon) = \mathbb{P}(X_n > \epsilon).$$

For $n$ large enough we have $\frac{1}{n} < \epsilon$. For such $n$, the event $\{X_n > \epsilon\}$ can occur only when $X_n = n$ (since $X_n$ can only be $1/n$ or $n$), hence
$$\mathbb{P}(X_n > \epsilon) = \mathbb{P}(X_n = n) = \frac{1}{n^2} \to 0.$$
Therefore $\mathbb{P}(|X_n| > \epsilon) \to 0$ for every $\epsilon > 0$, so
$$X_n \xrightarrow{p} 0.$$

(b) Does $X_n$ converge in quadratic mean?
**Solution:** Convergence in quadratic mean to 0 would require
$$\mathbb{E}[(X_n - 0)^2] = \mathbb{E}[X_n^2] \to 0.$$
Compute
$$\mathbb{E}[X_n^2] = \left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^2 \left(1 - \frac{1}{n^2}\right) + n^2 \left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right) = \frac{1}{n^2} - \frac{1}{n^4} + 1 = 1 + \frac{1}{n^2} - \frac{1}{n^4}.$$
Hence
$$\mathbb{E}[X_n^2] \to 1 \neq 0.$$
So $X_n$ does *not* converge to 0 in quadratic mean. Since convergence in quadratic mean to any constant $c$ would require $\mathbb{E}[(X_n - c)^2] \to 0$, in particular $\mathbb{E}[X_n^2] \to c^2$, this cannot hold for any $c$ because $\mathbb{E}[X_n^2] \to 1$ while $X_n \xrightarrow{p} 0$.
Therefore, $X_n$ **does not** converge in quadratic mean.

2. Let $U \sim \text{Uniform}[0, 1]$ and $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p = U)$.
(a) Find $\mathbb{E}(X)$.
**Solution:** Using the law of total expectation,
$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X | U]].$$
Given $U = u$, we have $\mathbb{E}[X | U = u] = u$ for a $\text{Bernoulli}(u)$ random variable. Hence
$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[U] = \int_0^1 u \, du = \frac{1}{2}.$$

(b) Find $\text{Var}(X)$.
**Solution:** We can use the law of total variance:
$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X | U)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X | U]).$$
Given $U = u$, $X | U = u \sim \text{Bernoulli}(u)$, so
$$\text{Var}(X | U = u) = u(1 - u).$$

3. Let $X_1, \dots, X_n$ be independent identically distributed $N(\mu, 1)$ random variables for some $\mu \neq 0$. Define $\psi = \mathbb{E}[|X_i|]$.

(a) Find the maximum likelihood estimator $\hat{\psi}$ of $\psi$. Hint: If $X \sim N(\mu, 1)$ then
$$\mathbb{E}[|X|] = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} e^{-\mu^2/2} + \mu (2\Phi(\mu) - 1)$$
where $\Phi(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-t^2/2} dt$ is the standard normal cumulative distribution function. You may use the normal MLE for $\mu$ without proof.
**Solution:** The MLE for $\mu$ is known to be $\bar{X}_n$. By the invariance property of the MLE, the MLE of $\psi = g(\mu)$ is
$$\hat{\psi} = g(\bar{X}_n) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} e^{-\bar{X}_n^2/2} + \bar{X}_n (2\Phi(\bar{X}_n) - 1).$$

(b) Obtain an approximate 95% confidence interval for $\psi$ based on $\hat{\psi}$. Hint: Use the delta method.
**Solution:** By the CLT/MLE asymptotics we have
$$\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \rightsquigarrow N(0, 1).$$
Define $g(\mu)$ as above. Then by the Delta method,
$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\psi} - \psi) = \sqrt{n}(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)) \rightsquigarrow N(0, [g'(\mu)]^2).$$
Compute
$$g(\mu) = 2\phi(\mu) + \mu(2\Phi(\mu) - 1), \quad \phi(\mu) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\mu^2/2},$$
so that
$$g'(\mu) = 2\phi'(\mu) + (2\Phi(\mu) - 1) + 2\mu\phi(\mu) = -2\mu\phi(\mu) + (2\Phi(\mu) - 1) + 2\mu\phi(\mu) = 2\Phi(\mu) - 1.$$
Thus asymptotically
$$\hat{\psi} \approx N\left(\psi, \frac{(2\Phi(\mu) - 1)^2}{n}\right).$$

(c) Consider the direct estimator
$$\tilde{\psi} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n |X_i|$$
Show that $\tilde{\psi}$ is consistent for $\psi$ and find its asymptotic variance. You may leave $\psi$ in the expression for the variance.
**Solution:**
$$\tilde{\psi} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n |X_i|.$$
Since $|X_i|$ are iid with mean $\psi$, by the Law of Large Numbers,
$$\tilde{\psi} \xrightarrow{p} \psi,$$
so $\tilde{\psi}$ is consistent.
For the asymptotic variance, note that $|X_i|^2 = X_i^2$, hence
$$\text{Var}(|X_i|) = \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] - (\mathbb{E}|X_i|)^2 = (1 + \mu^2) - \psi^2,$$
because $\mathbb{E}[X_i^2] = \text{Var}(X_i) + (\mathbb{E}[X_i])^2 = 1 + \mu^2$. By the CLT,
$$\sqrt{n}(\tilde{\psi} - \psi) \rightsquigarrow N(0, \text{Var}(|X_1|)) = N(0, 1 + \mu^2 - \psi^2).$$
Thus the asymptotic variance of $\tilde{\psi}$ is
$$\frac{1 + \mu^2 - \psi^2}{n}.$$

(d) Compute the asymptotic relative efficiency of $\tilde{\psi}$ to $\hat{\psi}$.
Recall (definition of ARE): Consider two estimators $T_n$ and $U_n$, and suppose that $\sqrt{n}(T_n - \theta) \rightsquigarrow N(0, t^2)$ and that $\sqrt{n}(U_n - \theta) \rightsquigarrow N(0, u^2)$. We define the asymptotic relative efficiency of $U$ to $T$ by $\text{ARE}(U, T) = t^2/u^2$.
**Solution:**
$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\psi} - \psi) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, t^2), \quad t^2 = (2\Phi(\mu) - 1)^2,$$
and from (c),
$$\sqrt{n}(\tilde{\psi} - \psi) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, u^2), \quad u^2 = 1 + \mu^2 - \psi^2.$$
By the given definition,
$$\text{ARE}(\tilde{\psi}, \hat{\psi}) = \frac{t^2}{u^2} = \frac{(2\Phi(\mu) - 1)^2}{1 + \mu^2 - \psi^2},$$
where $\psi = g(\mu) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} e^{-\mu^2/2} + \mu(2\Phi(\mu) - 1)$.

4. Let $X_1, \dots, X_m$ and $Y_1, \dots, Y_n$ be independent samples from $\text{Poisson}(\lambda_1)$ and $\text{Poisson}(\lambda_2)$ distributions, respectively. Let $\delta = \lambda_1 - \lambda_2$. Suppose we want to test
$$H_0 : \delta = 0 \quad \text{versus} \quad H_1 : \delta \neq 0$$

(a) Find the expected value and variance of the estimator $\hat{\delta} = \bar{X}_m - \bar{Y}_n$.
**Solution:** First,
$$\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}] = \lambda_1, \quad \mathbb{E}[\bar{Y}] = \lambda_2,$$
so
$$\mathbb{E}[\hat{\delta}] = \mathbb{E}[\bar{X} - \bar{Y}] = \lambda_1 - \lambda_2 = \delta.$$
Next, since the two samples are independent,
$$\text{Var}(\hat{\delta}) = \text{Var}(\bar{X} - \bar{Y}) = \text{Var}(\bar{X}) + \text{Var}(\bar{Y}).$$
For Poisson, $\text{Var}(X_i) = \lambda_1$ and $\text{Var}(Y_j) = \lambda_2$, so
$$\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\lambda_1}{m}, \quad \text{Var}(\bar{Y}) = \frac{\lambda_2}{n}.$$
Hence
$$\text{Var}(\hat{\delta}) = \frac{\lambda_1}{m} + \frac{\lambda_2}{n}.$$

(b) Use the expression for the variance that you found in (a) to provide a plug-in estimator $\hat{se}$ for the standard error of the estimator $\hat{\delta}$. The plug-in estimators for $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are $\bar{X}$ and $\bar{Y}$ respectively.
**Solution:** A plug-in estimator replaces the unknown parameters by their estimators. The natural estimators are
$$\hat{\lambda}_1 = \bar{X}, \quad \hat{\lambda}_2 = \bar{Y}.$$
Thus an estimator of the variance of $\hat{\delta}$ is
$$\widehat{\text{Var}}(\hat{\delta}) = \frac{\bar{X}}{m} + \frac{\bar{Y}}{n},$$
and the plug-in estimator of the standard error is
$$\hat{se} = \sqrt{\frac{\bar{X}}{m} + \frac{\bar{Y}}{n}}.$$

(c) Find an approximate level-$\alpha$ Wald test for testing $H_0$ vs $H_1$.
**Solution:** For large $m, n$, by the Central Limit Theorem,
$$\hat{\delta} \approx N\left(\delta, \frac{\lambda_1}{m} + \frac{\lambda_2}{n}\right).$$

We start from
$$\mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (Y'_i - \widehat{r}(x_i))^2 \right] = \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (Y'_i - Y_i + Y_i - \widehat{r}(x_i))^2 \right].$$

Expanding the square gives
$$\mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (Y'_i - \widehat{r}(x_i))^2 \right] = \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - \widehat{r}(x_i))^2 \right] + \frac{2}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E} [(Y'_i - Y_i)(Y_i - \widehat{r}(x_i))] + \mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (Y'_i - Y_i)^2 \right].$$

We now handle the second and third terms separately.
—
**Second term.** Fix $i$ and expand:
$$\mathbb{E} [(Y'_i - Y_i)(Y_i - \widehat{r}(x_i))] = \mathbb{E}[Y'_i Y_i] - \mathbb{E}[Y'_i \widehat{r}(x_i)] - \mathbb{E}[Y_i^2] + \mathbb{E}[Y_i \widehat{r}(x_i)].$$
Let $\mu_i = r(x_i)$. Since $Y'_i, Y_i$ are independent with mean $\mu_i$, and $Y'_i$ is also independent of $\widehat{r}(x_i)$,
$$\mathbb{E}[Y'_i Y_i] = \mu_i^2, \quad \mathbb{E}[Y'_i \widehat{r}(x_i)] = \mu_i \mathbb{E}[\widehat{r}(x_i)].$$
Also $\text{Var}(Y_i) = \sigma^2$ implies
$$\mathbb{E}[Y_i^2] = \sigma^2 + \mu_i^2.$$
Therefore
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E} [(Y'_i - Y_i)(Y_i - \widehat{r}(x_i))] &= \mu_i^2 - \mu_i \mathbb{E}[\widehat{r}(x_i)] - (\sigma^2 + \mu_i^2) + \mathbb{E}[Y_i \widehat{r}(x_i)] \\ &= -\sigma^2 + (\mathbb{E}[Y_i \widehat{r}(x_i)] - \mu_i \mathbb{E}[\widehat{r}(x_i)]) \\ &= -\sigma^2 + \text{Cov}(Y_i, \widehat{r}(x_i)). \end{aligned}$$
—
**Third term.** Again fixing $i$,
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[(Y'_i - Y_i)^2] &= \mathbb{E}[Y'^2_i] + \mathbb{E}[Y_i^2] - 2\mathbb{E}[Y'_i Y_i] \\ &= (\sigma^2 + \mu_i^2) + (\sigma^2 + \mu_i^2) - 2\mu_i^2 \\ &= 2\sigma^2. \end{aligned}$$
Thus
$$\mathbb{E} \left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (Y'_i - Y_i)^2 \right] = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n 2\sigma^2 = 2\sigma^2.$$
—

(b) Recall that a so-called linear smoother has the form $\widehat{r}(x) = \sum_{i=1}^n w(x, x_i) \cdot Y_i$. Calculate its effective degrees of freedom.
**Solution:**
$$\begin{aligned} \text{Cov}(\widehat{Y}_i, Y_i) &= \text{Cov}(\widehat{r}(x_i), Y_i) \\ &= \text{Cov} \left( \sum_{j=1}^n w(x_i, x_j) Y_j, Y_i \right) \\ &= \sum_{j=1}^n w(x_i, x_j) \text{Cov}(Y_j, Y_i) \\ &= w(x_i, x_i) \text{Cov}(Y_i, Y_i) \\ &= \sigma^2 w(x_i, x_i) \end{aligned}$$
