1. For a collection of random variables prove that:
$$\text{Var}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n a_i a_j \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j).$$
You can use the following result: for a set of numbers $x_1, \dots, x_n$,
$$\left(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\right)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n x_i x_j.$$

**Solution:**
From the definition of the variance,
$$\begin{aligned} \text{Var}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i X_i\right) &= \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i X_i - \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n a_i X_i\right]\right)^2\right] & (1) \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i (X_i - \mathbb{E}[X_i])\right)^2\right] & (2) \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n a_i a_j (X_i - \mathbb{E}[X_i])(X_j - \mathbb{E}[X_j])\right] & (3) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n a_i a_j \mathbb{E}[(X_i - \mathbb{E}[X_i])(X_j - \mathbb{E}[X_j])] & (4) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n a_i a_j \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j). & (5) \end{aligned}$$
For (2) and (4), we used the linearity of expectation and for (3), we used $(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n x_i x_j$.

Suppose that $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are independent and identically distributed with mean $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mu$ and variance $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$. Show that,

(a) $\mathbb{E}[Y] = \mu$.
**Solution:** Since $X_1, \dots, X_n$ are independent and identically distributed,
$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i] = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \mu = \mu.$$

(b) $\text{Var}[Y] = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$.
**Solution:** Let $a_i = 1/n$ and use the result of Problem 1. In other words,
$$\text{Var}[Y] = \text{Var}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{n} X_i\right] = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{1}{n^2} \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = \frac{1}{n} \sigma^2.$$
Note that $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = 0$ for $i \neq j$ since $X_i$ and $X_j$ are independent.

(c) $\mathbb{E}[S] = \sigma^2$.
**Solution:**
$$\begin{aligned} (n-1)\mathbb{E}[S] &= \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - Y)^2\right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 - 2Y \sum_{i=1}^n X_i + nY^2\right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 - nY^2\right] \\ &= n(\mu^2 + \sigma^2) - n(\mu^2 + \sigma^2/n) \\ &= (n-1)\sigma^2. \end{aligned}$$
We use the fact $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[X]^2 + \text{Var}[X]$ to prove the result.

3. Suppose we generate a random variable $X$ in the following way. First we flip a fair coin. If the coin is heads, take $X$ to have a $U(1,2)$ distribution. If the coin is tails, take $X$ to have a $N(1,2)$ distribution.
(a) Find the mean of $X$.
(b) Find the standard deviation of $X$.

**Solution:** (a): Define a random variable $Y$, which takes value $Y = 1$ if the coin is heads, and $Y = 0$ if the coin is tails with the same probability. Note that $X|\{Y = 1\} \sim U(1, 2)$ and $X|\{Y = 0\} \sim N(1, 2)$, so the means of these random variables are $3/2$ and $1$, respectively. The law of total expectation yields
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}(X) &= \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}(X|Y)] \\ &= \mathbb{P}(Y=1) \cdot \mathbb{E}(X|Y=1) + \mathbb{P}(Y=0) \cdot \mathbb{E}(X|Y=0) \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{3}{2} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot 1 = \frac{5}{4}. \end{aligned}$$

(b): Note that $X|\{Y = 1\} \sim U(1, 2)$ and $X|\{Y = 0\} \sim N(1, 2)$, so their variances are $1/12$ and $2$, respectively. Their respective means are $3/2$ and $1$. The law of total variance says that
$$\mathbb{V}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{V}(X|Y)] + \mathbb{V}[\mathbb{E}(X|Y)].$$
Starting with the first term on the right-hand side,
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{V}(X|Y)] &= \mathbb{P}(Y=1)\mathbb{V}(X|Y=1) + \mathbb{P}(Y=0)\mathbb{V}(X|Y=0) \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{12} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot 2 = \frac{25}{24}. \end{aligned}$$
Furthermore, using the definition of variance, law of unconscious statistician, and $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X|Y]] = \mathbb{E}[X]$, we have
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{V}[\mathbb{E}(X|Y)] &= \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}(X|Y)^2] - [\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}(X|Y)]]^2 \\ &= \mathbb{P}(Y=1)[\mathbb{E}(X|Y=1)]^2 + \mathbb{P}(Y=0)[\mathbb{E}(X|Y=0)]^2 - [\mathbb{E}(X)]^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \left(\frac{3}{2}\right)^2 + \frac{1}{2} \cdot (1)^2 - \left(\frac{5}{4}\right)^2 \\ &= \frac{1}{16}. \end{aligned}$$
Therefore,
$$\mathbb{V}(X) = \frac{25}{24} + \frac{1}{16} = \frac{53}{48}.$$
Take a square root to get the standard deviation of $\sqrt{159}/12$.

4. (a) Prove that the MGF of a standard normal random variable is
$$M_X(t) = \exp(t^2/2).$$
*Hint: You might find the fact that the Gaussian density integrates to 1 useful for your calculation, i.e.*
$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp(-x^2/2)dx = 1.$$

**Solution:** For all $t \in \mathbb{R}$, the moment generating function of a standard normal random variable $X$ is obtained as
$$\begin{aligned} M_X(t) &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp(tx) \exp(-x^2/2)dx \\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp(-x^2/2 + tx + t^2/2 - t^2/2)dx \\ &= \exp(t^2/2) \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}(x-t)^2\right)dx \\ &= \exp(t^2/2) \underbrace{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}u^2\right)du}_{=1} = \exp(t^2/2). \end{aligned}$$
The last step follows by the change of a variable $u = x - t$.

6. **Independence and conditional expectations.** Do Exercise 3.22 in Wasserman page 61
**Solution:**
(a). $Z$ and $Y$ are **not** independent. First, compute
$$\mathbb{P}(Z = 1) = \mathbb{P}(X \in (a, 1)) = 1 - a.$$
Using the conditional probability formula, noting $\mathbb{P}(Y = 1) > 0$,
$$\mathbb{P}(Z = 1|Y = 1) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(Z = 1, Y = 1)}{\mathbb{P}(Y = 1)} = \frac{\mathbb{P}(X \in (a, b))}{\mathbb{P}(X \in (0, b))} = \frac{b - a}{b}.$$
Note that $1 - a = \frac{b-a}{b}$ iff $b - ab = b - a$ iff $ba = a$ iff $a(b - 1) = 0$ iff either $a = 0$ or $b = 1$, which we know is not the case. Thus, $\mathbb{P}(Z = 1) \neq \mathbb{P}(Z = 1|Y = 1)$, so $Z$ and $Y$ are **not** independent.

(b): First, note that $Z$ can only take values 0 and 1. Note also that $\{Y = 1\} \cap \{Z = 1\}$ is the event $X \in (a, b)$ and $\{Y = 1\} \cap \{Z = 0\}$ is the event $X \in (0, a)$. We compute
$$\mathbb{P}(Y = 1|Z = 1) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(Y = 1, Z = 1)}{\mathbb{P}(Z = 1)} = \frac{\mathbb{P}(X \in (a, b))}{\mathbb{P}(X \in (a, 1))} = \frac{b - a}{1 - a}.$$
$$\mathbb{P}(Y = 1|Z = 0) = \frac{\mathbb{P}(Y = 1, Z = 0)}{\mathbb{P}(Z = 0)} = \frac{\mathbb{P}(X \in (0, a))}{\mathbb{P}(X \in (0, a))} = 1.$$
Therefore, we compute
$$\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[Y|Z = 1] &= 1 \cdot \mathbb{P}(Y = 1|Z = 1) + 0 \cdot \mathbb{P}(Y = 0|Z = 1) \\ &= \mathbb{P}(Y = 1|Z = 1) = \frac{b - a}{1 - a}. \end{aligned}$$

7. In this question, we will explore what is known as Monte Carlo integration. We often wish to calculate an integral of the form:
$$I(f) = \int_0^2 f(x)dx,$$
but cannot do so analytically. Note: $f$ here is just a function; it is not (necessarily) a PDF. The Monte Carlo method is a way to approximate this integral.

(a) Re-express this integral as an expected value of the function $f$. What is the distribution of the underlying random variable (the bounds of the integral are a clue).
*Hint: Think of the law of the lazy statistician.*
**Solution:** Let $X$ be a uniform random variable on $[0, 2]$, which has PDF $1/2$ on $[0, 2]$ and $0$ elsewhere. Then $\mathbb{E}[f(X)] = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^2 f(x)dx$. Rearranging, $I(f) = 2\mathbb{E}[f(X)]$.

(b) The above suggests that we can generate random variables in a certain way and approximate the integral of interest. Explain how this is done.
*Hint: How can you approximate an expectation? (This is in the lecture notes)*
**Solution:** Draw $N$ samples $X_1, \dots, X_N$ of a uniform random variable on $[0, 2]$. Compute $\hat{I}(f) := 2N^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^N f(X_i)$. By the weak law of large numbers, $\hat{I}(f)$ converges in probability to $I(f)$ as $N \to \infty$.
