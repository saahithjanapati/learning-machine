### 2 About the Course
**Course description** This is a one-semester course covering the basics of statistics. We will first provide a quick introduction to probability theory, and then cover fundamental topics in mathematical statistics such as point estimation, hypothesis testing and asymptotic theory. If time permits, we might cover special topics such as, for example, nonparametric inference, bootstrap, Bayesian inference, and regression.

The 700 core material roughly corresponds to *Wasserman Sections 1-6 and Sections 9-10*. (Sections 7-8, 11-13 and 20 are special topics, from which we may select examples to further illustrate statistical thinking.)

**Prerequisites** Required preliminary math tools are multivariable calculus and basic linear algebra. Familiarity of elementary probability and statistics will be helpful, but not required. You should have had some previous exposure to data analysis (applied statistics) — otherwise what we do in statistics makes no sense!

**How does this course differ from 36-705?** 700 is a concise course in statistical inference, and includes the basic probability theory you will need for statistical inference. The course seeks a balance between math concepts, statistical tools, and statistical thinking, and assumes less prior knowledge in probability and statistics than 36-705. 36-700 is suitable for graduate students in computer science, engineering and related fields, as well as advanced undergrad students who plan to continue graduate studies in statistics or machine learning. (36-705 is a theoretical statistics course meant for PhD students in statistics or machine learning who have already studied basic probability theory)

### 3 Reference Text
Our main textbook will be *All of Statistics* by Larry Wasserman (CMU library access to e-copy). We will use some supplementary material from *Statistical Inference* (second edition) by Casella and Berger and other texts, when needed.

### 4 Lecture Notes
I will post lecture notes on Canvas, typically in the evening before class; a given set of notes often covers more than one lecture and may contain pointers to sections from the textbooks. I will also post some (mostly blank) “slides” a few hours before class. The slides are to provide some structure to the in-person lecture, and will mostly contain blank pages for you to fill in during class. You will be tested on material that I cover or give pointers to during in-person lectures and in homework assignments.

Policies and advices about using Gradescope:
* If you are writing your assignment by hand (on paper), be sure to use a dark pencil or pen, and write clearly. **It is your responsibility to make your homework readable.** If the grader cannot read your submission, the default grade will be 0.
* When you upload your work to Gradescope, use a **paged PDF** (and not a scrollable pdf without page breaks). Be sure to (a) indicate where each question is located within your submission via the click-and-select interface and (b) after you submit, review each page of your uploaded submission to make sure everything is clear and legible.
* Give yourself some extra time to prepare and submit your assignment online to Gradescope, especially for the first few assignments when you are still getting familiar with it.
* Keep a soft copy of each scanned assignment for your records.
* Re-grading request must be made online within seven days the score is uploaded on Canvas Grades, which shall be about seven days after the homework due time. **So check your grades on Canvas often.** Re-grading requests must clearly state the issue and reason for re-grading. Without such information, the re-grading request will be ignored without notification.
* If you need help with technical issues related to Gradescope, email: `canvas-help@andrew.cmu.edu`.
